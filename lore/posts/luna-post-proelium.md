Echo hit the glass.

The impact was not a knock. It was a body throwing itself at a wall that would not open. Her palms slammed the pane. Her fists followed. Then her forehead — hard, desperate, the impact traveling through the glass and into the spell framework and into my bones. I saw her mouth open around Riley's name and heard the sound that came out — scraped and raw and wrong, a voice that had been a scream once and was now just the shape of one. "Let me in—" The words tore out of her wrecked throat like they were costing her something she did not have to spend. "Let me in— the box is still up— Riley is in there and she is down and I need—"

My left hand tightened on the spellbook.

Echo was pounding on the glass with everything she had left and the glass was not moving and Riley was on the floor inside and I was on this side holding a seal that was keeping her out. The Dawnbreak Sigil was still flickering — thin, guttering, the gold light barely there. Leo's dark was still threading through mine. The box was still standing. We were still holding. But the fight was over. Riley was down. Weave had her. The crystal was sealed. The caster was dead.

"Jesus Christ." The voice came from behind me — one of the cops, the younger state trooper, his words carrying clear through the night air. "Look at them. Look at their heads. They're glowing."

A second cop answered — lower, rougher, the kind of voice that had seen things it did not know how to name. "Both of them. Gold rings. Right above their — what the hell is that."

I looked at Echo. At her palms flat against the pane. At her forehead pressed to the smoked surface. At the smear of blood on the inside of the glass from the caster's head, inches from her face. At Riley on the floor beyond the wall she could not reach.

I could not keep it up one second longer.

I dropped the Dawnbreak Sigil.

Not slowly. Not carefully. I let it go — the spell framework releasing, the gold light unraveling from the spellbook page, the flat disc of the barrier dissolving into nothing. The sigil guttered once, brightened for half a breath like a candle refusing to go out, and then it was gone. The box came down. The black-glass walls rippled once — a distortion in the air, a shiver of wrong-light at the edges — and vanished. The road was a road again. The night was a night again. The bodies and the blood and the sword and Riley were not inside a box anymore. They were just there. On the asphalt. In the open air.

My legs gave.

I did not choose it. My body reached a limit it had been ignoring since the cruiser hit the box — since the impact traveled the spell line backward into my lungs and locked my knees — and the limit decided it was done being ignored. The spellbook slipped from my left hand and hit the dirt and then the hip was the ground and the ground was cold and the cold was the dirt and the dirt was where I was going to stay for a while.

Through our bond, I felt Leo go a beat after me.

His dark threading through my gold flickered once — the shadow-sigil collapsing, his magic pulling back into his body like water draining into sand — and then the bond hit me with the drop. Not pain exactly. Just absence, impact, the rough jolt of him going down hard. He was down. I was down. We were both down and I did not have the strength to turn my head to look at him, but I could still feel that he was there — not in neat details, just the rough proof of him: breath, presence, a thin weak flicker behind my ribs, a candle in wind and still burning. Still burning.

I stared at the sky. The stars were still there — cold and distant and completely indifferent to everything that had just happened on this stretch of road. My lungs burned. My hands were shaking hard enough to blur the ground. The nausea was a solid weight in my stomach and my right shoulder was a dull throb I had been ignoring for hours and my left arm had stopped being an arm and was now just a thing that hurt. I could hear Echo's footsteps — the scrape of her flip flops, the raw sound of her breathing as she moved past us toward the place where the box had been.

I could not get up. I did not need to. She was going to Riley. That was enough.

Somewhere in the blur, Marisol was moving. The cops were moving. Hicks was already barking orders I could not parse. The night was full of motion and noise and the aftermath of the thing we had just survived and I was on the ground and Leo was on the ground and Riley was on the ground and none of us were standing anymore.

But we were all still breathing. All of us. Still breathing.

Leo was on my left. I did not have to look to know — our bond gave me the rough pull of him there, down hard in the dirt, the weak thread of his dark still tangled with my gold. I shifted toward him, my left arm sliding under his shoulders, pulling him the last few inches into the space beside me. He did not resist. Could not. Neither of us could do much of anything right now except breathe and stay close and look up at the stars and the trees standing dark at the edge of the road, patient as witnesses.

I closed my eyes. The ground was cold beneath my back and the stars were still there on the other side of my eyelids and Leo was still there — his weight against my side, his breath warm, his stubborn presence refusing to go anywhere.

For a while neither of us moved. Not because we chose it. Because the body stops being a thing you steer and becomes a thing you wait for. My lungs burned. My hands were still shaking — fine tremor, the kind that lives in the bones after you spend everything and the bones have not forgiven you yet. The nausea was a solid weight. The right shoulder was a dull throb I had been ignoring so long it had become part of my pulse.

Leo's breathing was rough beside me. The crater. I had seen him go down. I had felt the impact through our bond before it went blurry with drain. His wrist was wrecked — I'd seen him cradle it after the sigil dropped — and I couldn't tell what else the crater might have done. He was hurt in ways I could not see and could not fix.

I turned my head toward him. It cost me. The motion pulled at the shoulder and the shoulder pulled at the neck and the neck pulled at everything. I did it anyway.

"You still with me?"

A long pause. Long enough that my stomach dropped.

"Unfortunately." His voice was hoarse. Thin. The kind of sound a body makes when it has used up all its air on impact and has not gotten around to replacing it. But it was his voice. Still his.

I laughed. It came out wrong — more breath than sound, the kind of laugh that is mostly relief and a little bit of the thing that comes right before crying. I did not cry. Too tired.

"Gross." I stared at the sky. The stars had not moved. The trees had not moved. The world was still the world and we were still in it. "Riley did it."

"I saw."

"She's—" I stopped. I had not let myself check. Echo was at the glass the whole time — still there, still waiting, still alive. Riley was down. I knew she was down. I could not turn my head to see. "She's alive. I know she's alive."

"She is." Leo's voice was steadier now. Still wrecked. Still thin. But steadier. "Luna. She is."

I believed him. I do not know if it was our bond or just the fact that Leo does not lie to me about things that matter. Maybe both. Maybe it does not matter.

I let my head fall back against the dirt. The stars were indifferent and beautiful and exactly where they had been before the headlights and the box and the blood. The crickets had come back — I could hear them now, the night-sound filling the space the fight had hollowed out. Cop radios crackled somewhere in the dark. An engine idled — low, steady, the sound of a car that was not going anywhere.

"The sheer audacity," I said.

Leo made a sound that might have been a laugh. "Of what."

"All of it. The clones. The box. The caster slow-clapping down the road like he was walking into his own third act. The fact that we are still alive and lying in the dirt on an Arkansas highway at—" I had no idea what time it was. "—whatever o'clock this is."

"The audacity of Arkansas."

"Don't." I was still looking at the stars. "Don't make me laugh. I am not in a laughing mood."

"Noted."

A pause. The crickets kept at it. The engine kept idling. Someone was shouting orders I could not parse — Hicks, probably. Hicks was always the one shouting orders when things went sideways and sideways was where we lived now.

I shifted my left arm under his shoulders. Not much. Just enough to feel him there. The weight of him against my side. The warmth.

"Your arm," I said.

He did not answer.

"Leo. Your arm."

"Broken." Flat. The way he said things when they were true and he did not want to talk about them. "Wrist. The clone. Before the crater."

I closed my eyes. Opened them again. The stars were still there. "I saw her grab you. I saw you go down."

"I got up."

"You always get up. That is not the point."

He turned his head. I felt the motion more than I saw it — the shift of his weight against my shoulder, the small sound of dirt under his jacket. His face was gray in the darkness. His jaw was tight. But his eyes were open. Still here. Still him.

"The point," he said, "is that you are also lying in the dirt with a shoulder that has not worked since the church."

"Fair."

"Luna."

"I know." I did not know what I was agreeing to. The whole thing, probably. That we were both wrecked. That we had both spent everything. That lying in the dirt on a dark road was the best we could do right now and that was going to have to be enough. "I know."

For a moment neither of us spoke. The night was filling back in around us — the crickets, the radios, the low idle of the engine somewhere down the road. Footsteps. Distant voices. The sound of people doing the slow work of aftermath. I did not have the strength to track it all. I did not need to.

"You should not have held the sigil that long," Leo said.

"Neither should you."

"Different."

"It is not different." I turned my head toward him. The motion pulled at the shoulder and I did not care. "You were in a crater. Your wrist was broken. And you got up and you held the sigil through the cruiser impact and the whole fight and you are going to tell me I should not have held mine?"

He did not answer.

"Yeah," I said. "That is what I thought."

"Don't say I'm being dramatic."

"I was not going to. I was going to say you are a hypocrite."

A beat of silence. Then, very quietly: "You are also a hypocrite."

"Yes, I know. I should have left."

"No." His voice was firm now. Not loud. Just firm. "You should not have."

I did not have an answer for that. The stars were still there. The crickets were still there. Leo was still there — his weight against my side, his breath shallow but steady, his heart stubborn and still beating.

I lifted my head. It cost me — the right shoulder protested, the neck pulled, the nausea surged and settled — but I lifted it far enough to look down the road toward the place where the box had been.

Riley was in Echo's lap.

I could see them now. The angle was wrong and my eyes were still blurry from the drain and the dark, but I could see the shape of it. Echo against a parked cop car — one of the cruisers, engine idling, headlights washing amber across the asphalt. Riley across her lap. Echo's arms around her. Weave's bismuth shimmer wrapped around both of them — gold-green, cycling slow in the headlights. Riley was not moving. But she was not dead. Echo's hand was in her hair.

Relief hit me like a second wave of exhaustion. Not the sharp kind. The deep kind. The kind that settles into your bones and says you can stop now. She was alive. Riley was alive. Echo had her.

I let my head fall back.

"She's okay," I said. "Riley. She's in Echo's lap. Against a cop car." I did not question how the cop car got there. I did not have the spoons for it. "She's okay."

Leo exhaled. One long breath. I felt it through his ribs against my arm. "Good."

"Yeah."

The crickets had come back. I let them fill the quiet. We were still breathing — that was the only thing that mattered.

I found my spellbook in the dirt beside me.

I felt the footsteps through the dirt before I heard them. The vibration traveled up through my spine — two rhythms, two sets of boots beating into the packed ground under my back. The heavier tread landed in long strides, the weight of someone who had been on his feet all night and was not done yet. Hicks. The lighter one moved quicker, shorter steps, the pace of someone who read the road before she walked it. Marisol.

I heard her voice before I saw her face. Low. Steady. "They are here. Both down."

Hicks said something I could not catch. The tone was the same one he always used — calm and capable and utterly unflappable. The man had just watched a black-glass box seal a stretch of highway and a decapitated caster and he sounded like he was coordinating a traffic stop.

Marisol knelt beside us. I heard the rustle of her coat, the small sound of her boots finding purchase in the dirt. Her face came into my field of vision — sharp features, dark hair, the glass in her hair still glowing low lavender in the dark.

Her hand found my shoulder. The good one. The left one. A squeeze — brief, professional, the kind of check-in that asks are you hurt without asking and reads the answer in the way your body responds.

"Luna," she said. "Can you move?"

I considered the question. My lungs were still burning. My hands were still shaking. The nausea was still there. My right shoulder was a dull throb that was going to be a sharp throb the moment I asked it to do anything useful. My magic was gone — not recovering, not resting, just gone, the tank empty and the pump dry.

"Define move."

The corner of her mouth twitched. "Fair."

Leo shifted beside me. The motion was small — just his good arm adjusting, his weight resettling — but I felt it. The broken wrist was cradled against his chest. His face was still gray.

Hicks stepped into position on Leo's other side. I saw his boots first — cop boots, practical, scuffed from the road and the night and everything that had happened on it. Then his hand. Extended toward Leo. Toward the uninjured side.

"Come on," Hicks said. One word. Steady. No pity. No urgency. Just the tone of someone who had been getting people off the ground for a long time and knew that the tone mattered more than the words.

Leo looked at the hand. Then at me.

"I'm fine," I said. It was not true. "Go."

I pushed myself up. Not fast. Not clean. My left hand planted against the dirt and my body followed in increments — shoulder, hip, the slow negotiation of muscle and gravity and the part of my brain that was saying absolutely not. The right shoulder screamed once — a sharp bright note of pain that traveled down into my ribs — and then settled into a throb I could work around. Marisol's hand was still on my left shoulder. Not lifting. Just there. Then her other hand pressed my staff into my palm — the silver quarterstaff Echo had been holding, cold and solid and familiar. I closed my fingers around it. Leaned my weight on it. The crystal tip was dark. That was fine. I did not need it to cast. I needed it to stand.

I got my knees under me. Then one foot. Then the other. The world tilted and I let it. The nausea crested and I breathed through it. The ground was still there when my vision cleared. I was standing. Barely. But standing.

Leo took Hicks's hand.

I watched it happen — the moment his weight shifted and the broken arm moved and the pain hit his face. I felt it too. Not through the bond. Just the way you feel it when someone you love is hurt and you cannot take the hurt away.

His halo flashed.

Thin gold ring above his head. Not bright. Not sustained. One pulse — gold light, there and then not, the way candlelight catches a breath of wind and gives. His jaw stayed tight but his shoulders eased a fraction. His grip on Hicks's hand steadied.

Then it was gone. The halo. The flash. Just Leo rising — slow, careful, Hicks bearing the weight on his good side. The broken wrist stayed cradled against his chest. His face was still gray. But he was up.

I did not say anything. What was there to say? That was Leo. That was what his halo did when things hurt. It did not need commentary.

I looked past him. Past Hicks. Past the place where the box had been. Echo was still against the cop car. Riley was still in her lap. Weave's shimmer was still wrapped around both of them. The caster's body was on the road — a dark shape in the headlights. The blood was on the asphalt. The night was still the night.

We were all still here. All of us. Still breathing.

Marisol's hand left my shoulder. She stepped back — not far, just enough to give us room. Her eyes moved between me and Leo the way a medic reads vitals. I did not know what she was looking for. I did not ask.

Hicks was still holding Leo's arm. Not tight. Just steady. The way you hold something that is not quite sure it wants to stay vertical.

"You good?" Hicks asked.

Leo did not answer. He looked at me. I looked at him. The bond gave me nothing but the quiet hum of his presence — too drained for anything clearer. But his eyes were open. Still here. Still him.

"Yeah," I said. For both of us. "We're good."

It was not true. But it was the kind of lie that kept people standing.

Hicks nodded once. The kind of nod that said he heard the lie and was choosing to believe it. His hand stayed on Leo's arm a moment longer. Then he released. Leo stayed vertical. That was enough.

Riley's glasses. The caster's fist had knocked them off inside the box — I remembered seeing them fly, Weave tracking the arc. They were somewhere on the road now. In the dark. In the debris.

Leo raised his good hand. Light. The glow was thin — his tank was as empty as mine — but it caught the road surface and held. A flicker. Then another.

"Sweep left," Hicks said. "Got something over here."

Leo shifted. Slow. Careful. His broken wrist stayed cradled against his chest and his light swept left. The beam caught something.

"Got it," he said. "Here."

I watched him bend. Awkward. One-handed. The motion cost him — I saw it in the way his jaw tightened and his halo pulsed once, thin gold, there and gone. He came up with the glasses in his good hand. Intact. One small thing not broken.

Marisol's light spell gave out. "Good," she said. "Bring them."

I needed to move. The sword — Riley's sword — was still suspended in Weave's shimmer. Gold-green. Cycling slow in the headlights. It should not stay there. It should not stay on the road.

I walked toward the cop car. My right shoulder reminded me with every step that walking was a negotiation. The staff helped. The nausea did not.

Weave's field was warm when I got close. The sword hung inside it — bare, the blade exposed, the bismuth holding it a handspan above the asphalt. I did not want to touch the shimmer. I did not want to touch anything. But I wanted the sword.

"I've got it," I said. "Release."

Weave's voice came in the back of my mind. __Releasing.__

The bismuth field withdrew. The sword dropped — six inches, no more — and I caught it. My hands were shaking but they remembered how to hold a weapon. I knelt beside Riley. The motion was slow and it hurt and I did not care. She was still against Echo's lap. Still breathing. The wrong-light was on her arms — stopped at her shoulders, not moving, not fading. The blood on her shirt was dry now. Dark. I did not look at it longer than I had to.

I unclipped the holster from her side. Careful. My right arm worked for this — low range, close to my body, the motion small. The clip came free. I pulled the holster free of her belt and clipped it onto my own. Right hip. The weight settled. A second sword. My friend's weapon. Still warm from what it had just done.

I stood. Slow. The staff took my weight and my right shoulder took the complaint. I did not look at Riley again. I did not need to. The sword at my hip was enough.

Echo moved. Barely. Her hand lifted from Riley's hair — the motion unsteady, her throat working. "...the case," she said. The words were ragged. Almost not words at all. "Her bag."

Weave's voice came again. __Sealing. Case secured.__

The shimmer shifted. I watched a thread of gold-green separate from the field around Echo and Riley — guiding something small and dark toward the bag on Riley's side. The safety case. The Lacuna Crystalli, sealed inside. I knew what it was. I knew what it had done. The case vanished into the bag. Done.
