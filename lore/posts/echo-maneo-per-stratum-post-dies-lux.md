My throat is still rough when I swallow but the scrape has dulled some. Not gone. Just... quieter. I can talk. It rasps. Words catch on certain sounds and don't come out clean. But they come out. That is more than I had on the road.

I've been on my feet since late yesterday. Not a decision. My body healed and the wheelchair started feeling like a cage. The nurse looks at me sidelong but doesn't argue.

The morning is full of staff doing things. A proper splint for Leo's wrist. A proper assessment for Luna's shoulder. Neither of them argues once Marisol says "Both of you" in a tone that ends the discussion.

James gets there just before Luna and Leo leave.

I see him in the doorway — navy coat, messenger satchel across his chest, the particular way he holds himself when he's been worried and doesn't want to show it. He stops for just a second. Looks at me. Looks at Riley. Then he crosses the room without saying anything and takes the chair Luna was in.

Luna's hand brushes my shoulder on the way out. "James is here." I nod. Don't say anything. She knows I know.

Leo follows. The door clicks. The room settles into a different kind of quiet — not empty the way it was after they first left. Occupied. James sits close enough his knee is near mine. He doesn't ask if I'm okay. Doesn't ask what happened or what I need. He just sits. His knee near mine. The room holds easier with him in it.

I sit with my hand on the blanket near Riley's wrist. Close enough to feel the warmth through the thin hospital fabric, far enough not to tug the IV line. My thumb moves without permission. Small circles. Same path over and over. I do not know if she can feel it. I hope she can.

James doesn't push me to talk. He watches the monitor. He watches Riley. He watches me. Not heavy.

At some point he puts a paper bag on the tray table. Food. I don't look inside. My stomach doesn't want anything. He offers to stay in touch via Sending — calls, the life stuff I've been ignoring. I nod. He has a phone. I don't. It helps.

He doesn't bring me clothes. I know why. My body changes when I cast. He can't know what fits. All of my adjustable clothes are at the Midori's place.

The gold-green shimmer moves through the room — particles drifting along the frame, then the monitor, then the IV pole, then back around. She does not speak unless I ask. But I can feel her there. Steady. Not going anywhere.

Around midday a nurse comes for me. Tests. I do not want them. My hand tightens on the blanket.

"Go," James says.

I look at him. He doesn't blink.

"I'll be here. Weave is here. The monitor beeps if anything changes. Go."

My jaw sets. My hand trails off the blanket slow — like leaving something unfinished, which is exactly what I am doing. But James's face says he will physically remove me if I argue and I am too tired to test whether he means it.

The tests take forever. I don't know how long. My body says forever. Someone shines lights in my eyes and asks what day it is. Someone presses along my throat — I flinch and they say sorry and keep going. There is a cold room with a machine that hums and a tech who tells me to stay still. My throat gets looked at again, some kind of scope thing I don't watch. At some point someone hands me a sandwich. I hold it for a while and put it down. My stomach does not want food. My stomach wants to be back in the room.

The whole time there is a thread of gold-green at the edge of my vision. Near the door frame. Near the ceiling. I catch it once when the lights dim for the machine — a thin ribbon of particles, barely visible, holding near the corner like she is trying not to be in the way. She does not say anything. Does not need to. I think she split herself to follow me. The rest of her — I don't know what the rest of her is doing right now. I can't see the room from here. But this ribbon is here.

When I finally get back the room has changed.

James is gone. His chair is empty. The paper bag is still on the tray table — brown paper, the top rolled down, his handwriting on the side I can't read from here. He was here. He waited. He left food. Something tightens in my chest and then lets go.

Luna is in the chair on Riley's other side. Laptop. Hoodie that doesn't smell like hospital. Her eyes flick up to the monitor. Leo is at the small table near the window, laptop balanced on textbooks, headphones plugged in. His voice is steady through whatever screen he's talking to. Luna and Leo came back while I was gone. James left. The paper bag is still on the tray table. The room is full again.

Neither of them says anything about the bag. Neither asks where James went. They don't have to.

I still have the sandwich they gave me — cold, still in the wrapper, uneaten. I drop it next to the paper bag without looking. Then I walk straight to the chair and put my hand on the blanket. My fingers find the same warmth, the same ridge of fabric, the same small space near her wrist. It feels like undoing a knot in my chest. Like my hand was not doing its job the whole time it was somewhere else and now it is back where it belongs.

The gold-green thread slips through the door behind me and I lose sight of it near the window — back with the rest of her, I think. No one says anything. The room picks up where it left off.

At some point after the tests — time still doing its stretched-out thing — I notice the pile on the counter. Folded. My dress. The cardigan on top, sage-green wool, the shape of it so familiar I stop breathing for one beat. A note tucked under the edge: washed and returned, nursing staff handwriting. Clean. The cardigan smells like hospital laundry — that particular clean, not floral, not chemical, just washed and warm.

Four days in the same clothes. I didn't notice until right now, and now I can't un-notice. The tape on my right forearm from the IV is starting to lift at one edge, tugging when I flex my hand wrong. My spine aches from the chair. My shoulder — the one that hit the road — is still sore when I lift my arm.

I look at the gold-green near the window. "I need a shower."

__I will be outside.__ The shimmer pulls toward the door and stays there. She doesn't close it all the way. I think that's for me — so I can hear the room, so I am not cut off.

The bathroom is small. Hospital-shower small. Grab bars, a plastic chair I don't use. The water takes a minute to get warm and when it does I just stand under it. The tape on my forearm stays mostly dry — I keep that arm angled out. The water hits my shoulders and my spine and the ache unwinds a little. Not gone. Just quieter. My hair takes forever to rinse. Didn't realize how much road dust was still in it.

When I get out my body feels like mine again. Not the hospital-chair body. Not the tests-and-machines body. Mine. The dress goes on clean and soft. The cardigan goes on over it — the wool still holding warmth from the dryer, that particular soft-heavy weight of something washed a hundred times and still keeping its shape.

I try a word under my breath just to test. It comes out clearer. Not all the way back, but closer. The half-voice from before is gone.

The gold-green is still at the door when I come out. I don't say anything. It brightens — attention becoming visible, the way it did earlier when someone said her name. I get back in the chair. My hand finds the same spot on the blanket. The cardigan stays on.

That evening Luna's phone lights up on the tray table — video chat. Their parents. English accents filling the room, their faces small on the screen — careful questions. Leo answers most of it. His voice is the factual register he uses when someone needs information more than emotion. Luna fills the gaps. Marisol adds a line when they ask about the medical outlook.

I listen from my spot at the bedside. I do not say anything. My eyes track the phone on the tray table like it is another person in the room. Their mother says "Tell Weave we're thinking of her too" and the gold-green shimmer at the window brightens — not a pulse, more like attention becoming visible. My throat does something tight. I swallow around it.

They offer to send money for food and whatever else. The offer hangs in the air. Practical. Generous. Not enough.

The call ends. Quieter after, but not heavier.

Later — I don't know what time, time still doing its stretched-out thing — Grandma Gustin and two younger women arrive.

Grandma Gustin comes through the door first. She remembers me from before. Two weeks ago, the other hospital. She takes in the room without a word — Riley's face, the monitor, the wrong-light still fading on her arms. Her expression tightens and then smooths. She doesn't say anything. Just moves.

Behind her, a woman with dark hair, same jaw as Grandma Gustin. Then a younger woman — late twenties, maybe — who freezes in the doorway when she sees Riley. Her jaw sets. The air leaves her in a short breath. Then she steps forward on her own.

I do not get up. My hand stays on the blanket. My thumb keeps moving. Grandma Gustin looks at me once and doesn't ask me to move. She talks to Luna instead — quiet words I don't catch. Whatever they're saying, it's between them.

The younger woman circles to the other side of the bed. She touches Riley's hair. Her fingers work through a small section near Riley's temple. She leans down. Whispers something into Riley's ear.

I don't hear it.

She leaves something on the bedside table. Small. Personal. I don't look at it. It's not for me.

They don't stay long.

An older woman and a man come after.

Grandma Schaefer and Grandpa Schaefer. Riley's other side of the family. I've never met them. But they know who I am. Riley told them — showed them photos, I think. Luna asks something and Grandma Schaefer nods. "She talks about you constantly."

She says it looking at me. Direct. Warm.

She takes my hand. The one that's not on the blanket. Her grip is firm and dry and brief.

Grandpa Schaefer is quieter. He stands near the foot of the bed and watches Riley, his face not giving much away. They brought food — real food, not hospital stuff. Blankets — maybe from Riley's home, something familiar. They give Luna contact info, say call for anything. Errands. Money. Whatever.

I stay planted. My hand doesn't leave the blanket. They work around me the way the Gustins did. No one asks me to move. No one seems to expect me to.

The gold-green particles are gone from their usual places. I notice it slow, the way you notice a sound that stopped — not the monitor, the IV pole, the window frame. All the spots Weave has been for days are empty. My chest does something tight before I spot it. On the corner chair. Where Riley's bag has been since intake. A small gold-green shimmer inside a cup — clear plastic, lid on, the kind hospitals leave in every room. The particles are barely moving. Circling slow.

She's in there. Hiding.

I don't say anything. Neither does anyone else. Luna glances at the cup once and doesn't react. Whatever Weave is doing, Luna already knows.

Grandma Schaefer doesn't see it. She's looking at Riley, then at me.

The Schaefers leave. Grandpa Schaefer's hand lands on my shoulder once on the way out — light, brief. Then they're gone.

The gold-green particles drift out of the cup slow. They spread back through the room — back to the monitor, the IV pole, the window. Settling into the same places they've held for days. I watch them do it. My thumb is still moving on the blanket. The monitor beeps.

The next day is the same rhythm. Leo teaches his other class — same window table, same steady voice, same warning that he might need to leave. Luna works again — more emails, same flick of her eyes to the monitor every few minutes. Hicks shows up between shifts. He stays for maybe forty minutes, checks on everyone, leaves a bag of convenience-store snacks on the counter, and goes back to work. He doesn't make a thing of it. Just shows up and leaves food and drives back.

Marisol comes back at dusk for the overnight watch. Coffee in hand. She takes the chair by the door — the one no one else sits in. The room feels safer with her here.

I do not leave the bedside. The cardigan's wool sits soft at my wrists — familiar, one of the few things in this room that still feels like mine. The nurses stopped trying around noon on day two. I think they saw my face the first time they suggested it and decided the fight wasn't worth it.

Riley does not move.

The monitor keeps the same green line it has held since the doctors said days to unknown. Nothing. No twitch of her hand. Her breathing stays shallow and even, measured out by the machine. She still doesn't wake up.

The wrong-light on her arms — I watch it without meaning to. Every time the sunlight shifts through the window. Every time the fluorescents change angle when someone walks past. It has been fading. The pale bleed at the edges is softer now than it was on the road. Still inert — the doctors said scar-light, staining not burning. I do not know if the fading means anything about when she will wake up. The doctors said it doesn't. I believe them and I also don't. The wrong-light is fading and Riley is still out. I don't know what that means.

At the end of the day Luna closes her laptop. Leo shuts down his lecture slides. My thumb is still tracing the same path across Riley's knuckles.

The gold-green shimmer moves through the room. I watch her do it. The particles pass near Riley's face first — a slow sweep like checking something. Then past the monitor. Then past my hand on the blanket. Then a wider arc — past Luna slumped in the visitor chair, past Leo still at the window. She makes the whole circuit and comes back to the bed. And then the particles draw inward — tighter, closer to Riley, gold shifting to green shifting back to gold. A quiet shape around the bed. Settling. Steady.

My thumb keeps moving. Riley still does not.

The morning starts with my thumb already in the blanket.

Same square of fabric. Same weave. My throat scrapes when I swallow, and the sound of it — dry click, wrong-small — is the first thing that tells me I'm awake. The monitor keeps its rhythm. The room is gray with that thin hospital light that never lands on anything solid.

I don't lift my head yet. My cheek is on the rail, cold bar, and somewhere behind me Luna is breathing slow and deep in the visitor chair. Leo's chair creaks near the corner. I can feel Weave there too — not a sound, not a shape exactly, but the particles move at the edge of my vision like light on water and I know she's watching the same monitor I am.

"Did anything."

My voice comes out scraped flat. I don't finish the question. I don't have to.

Her voice in the back of my head, quiet and level. __No significant change overnight.__

No change. Four mornings of no change. Four mornings of my thumb on this same square of blanket and the same monitor beep and the same wrong-light through the blinds and the same body in the bed that is Riley and is also nothing like her because Riley moves, tracks sound with her eyes, smirks when I say something dumb and now there's just—

I'm on my feet. I don't remember standing up. The chair leg catches when I shove back from it.

"Four mornings," I say. "Four mornings and nothing."

My voice cracks on the nothing. It's not loud. It's worse than loud — it's the sound something makes right before it breaks.

"You can't just—" My breath hitches. The sentence breaks. "You can't do this and then just stay gone. You can't."

My palm cracks against the rail. Metal rings.

"I can't fix this," I say, louder now, tears already in it. "I can't help you. I can't do anything, and you are still not waking up."

I swing again on the next breath, sloppier than the first. Weave moves first this time. Gold-green particles sweep in between me and the bed, sealing off the IV line, the monitor leads — anything that could turn sharp. The rail stays where it is, too close to Riley to wall off cleanly. __Echo. Stop.__ A beat, steadier and more personal. __You are going to hurt yourself.__

I shove at the light anyway. My hand goes through it and catches the rail on the follow-through hard enough to ring the room a second time. The sound that tears out of me is raw enough to make me double over on it.

Then Luna is there. Her hands on my face, thumbs on my cheekbones, and she's saying my name but I can't hear which time. For one breath it works. Then my eyes tear sideways toward the bright knot of Weave at the bed. I twist after it hard enough that my shoulder clips Luna on the way through.

"No more updates." The words snap out of me, trying to wrench toward Riley and not getting free. "No more calm voice. No more floating there like this is fine." The next breath shreds on the plea. "If you can do more for her, do it. Please. Fucking do something."

Luna takes the hit and changes her grip instead of losing it. One hand stays at my jaw. The other slides down to my shoulder and keeps me from pitching forward into the bed. I grab at her wherever I can reach — wrist, sleeve, forearm — hard enough to leave marks. Then the fight runs out of me all at once and folds into sobbing. Not louder. Worse.

"She's not waking up."

It comes out wrong. Not a statement. Not a question. A sound that used to be words before it passed through my throat. I'm shaking. When did I start shaking. Luna's arms go around my back and the pressure is too much and not enough at the same time. My face is wet. I don't remember that starting either.

When I try to talk, what comes out first is a torn, useless apology.

Luna's thumbs brush once beneath my eyes. They stay there for another breath, grounding me to the touch. "There you are."

Then she pulls me in. Not a big motion, not a rescue, just one brief, solid hold that gives me somewhere to drop my weight. She goes with me, one hand sliding to the back of my neck, the other staying at my shoulder until we sink back into the visitor chair.

I fold forward without fighting it. For a few seconds I stay bent over myself, breathing raggedly into the space between my knees, empty-handed, eyes shut. Then one hand gropes back toward the bed. It stops at the blanket near Riley's wrist. Not skin. Not yet. The place I have been keeping. My fingers find the fabric and hold.

Only then does Leo move. He steps in just far enough to set a glass of water on the side table within easy reach. Close enough to take. Far enough not to feel like pressure. Then he goes back to the window.

I don't drink it. My hand stays on the blanket.

Weave's voice cuts through the monitor rhythm.

__Echo friend. I have been running this problem in a loop. There are actions available to me beyond waiting. I have not taken them yet.__

A pause. A hesitance I've never heard from her.

__Fear entered the calculation. Not only yours. Mine. I may have one way to help Riley, but I do not yet trust it to help more than harm. Let me turn it over a little longer.__

The last line goes wider than the rest. The gold-green shimmer brushes the doorframe, flashes once against the far wall, and snaps inward so hard the orbit cinches tight above the bed. __Apologies. Range corrected.__

My fingers dig into the blanket. For one bad second I just stare at the particles.

I have never heard Weave sound like that. Not uncertain — uncertain she does, sometimes, when she's working through a problem and needs a beat to think. This is different. This is afraid. And the fact that Weave is afraid enough to say so out loud lands in my chest like a second rail hit.

"If you do try it," I say, voice scraped open, "don't— don't lose her trying."

My voice is wrecked. It comes out thinner than I want — less a demand and more a thing I'm handing her.

Luna doesn't take her hand off me. "Then take the extra time," she says quietly. "Riley first."

I watch them until my eyes dry out and I have to blink.

The monitor chirps.

Not the steady rhythm. A change. An alert small enough that I almost miss it, but I've been listening to that machine for four days and I know the shape of every sound it makes.

I look up.

Riley's eyes are open.

Not blinking. Not tracking. Fixed on the ceiling like she's looking at something I can't see, and her face is blank in a way that makes my stomach drop because Riley's face is never blank. Even when she's exhausted, even when she's hurt, there's something behind her eyes that is purely her and right now there is nothing.

"Riley."

My voice scrapes. I'm leaning forward, my hand still on the blanket, careful not to jostle anything, careful like she's made of something that might crack.

"Riley, it's me. It's Echo."

Luna is already leaning in. One hand stays firm between my shoulders, holding me in the chair. The other finds the call button by feel and presses it without her taking her eyes off Riley. "Riley," she says, low and steady. "I'm here."

By the window, Leo straightens before the silence fully breaks. "Riley," he says. "It's Leo."

Nothing. Her eyes don't move. Her hand under mine doesn't twitch. The monitor keeps chirping. I slide my hand off the blanket, touch her wrist, then settle light across the back of her hand, careful as if too much pressure might close this back up. "Hey... hey, okay. You don't have to look. It's me. I'm here."

Still nothing. Leo keeps his distance and watches the monitor instead. "Monitor's steady," he says. Then, quieter: "Riley. We're here."

The seconds stretch. Her eyelids come down slow, the way they do when someone isn't choosing to close them, when the body just runs out of the energy to stay open. And then they're closed and the monitor is back to its old rhythm and my hand is shaking on the blanket.

Doctors come fast after the monitor chirps and Luna presses the call button.

Nurse Collins wheels the vitals cart in quiet at her hip, Dr. Brown behind her. Collins looks at Riley, then at my hand, then at Luna. She doesn't ask me to let go.

"What changed?"

Luna moves because the cart needs space. "Her eyes opened," she says. "No tracking. No words. No response to us. Then they closed again."

"Vitals stayed steady," Leo says. "Heart rate didn't jump. Breathing didn't change."

Collins nods. "Okay. Echo, you can keep your hand there unless I tell you otherwise. If I need it, I'll say it first."

Weave draws back from the bedside rail before Collins steps in — gold-green particles thinning away from the IV line, the call button, the space where hands and stethoscope need to pass. She stays bright enough to watch.

Collins sets two fingers at Riley's throat instead of reaching for the hand I'm holding. "Riley, it's Collins. I'm going to check your eyes."

The penlight clicks on. Marisol casts a low pulse of darkness across my face — not across Riley, not over the doctor, just enough to blunt the hard spill of light. The glass rod in her hair glows once, brief and violet, then fades. I blink once inside the shadow and don't look away from Riley.

Collins lifts one lid, then the other. The light comes and goes. Riley doesn't blink for it or turn. Dr. Brown steps in only when Collins shifts back, his touch landing at Riley's shoulder with warning first and pressure second.

"Riley, this is Dr. Brown. Can you open your eyes for me?"

Nothing.

"Can you squeeze Echo's hand?"

My fingers tighten because the question has nowhere else to go. Riley gives nothing back.

Collins clicks the penlight off. The darkness stays. Then it pulls back slow. My eyes don't scream.

"She's not awake," Dr. Brown says, soft enough that it doesn't snap the room in half. "And she is not showing awareness yet. I don't want to dress this up as more than it is."

"But it is a change," he says. "After this many days with no response, spontaneous eye opening matters. If this pattern holds, we may start seeing signs of awareness in the next three to four days."

Three to four days.

Collins adjusts the blanket where the exam shifted it and leaves my hand alone.

She pauses on her way back to the cart, one hand on the rail. "Walked past on the way in. One of the long-termers next door. Said someone told them *let me turn it over a little longer* while they were in the hall."

Weave's particles brighten — a quick flare of gold-green — then pull back to dim. She sends nothing.

"Sorry about that. Echo had a meltdown, and Weave was a little loud about it. We're settled now." Luna's voice is even and unrushed.

Leo looks from Riley's face to the monitor. The set of his jaw changes by one small degree. "Still steady."

The cart wheel catches once on the floor seam, a soft plastic bump that makes my thumb stop on Riley's hand.

"Call if she opens her eyes again, tracks movement, squeezes, or if her breathing changes," Dr. Brown says.

Luna nods. Marisol shifts just enough to let Collins turn the cart through the narrow gap without brushing my chair. The penlight disappears into Collins's pocket.

Then the door closes.

It's not loud. Just the one small sound. Metal settling into metal. But it takes the doctor voices with it and leaves the room quiet except for the monitor.

"She didn't."

I don't realize I've said it out loud until Luna's hand stills on my back.

"She didn't squeeze. I thought—" My voice catches. I squeeze the blanket. Riley's wrist is under my fingers and it's warm and still. "I was right there. I said her name. She didn't squeeze back."

Luna's hand moves again. Slow circle. The kind of touch that means I hear you more than There, there.

I bend forward until my elbows are on the mattress and my forehead is near my knuckles and Luna's hand follows me down, stays between my shoulder blades like she anchored it there. My breath is hot against the blanket.

"Three more days."

It comes out small. Smaller than I want. The blanket smells like hospital soap and the wrong kind of clean and underneath that, barely there, something that might be Riley's shampoo from before.

"I can't—"

The words stop. Not because I'm done. Because the rest of them won't fit through my throat. Three more days of this. Watching the wrong-light move across the ceiling while she stays gone. Keeping my thumb on this square of blanket and pretending it's enough when it's never going to be enough because the only thing that would be enough is her opening her eyes and looking at me and right now that's not happening and I am out of things to do with my hands.

I breathe. It takes a long time. Luna waits.

__There is one deeper option.__

Weave's voice, flat and clear, the way she speaks when she's decided something. When she needs us all to hear it. But something in the shape of the words is different now. Smaller. Less certain.

__Layer 1 would not read her mind. It would knock and ask permission.__

Something tightens in my chest. Hope that hurts. Hope that might be real.

__If Riley refuses, shows distress, or gives no stable permission, I stop.__

I've never heard her pause between words like this. When she continues, the words come slower.

__I am asking the room to witness this with me. I am asking because I am afraid.__

The monitor beeps. Somebody shifts in a chair. The room is holding still.

I lift my head. Not all the way. Enough to see Weave's particles gathered near the head of the bed, brighter than they've been in days.

"Ask her."

My voice is scraped thin. I'm looking at the particles because I don't have anything else to look at that can answer me.

"Not us. Her."

Luna's hand on my back goes still.

"If she says no—" My thumb stops on Riley's knuckles, frozen mid-drag. "If she is in there and she says no, I need to know she can say no. Not to me. To you. To all of this."

Luna's thumb moves once against my back and stops. "Riley first," she says, and then lower, like it costs her to put it into words: "I'm scared too, Echo. I'm not going to pretend I'm not. But if Weave can ask and Riley can answer, that's more than she has right now."

"You're scared," I say, not quite a question. My voice has gone rough and thin at the edges.

"Yeah." Her hand presses flat between my shoulders, warmth spreading across the fabric of my shirt. "I'm scared. I'm still here."

Leo's voice from the corner. Steady. Low. Low and careful. The voice he uses when things are bad.

"Can you stop fast?"

Weave's answer is immediate. No hesitation on that one.

__Yes. If the answer is absent, unstable, or no, I withdraw.__

"What if her vitals drop while you're in? What if the monitor goes wrong and we don't know if it is you or her?"

__I would not cause a change in her heart rate or respiration through a knock alone. If the monitor shifts for reasons unrelated to my contact, I will withdraw immediately. If you call stop, I stop.__

Marisol pushes off the wall. "And if staff come back, I move first. No one crowds the bed. No one explains over Riley. If Collins or Dr. Brown step in while Weave is working, I brief them before they come through. The room stays open. Staff don't come in blind — not on my door."

Leo holds the answer for a second. "Reasonable." Then, quieter: "That's more fear than I have heard from you."

__Yes.__

Marisol moves to the door and stands with her back to it, arms crossed. Nobody comes in.

I turn back to the bed. The particles are still there, bright and waiting. Riley's face is still blank. The blanket is still warm under my hand.

"Don't scare her," I say, smaller. "If she is in there and she says no or she can't answer or she doesn't understand what you are asking... don't make her stay because we're scared. Don't make her feel like she hurt us by not being ready."

Weave's voice. Softer than I've ever heard it — not in volume, the volume never changes, but in the shape of the words, the way they land.

__I will not hold her. I will ask. Only ask. I will stop at the first sign of refusal, distress, or absence. She will not carry the cost of my fear.__

I close my eyes. Nod. My hand finds the edge of Riley's wrist through the blanket — not grabbing, just contact, just enough pressure to say I'm here in case any part of her can feel it.

"Okay."

Luna's hand presses once between my shoulders. A squeeze. Then still.

Leo's chair creaks. Marisol doesn't move from the door.

The particles at the edge of my vision start to change.

It's not fast.

The light around Weave draws inward — not disappearing but concentrating, pulling toward Riley's head in a way that makes the rest of the room feel dimmer by comparison. The particles slow. They move differently now. Deliberate. Like someone testing each step before they take it.

"Weave?"

No answer. Not public. Not in my head. The particles don't acknowledge the sound.

My hand tightens on the blanket. The monitor keeps its rhythm — beep, pause, beep — and Riley doesn't move and the particles keep drawing in, smaller and brighter, like a door closing that I can't follow through.

Luna's hand is still on my back. I can feel her breathing. She's not saying anything either.

The particles reach the crown of Riley's head and stop. They hover there. Waiting. I don't know what they're waiting for. I don't know if I'm supposed to know. I'm watching light behave in a way that doesn't make sense and the only thing keeping me in the room is the blanket under my hand and Luna's palm between my shoulder blades.

Something shifts in the quality of the particles. A color I can't name. A stillness that makes my lungs feel tight.

"Oh."

It comes out of my mouth before I know I'm saying it. The sound is barely there — breath more than voice. I'm looking at the light around Riley's head and I know. I don't know how I know. The space around the bed feels wrong without her particles scattered in it, and four days of watching them has taught my body something my brain hasn't caught up to.

My voice is thin and scraped. A whisper that hurts to produce. I'm not talking to Luna or Leo or Marisol. I'm talking to the blanket. To Riley's wrist under my fingers. To the particles I can't follow.

"She's in there."

Luna's hand presses down.

The monitor beeps. The particles hold. The room waits.

Two words.

Her voice — not loud, never loud, but impossible to miss.

__Contact established.__

My forehead drops.

Not fast. Not hard. Just — down. Until the heel of my hand is pressed against my brow and my fingers are still tangled in the blanket and my shoulders round forward and I can feel every vertebra in my spine as it gives up the job of holding me upright.

"Okay."

The word comes out into the blanket. Muffled. Small. Not for anyone but her.

"Okay, Riley. Okay."

I'm not crying. My eyes are hot and my throat is scraped raw and my whole body is shaking with something I don't have a name for but I'm not crying. My face stays dry and my breath stays even enough and the monitor keeps beeping and Riley still doesn't move but somewhere behind her eyelids something is happening that I can't see.

Luna's hand doesn't leave my back. Leo doesn't creak his chair. Marisol doesn't move from the door.

Nobody speaks.

The particles at the crown of Riley's head shift — slow, deliberate — and the room goes so quiet I can hear my own pulse.

I keep my forehead on my hand and my hand on the blanket and I wait.
