My body hits the glass.

"Let me in." The words tear out of me raw and wrong — not a request, not a question, the sound a body makes when need overrides everything else. My throat is nothing. Scraped hollow. But my hands are on the glass and my fists are on the glass and my palms are flat and pounding and I do not stop. "Let me in— let me in— the box— the box is still up— Riley is in there and she is down and I need— I need to—" My forehead hits the pane. Hard. The impact shoots through my skull and I do not care. I push. The glass does not move. Nothing moves. I push anyway. My shoulders strain. My spine strains. Every part of me that still works is trying to get through a wall that will not open and I cannot— I cannot— she is right there. Right there. On the floor. And I am on this side of the glass and my body is screaming and the box does not care.

I turn my head. Look for Luna. For Leo. They are still outside the box. Hurting — I can see that, Luna's light barely there, Leo trying to get up — but still holding. The box is still sealed. The black glass is still standing.

I cannot get to her.

"Riley—" My voice breaks on her name. My palms press harder. My forehead presses harder. The blood smear from the caster's head is still there on the inside of the glass inches from my face and Riley is on the floor and I cannot get to her and my body is done. My knees are not my knees. My spine is not my spine. And the glass is the only thing holding me up.

Marisol's arm tightens. Her shoulder braces my weight. Her wings fold in close.

"She is down but she is breathing," Marisol says. Low. Direct. The same steady voice from before. "Weave has her. The crystal is sealed. The fight is over."

I nod. Cannot speak. Do not need to.

The box is still up. The twins are still holding. Riley is on the floor. And I am still at the glass. Pressed against the cold. Watching her breathe.

I press my palms flat. The glass is still cold. Riley is still Riley.

The glass moves.

Not a shudder. Not the dull impact I have been feeling through my palms. Something else. Something deeper. The cold under my palms changes. Warms. Softens. The hard flat surface I have been pressed against since the box went up — it flexes. Gives. It is warming. Softening. It is not going to stay solid.

I feel it in my hands first. Then my forehead. The glass that has been holding me up is not holding me up anymore.

"Marisol—" My voice scrapes out. Nothing. Barely breath. My throat gives me the start of her name and no sound to carry it.

Her arm tightens under my shoulder. The box is coming apart.

The glass ripples. I see it happen — the black surface moving like water after a stone. The cold dissolves under my palms. The solid gives way to something softer, something thinner, something that is not going to be here much longer.

I lean back into her wings. Not a decision. Instinct. My knees are done. My spine is done. Every part of me that has been holding itself upright against the glass needs something solid behind it and Marisol is the only solid thing left.

Her wings are warm. Dark. Still folded around me like a shield. But they are not as solid as they were. I feel it — the edges going soft, the substance thinning. The weight of them pulling back. She has been holding them out so long and I can feel them withdrawing — the warmth fading, the span of them contracting. Not sudden. Gradual. Pulling back inch by inch. Quiet.

"I've got you," she says. Low. The same steady voice from before. "Almost. Almost through."

I cannot answer. No voice left for answering.

The box goes.

Not breaking. Not shattering. Dissolving. The black glass ripples once — twice — and then it is not there anymore. The road is open air. The sealed space that was a fight and a cage and a grave is just asphalt again. The cold pressure that has been humming through my palms and my teeth and my bones — gone. Just the night. Just the road. Just the open.

My body lurches forward. The glass is gone and nothing is holding me up and I almost go down — my knees buckle, my weight pitches — and then Marisol's arm is there, one last brace, one last catch before—

Her wings withdraw.

Luna's staff.

I have been holding it since she pressed it into my hands on the ground. Since before the box went up. Since she gave it to me and I did not understand why — just the cold silver in my palms when everything else was warm and wrong. It is still in my grip now. Crystal tip tucked somewhere near my chest. My fingers have been closed around it so long I forgot they were closed.

I let go.

Not a decision. My fingers open and the staff drops. The sound it makes when it hits the asphalt is loud and clear and final — a clean metallic clatter, silver on road, one sharp impact and then a small rolling rattle until it settles flat against the ground. The weight leaves my hands. Something in my chest lets go with it. I am done holding other people's things. I am done being anything except here.

The staff is on the ground. My hands are empty. That is right.

I push forward.

Not a decision. My body has been trying to get to Riley since the box went up and now there is nothing between us and the only thing that works is the part that needs to move.

My flip flops hit asphalt. The sound is wrong — soft rubber on rough road, the scrape of it cutting through the quiet. My knees are not my knees. My spine is not my spine. Every step costs me. The first one almost drops me. The second one finds better ground. The third one is pure desperation and no technique at all.

I run anyway.

Past the caster's body. It is on the road now — what was the box floor, what is just asphalt. His shoulders. His chest. The place where his neck was and is not anymore. The blood — still there, dark and wet on the road. I see it out of the corner of my eye and my stomach turns and I do not stop.

Past the head. Still — on the road. On the asphalt. The smear of blood still there on the ground. Inches from where my face was pressed against the glass. I do not look at the face. I cannot look at the face. I step around it and my flip flop skids in something wet and I do not stop.

She is right there.

Riley on her side. On the ground. The same position I have been watching through the glass — curled slightly, her weight settled into the asphalt. Weave's bismuth field is still around her — gold-green shimmer, violet to teal to rose cycling slow across the particles near her shoulders, her back, her hips. Not gripping. Just present. Just there. The sword is beside her — glimmersteel now, no Fracture, no wrong-light, just a blade hanging in the shimmer within reach. The safety case is sealed.

My knees hit the ground beside her.

Hard. The impact jars through my whole body. The asphalt is rough through the thin soles of my flip flops. I do not feel it. Or I feel it and it does not matter. Same thing right now.

"Riley." My voice comes out as a scrape. Raw. Hollow. The shape of her name and barely any sound. "Riley— Riley—"

My hands find her. One on her shoulder — the shoulder that took the second hit, I remember, I saw it land through the glass. One on her side. Her sweater is still warm. Still her. She is still warm. I can feel her breathing — the rise and fall of it under my palm. Shallow but steady. Still there. Still going.

"Riley." I say her name again because I need to hear it. Need to hear myself say it. Need my throat to make the sound even if it costs me. "You're— you're okay. You're— still here. Still— breathing. You're—"

My voice breaks. I let it.

She is on her side. Her face is turned toward the ground. I can see the side of her head — the temple, the cheekbone, the place where the fist hit. There is a mark forming. Dark. Swelling at the edge. Her glasses are gone — somewhere in the dirt, somewhere she could not track. Her eyes are closed. Her breathing is shallow but even. She is still here.

"Weave—" My voice scrapes. "Is she— is Riley—"

Her voice comes through. Steady.

__Riley is stable. Breathing confirmed. The crystal is sealed. The amplification has ceased. She is exhausted.__

A pause. Barely a pause. The space between two thoughts.

__She is alive.__

I nod. Cannot speak. My throat is done. Everything is done.

My hand stays on Riley's shoulder. My other hand finds her arm — the arm the caster grabbed, the sword arm. I touch the place where his fingers dug in. I can feel the sleeve of her sweater still pressed with the shape of his grip. The skin underneath is going to bruise. But it is warm. She is warm.

"You— you did it," I whisper. Barely there. Just breath. "You— finished it. You're— still—"

My throat closes. The rest of the words stay behind my teeth.

I stay there. On my knees. On the asphalt. One hand on her shoulder. One on her arm. My body is done. My voice is gone. But she is breathing. Weave has her. The crystal is sealed. The fight is over.

And I am here. Not on the other side of the glass anymore. Here.

I look down at Riley. She is still on her side on the asphalt — curled slightly, her weight settled into the road. Her sweater is dark with blood where the blade went through and Luna's healing closed it. Her glasses are gone — somewhere in the dirt, somewhere she could not track. Her face is turned toward the ground. But she is breathing. The rise and fall of her ribs under my palm. Her voice said stable. Said alive. I am holding those words in my chest the way I was holding the staff.

I want her closer.

My body is wrecked. I know it the way I know the cold through my dress and the ache in my spine and the wrong in my hands that are not my hands anymore — the knuckles sharper, the ache deep, the residue still in my bones. The `Misty Step` had left my body feeling scooped-out and strange and the residue had filled that space with its own wrong weight and neither one asked my permission. But I want her off the ground. I want her weight against me. I want to feel her breathing from somewhere closer than arm's length.

"Weave" My voice scrapes. Still raw. Still hollow. The hand is gone from my throat but my throat does not know it yet. "Help me. I want her— I want her up. On me."

Her voice comes through. Steady. The way it always is when she is doing something precise.

__Adjusting field.__

The gold-green shimmer around Riley shifts. The particles that have been wrapped around her shoulders, her back, her hips — they move. Not gripping. Not lifting all at once. Adjusting. The way Weave does everything — careful, deliberate, reading the body before she moves it.

__Hold still.__

I hold still.

The field lifts Riley gently. The particles reconfigure underneath her — sliding into place, supporting, redistributing her weight across a wider surface of shimmer. She rises in increments. Her head. Her shoulders. The curve of her back leaving the asphalt. The field turns her in the air — slow, controlled, the way you turn something fragile that you are not going to drop. Riley does not wake. Her breathing does not change. Her voice knows her weight now. Knows the places that hurt and the places that can take pressure.

She settles across my lap.

The weight of her lands against my thighs and my stomach and my chest — solid, warm, real. Her shoulder presses into the hollow below my collarbone. Her head finds the crook of my neck. Her legs drape across the asphalt on my other side. The field stays wrapped around both of us now — gold-green shimmer cycling slow across her back and my shoulders, violet to teal to rose. She does not pull back. She is still here. Still holding us both.

My arms come around her without permission. One under her shoulders. One across her back. My fingers find the fabric of her sweater — the gray stained red-brown all through, the place where the blade went through and Luna's spectral bands sealed it closed. She is warm. She is heavy. She is breathing shallow and steady against my neck.

I am holding her. Finally. Actually holding her. Not through glass. Not at arm's length. Her weight is in my lap and her breath is on my skin and my body hurts everywhere but none of it matters because she is here.

My hand moves to her hair.

Not a decision. The way my arms came around her. The way my body knows what it needs before my brain catches up. Her hair is long — black roots, deep blue mid-lengths, light blue to cyan at the tips. I have seen it a thousand times. I have never touched it like this. Loose. Tangled. Spread across my arm and my knee and the air between us.

My fingers find the strands at her temple first. The place where the caster's fist hit. There is a mark forming under the skin — dark, beginning to swell at the edge. I touch above it, not on it. Gentle. The hair is soft. Softer than it looks. My fingers work through the tangles one at a time — slow, repetitive, grounding. The motion is as much for me as it is for her. My hands need something to do that is not fighting. My body needs something to hold that is not a staff or a wall or a wound.

I comb my fingers through the blue-to-cyan at the ends. The strands catch the cruiser headlights — pale gold and white washing across the road and lighting up the color in her hair. Black. Blue. Cyan. Black again where my fingers move. She does not stir. Her breathing does not change. But I feel her body ease — a fraction, just a fraction — against my chest. Maybe she knows. Maybe the body knows even when the mind is gone. I hope it does.

The ground is cold through my dress. My flip flops are still on my feet — I think — I cannot feel them anymore. My spine is screaming and my throat is raw and my hands feel like someone else's hands. But Riley is in my lap and her hair is soft under my fingers and Weave's shimmer is still wrapped around us both.

Then the shimmer on my shoulders shifts.

Not withdrawing. Redistributing. A thin ribbon of gold-green peels away from the outer edge of the field and drifts through the night air. Away from us. Toward the lineup of cop cars on the shoulder.

I track it with my eyes because there is nothing else to track. The ribbon is thin — a streamer of dust, gold-green and luminous, moving low across the asphalt. It passes the caster's body. Passes the head. Passes the blood. Reaches the line of cruisers parked on the shoulder with their engines idling and their lights washing white and amber across the road.

The cops notice. Of course they notice. Flashlights swing toward the ribbon — beams of white cutting through the dark and tracking the gold-green shimmer. Someone shouts — a word I cannot catch, the tone of someone who has already seen too much tonight and is seeing one more thing. But nobody moves to stop it. Nobody steps into its path. I do not know why. Maybe she spoke to them while I was still at the glass. Maybe they have seen enough tonight that a ribbon of gold-green dust heading toward a parked car does not even register anymore. Whatever the reason, they are holding.

One of the cruisers changes.

The engine note shifts — a subtle thing, a change in the idle I feel more than hear through the cold air. The headlights flicker once — a pulse of bright to dim to bright. Then the car begins to move.

No driver. The cruiser rolls forward — slow, deliberate, the way a car moves when someone is parking it with care. Not fast. Not threatening. Just... moving. The cops near it step back. More shouting. Flashlights converging on the empty driver's seat. Someone's hand goes to their holster and stops there. Nobody approaches the car. Nobody tries to open the door. They let it go.

It rolls past the blood and the body and the head the same way the ribbon did — not avoiding, not acknowledging, just moving on a line that someone calculated. The tires crunch on asphalt debris left from the box collapse. The engine hums low and steady. It does not stop until it is beside us.

The driver's door aligns with where I am sitting. Inches away. The engine idles. The headlights cast warm cones across the road — across Riley in my lap, across my hands in her hair, across the gold-green shimmer still wrapped around us both. The door is solid and metal and radiating engine-warmth into the cold night air.

I lean back.

My spine finds the door. Not hard. Just the weight of my body settling against something that is not cold asphalt and is not the open night. The metal is warm from the engine and the headlights and the long drive to get here. My shoulders press back. My head tips against the window frame. My body screams at me in a dozen places and I tell it shut up because Riley is still in my lap and her hair is still under my fingers and I am not moving from this spot.

"Thank you." I do not know if I say it out loud or just think it. Does not matter. She hears me either way.

The shimmer settles. The field wraps around the three of us now — me and Riley and the warm metal of the car door. Weave does not speak. Does not need to. The car is here. The staff is on the ground. Riley is in my arms.

The car door ticks as the engine cools. Riley breathes slow against my shoulder. I stay right here.

Hicks and Marisol walk over to where Luna and Leo are down in the dirt.

Luna has not moved. Her arm is still under Leo's shoulders. Her halo is dark. Leo is half-propped against her — right wrist bent wrong at an angle that makes my stomach turn before my brain finishes the math. His face is gray. But his halo is flickering.

A thin gold ring above his head. Barely there. It pulses once — soft, the kind of light that does not illuminate anything. His shoulders ease a fraction. His jaw stays tight but something behind it lets go.

His halo pulses and his shoulders ease a fraction. Whatever it does, I've seen it work before.

Marisol kneels beside them. No wings now — just her, the glass in her hair still glowing low lavender. Her hand finds Luna's shoulder. A squeeze. Then Leo's good arm. She reads their faces the way she reads everything. Quick. Quiet.

Hicks is there a beat later. Standing. Radio still in his hand. He says something I cannot hear — short, steady — and offers Leo his good hand.

Leo takes it. The halo flashes — brief gold pulse as his broken arm shifts and the pain demands it. Then gone. His body accepts the help.

My fingers keep moving through Riley's hair. I watch them get up. Not fast. Not clean. But up. Everyone is getting up.
Light moving near where the box was.

Not headlights. Not the gold-green shimmer around us. Something smaller. A pale wash of spell-light — Leo's, I think, the kind that comes from a focus held steady, sweeping across the asphalt. Marisol's light joins it. Cool. Low. Two different colors I cannot name from here. Hicks' flashlight cuts a third line through the dark.

They are looking for something. I know what. Riley's glasses. Gone from her face since the box dropped.

The lights converge. Huddle. A small motion — the way lights move when a search ends. The three of them trade something I cannot hear — but the lights ease, the flashlight pulls back, the bodies straighten just a little.

My chest does something. Not big. Just... the lights are not searching anymore.

Luna approaches.

Not walking so much as arriving. She is just here now — close enough to touch, close enough that I can see the dark place where her halo used to be. Her right arm is tucked. She holds it careful. But she is moving. She is up. She is here.

"I've got it. Release."

Her voice is low. Spent. But steady.

Her voice in the back of my head. __Releasing.__

The gold-green shimmer around us shifts. A section of the field that has been holding something separate — a gleam of metal suspended in the particles — loosens. The sword drops a few inches. Luna catches it. Her right shoulder protests — I see the muscle jump — but she does not let go. She kneels. Right here. Beside Riley's hip. She works the holster free, unclips it from Riley's belt with both hands, the right arm careful, the left doing most of the work. Her face is tight.

She clips the holster — sword sheathed, glimmersteel now — onto her own belt. The weight settles against her hip. Her hand rests on the pommel for half a second. Then she stands.

She does not say anything else. Does not need to. She has the sword. Riley's weapon is not on the road anymore.

I try to speak.

My throat is not my throat anymore. The hand is gone from my neck but the place where it was is still hollow — raw, scraped open from the inside. I open my mouth and what comes out is not a word. Just air. Just the shape of a sound.

I try again.

"...the case."

My voice is wreckage. Barely sound. Barely breath. But she hears me. She always hears me.

"Her bag."

I gesture. Weakly. A lift of my hand from Riley's hair toward the shimmer. I cannot do more than that. My fingers do not close all the way anymore. The knuckles are sharper than they were. The skin is tighter. These are not my hands. But they are the hands I have.

Her voice. __Sealing. Case secured.__

The particles move. A stream of gold-green — thin, directional — detaches from the field and guides the safety case toward Riley's bag. The case is dark. Opaque. Sealed. The crystal inside — the `Lacuna Crystalli`, the thing Riley used on him, the thing that made his spells break apart. The case slides into her bag on her body. The particles close the flap. Done.

I let my hand fall back to her hair.
