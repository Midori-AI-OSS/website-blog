Then the building shows up all at once.

Big. White-lit. Too many windows. The kind of place that has automatic doors and questions and people who notice blood.

Hospital.

The cruiser eases into the drop-off lane and stops.

Not danger. Just stop.

__Washington Regional Medical Center.__
__We are here.__

The words move through me slower than they should. Here takes a second to mean anything.

Ahead of us, Hicks's cruiser is already parked. I can see shapes moving through the glass and headlights — Luna getting out with her staff first, Leo on the far side slower and more careful. Both upright. Good. Good enough for now.

A pulse of headlights flashes off to the side. The Malibu rolling forward by itself from the visitor lot. Empty driver's seat. Of course. Weave threads it in behind us like this is the easiest thing in the world.

Cold air touches the car for a second somewhere outside my side. The field shifts. My broken staff lifts off the seat in a ribbon of gold-green and drifts away through the open space. I watch the two halves go. I don't have the energy to be upset about it. Better in the other car than broken under somebody's feet.

More metal moves outside — sword shapes first. Luna's. Leo's. Riley's. Then the smaller shape of Riley's gun lifting off her side and disappearing into the Malibu. Hard to track through the glass and the light and how tired I am, but I get the idea. By the time the Malibu settles back into the visitor spot, the sharp things are gone from sight. Better.

Then I see her.

Marisol is standing near the entrance like she has been there long enough to settle into the space, her flannel open over her shirt. The glass rods in her hair catch the hospital lights — lavender and amber both. Her wings are there too, dark and smoke-soft behind her, not hidden, just folded in.

Relief is weird when you're this tired. Mine feels like my shoulders dropping half an inch.

She starts walking toward us.

The field around us brightens once — a small gold-green pulse at the edges of the window. A yes. A hand at the back of my neck without touching. We're still here. Someone is coming. We are not doing the next part alone.

I keep my hand on Riley's arm.

Good, I think again, because it is still the only word that fits.

Marisol gets closer. The entrance lights catch on the window. My eyes start losing the edges of things.

That is fine.

She is here.

The automatic doors at the entrance sigh open. Two people come out with a gurney — not running, but moving with that pace that means business. A nurse follows them. Navy scrubs. Tablet in one hand. Something looped over her other wrist — a soft collar, white and padded, the kind that holds your neck still.

Nobody looks twice at the empty driver's seat. Nobody looks twice at Marisol's wings. This place has seen enough that it knows what matters first.

"Riley first," Marisol says. She is already at the rear door. Low and steady. The kind of voice that makes people fall into step with it without deciding to. "Penetrating wound. Sealed on scene, but she's getting worse. Echo has a concussion and cervical compression. Leo's hand is bad."

The rear door opens.

Gold-green light washes across the cabin in a soft pulse. Riley does not stir when two people in scrubs lean in — one with dark hair pulled back tight, the other broad-shouldered at her feet. Her face has gone pale under the entrance lights — paler than at the roadside, paler than five minutes ago. Her breathing has dropped to thin uneven pulls with too much space between them. The wound is closed but the rest of her looks worse. Her bag is still with her, the strap curled across her like she held on even unconscious.

I blink at the light. Then I blink harder. The brightness keeps arriving in separate hits — one, then another, then another, like each one is a new surprise my eyes were not ready for. My hand tightens on Riley's arm. My other hand tries to find the edge of my cardigan and misses, catches on the blanket instead.

"Hey." The nurse leans into the cabin. Immediate but not sharp. "Don't move your neck for me. Stay right there."

Marisol's fingers touch my wrist. Two points of pressure. "She's going in now," she says. "You stay still. I'm here."

One of her wings shifts forward. Dark. Smoke-soft. Deliberate. It slides between me and the worst of the ceiling glare and the light stops hitting my face quite so hard.

I swallow. The words take effort to line up. My throat does not want to cooperate.

"Her... trigger..." I manage. I squeeze my eyes nearly shut against the lights and try again. "Needles. Luna..."

"I heard you," Marisol says at once. "I'll tell them. Luna is here."

The gold-green field around us loosens.

It does not vanish. It peels back from the places where hands and board and collar have to work — opening lanes, making space. But most of it goes to Riley at once. Not clinging. Widening into a slow deliberate orbit around the board and the breathing space along her left shoulder and ribs. A thinner veil stays over me for one unsteady beat.

Then that pulls after the gurney too.

The thinning hits me somewhere under my ribs. Loss. Like a blanket sliding off in the cold. The field that was holding both of us is going where she goes and what is left over me is barely there.

Hands move fast. Riley's body shifts onto the board with that awful gentleness. She does not flinch when the board slides under her. When the bag strap drags and settles lower across her side, her hand never moves to fix it.

Fingers press briefly at her throat. Checking color. The nurse looks up.

The whole pace changes.

"Move," she says, and the word sends the gurney rolling.

"Needle trigger," Marisol calls after them.

A voice at Riley's shoulder repeats it without breaking stride. Sharp enough to stop the nearest reaching hand before it touches Riley's arm. "Needle trigger," she echoes to the team ahead.

Riley disappears toward the doors in a rush of rubber wheels and swinging badge clips and a woman with short gray hair already calling ahead. Her bag goes with her. The gold-green shimmer stays with her — still moving, still holding close, not crowding the hands that guide the gurney. Just there. Still hers.

Another stretcher is already coming through the entrance behind the first one. For me.

I try to follow Riley with my eyes.

I almost lose the effort halfway through. My gaze will not track right. The lights smear. The movement smears. Riley gets smaller and then she is through the doors and I cannot see her anymore.

A hand steadies the back of my head. The nurse fits the soft collar into place around my neck — padded, close, pressing just enough to remind me not to turn. She talks me through each step in a calm tone. The kind you use for someone who needs everything broken down one piece at a time.

"Don't help," she says. "Let us do the lifting."

"Riley?" The word comes out before I can think about it.

"Inside," Marisol says. Her thumb presses once against my knuckles. Steady. Countable. One press. One point of contact I can hold onto.

That is enough for one second. Maybe two.

Then Riley vanishes through the doors and my breathing turns fast and ragged. The lights overhead are too white. The collar is too present. The worst of the ringing Luna had quieted is already trying to come back at the edges — high and thin and pushing in from somewhere outside the room. The questions start stacking before the first one has finished landing. I let my eyes close for one breath. Then I open them again on effort alone because not seeing is worse.

The second team transfers me onto the fresh stretcher. My face tightens when they move my shoulders. My free hand worries at the blanket in short repetitive motions that only get quicker when I lose sight of Riley. By the time they are rolling me inside I have lost track of which voice asked what, and someone has to repeat the next question before I can force my breath around it.

I catch pieces as we pass the intake desk. Printer running. Phone ringing. A half-drunk coffee sweating beside a keyboard. The waiting area lights are too bright. Too many edges. Too many sounds arriving from too many directions at once.

Marisol's voice, somewhere to the side — names at the desk. Riley's. Mine. Maybe Leo's. I lose the order halfway through. Then Luna's voice, flat and hard, cutting through: needle trigger. Again. That much I hear.

A woman behind the counter looks up — dark hair, tired lines around her eyes. "Twins?"

"Yeah," Luna says.

Two cards pass through someone's hand near the counter. Species cards. I cannot track whose hand is whose from here. The words that come after do not line up. Something about medication. Something about imaging. The fragments scatter before I can hold them.

Behind me, someone stops Luna. I hear the word shoulder. Luna's voice comes back in three clipped words. Old, healing, not tonight. Then she is moving with Leo again.

I hear Hicks's voice too. Somewhere close, then moving away. Something about morning. Something sorry. Then his voice does not come back.

Riley goes through the inner doors first and vanishes behind them. For one second longer — just before the doors seal — a flash of gold-green. Still with her.

Nobody with her gets to go farther.

The inner doors take Riley and close hard on the rest of us.

That is the worst part.

The doors close. The seam where she was is just a seam again. She is on the other side of it and I am not and there is nothing I can do about it. My chest does something tight and wrong. My grip on the blanket goes white-knuckled. The ringing pushes harder at the edges.

Then they are rolling me. Close after. Someone at my head. Someone at my side. Marisol's hand locked with mine. One smoky wing stays angled between me and the harshest bank of lights as long as she can manage it without getting in the staff's way.

The fluorescents still hit hard. The wheels rattle over a floor seam and the sound goes through my skull. Clipped voices from two directions at once — asking things, confirming things, overlapping. The shift from cold night air to hospital heat lands on my skin like a second weight. When the stretcher turns, my stomach rolls a beat behind it.

I squeeze my eyes nearly shut. Then I open them again because not seeing is worse.

Somebody asks concussion questions. Where are you. What day. Can you follow the light. Somebody else repeats, twice, not to let me turn my neck. By the third question my breathing has gone thin and fast again.

"Fuck. Card," I manage. The word snags in my sore throat like it caught on something. "On me."

Marisol catches it. Passes it on without letting go of my hand. I keep hold of that pressure even when the hallway stops making sense in order — doors and curtains and lights and signs all arriving without sequence. When I cannot get the rest of the answers out cleanly, Marisol fills the gaps with facts and keeps her own words short and concrete.

The weight of the blanket someone put over my legs — gray, thicker than the one from the car. Voices stitching together and pulling apart. Luna's. Leo's. Marisol's. I caught words. Lost them. Surfaced. Sank.

By hour three I was awake. Real awake. The meds had pulled back enough and I needed to know every single thing that had happened while I was under. I asked. They told me. My throat scraped on every word and I didn't care.

By hour five they helped me back into the wheelchair. Same floor. Just a few doors down. We went.

The room is too bright and too small and there are too many machines making too many different sounds.

The collar is gone — my neck still feels like it is not quite attached right. I keep one hand pressed against my throat like I can hold the soreness in by keeping it there. It does not work. But I keep doing it anyway.

The nurse says five minutes. Luna says thank you. The nurse says if I say move you move. Luna says yes ma'am.

I do not wait for anyone to push me.

My hands find the wheels and I roll myself forward before anyone can offer. The motion is clumsy. Too fast. My arms shake with it. But no one stops me. I feel Marisol's hand lift behind the chair — not taking over, just ready. I do not turn to look. I only have eyes for one thing.

Riley.

She is under a thin hospital blanket. The gown is pale and wrong — not her clothes. Her clothes were cut away downstairs. There is tape at her hand. An IV line. A blood pressure cuff. A monitor counting above the bed.

None of it is the worst part.

The worst part is the stillness.

Riley is never still. Not really. She fidgets. Adjusts things. Moves her foot when she's thinking. Even when she's asleep I catch her hand twitching or her mouth moving — like she's arguing with someone in a dream and she's winning. Little shifts. Little signs.

Now none of that is happening. Just the blanket. One small rise. One small fall.

The machine counts it for her.

I stop beside the bed. My fingers twitch against the armrest and the movement pulls at my knuckles in a way that is wrong. Sharper than they were. Not quite mine. I do not look at my hands. I look at hers.

The tape. The line. The place where touching will not interfere if I am careful.

"Can I."

My voice comes out rough. Barely above air. The words scrape through my throat and hurt the whole way out.

Luna looks toward the nurse. The nurse glances in, sees where I am looking, nods once.

"Gently. Don't pull."

I nod like the instruction matters more than anything else in the room. It does.

I reach for her hand.

Slow at first. Then not slow enough. The last inch collapses on me and I have her fingers between both of mine before I can make myself be careful about wanting it.

My face does something. I do not know what.

"Hey."

__Riley status stable.__

And Riley is still in the middle of all of it — asleep but held here by the stubborn fact that no one has gone.

A woman appears in the doorway. Short graying hair. The steady exhaustion of someone finishing a night shift. The woman in uniform steps aside to make room. Behind the first one is a younger man in a white coat with a lapel pin I cannot read from here. His eyes track immediately to the wrong-light on Riley's arms and stay there.

I see where he is looking. My hand tightens on the blanket. I say nothing.

The woman with gray hair speaks without preamble.

"We've been monitoring the light pattern on her arms. We've seen this before." A pause — the pause of someone who knows the next words matter. "It's residual from Fracture exposure. The particulate is inert. It's not causing damage and it's not spreading. It will fade on its own."

The younger man nods. "Quiescent. No active magical signature. Scar-light, essentially — staining, not burning. That's the good news."

Luna exhales. It is not relief. More like one clamp loosening out of many.

I do not loosen. The light was never what I was afraid of. I am watching Riley's face.

"Echo," the gray-haired woman says. "Your concussion is mild. Throat trauma is healing well. Your recovery rate is faster than typical. You're in good shape."

Leo's halo flickers once — brief dimming, then steady. I catch it from the corner of my eye. If it is pain from his wrist or something else, his face does not show it.

Then her voice drops half a register and the room feels it before she speaks.

"Regarding Riley's consciousness — the wrong-light isn't what's keeping her under. We've ruled that out."

She lets the pause hold.

"What we're seeing is systemic depletion. Total-system exhaustion from the fight and the crystal. Her body is in a deep rest state, prioritizing core recovery above everything else. She is not waking up today."

The younger man adds, quieter: "And not tomorrow. We're looking at days — possibly longer. We can't give you a firm timeline."

A sound comes out of me. Small. Broken. The kind that comes when you have been braced for a door to open and it stays shut. I do not look away from Riley. My jaw tightens. That is all.

Luna's coffee stops halfway to her mouth. She looks at Leo — short, question and answer compressed into a shared breath. Neither speaks.

"She's got people in the room," Hicks says. Quiet. Practical. "Around the clock. We'll make it work."

Marisol has been still through most of it but her eyes have been moving — monitor to wrong-light to the doctors' faces. "The fading timeline on the residue. Linear."

"Linear degradation. No known accelerants. Magical light exposure doesn't interact," the younger man answers.

Marisol nods once.

The doctors leave. The door closes soft — the way hospital doors always do.

The room is heavier now. I still have not moved my hand. Luna finally drinks her coffee. It is cold.

From the window frame — a shimmer of gold-green and her voice in the back of my head.

__Timeline undefined. Recovery window: days to unknown. Logged.__

The woman in uniform straightens away from the wall. Wide-brimmed hat in hand.

"I'm not here for statements," she says. Plain. Unforced. "I've got the broad picture — caster dead, crystal deployed. But I need the shape of it from the people inside it. Before I write anything down."

Luna speaks first. Voice rougher than usual.

"The clones came first — a false Lady Light, a false Lady Darkness. The caster was behind them. Farm-hand look, nothing you'd clock. Turned out he was the source behind the crystals in the cities." She pauses. Glances toward the bed. "Leo and I cast the black-glass containment box together and sealed him inside. Riley went in. She deployed the Lacuna Crystalli — it fractures spell sequencing. His magic stopped working. She fought him hand to hand with Fracture and won."

Leo picks up. Factual. Flat. "There were clones first. One of them grabbed Echo — false Lady Darkness. My wrist broke during the engagement. Luna and I held the sigils through the cruiser impact and the whole fight." His halo flickers once — pain or memory, hard to tell.

Her eyes move to me. I have not turned my head since the doctors left.

"Echo. You don't have to—"

"The clone—" I try. The words die in my throat. Raw scrape and nothing behind it. I shake my head once. Sharp. Cannot do it.

Luna finishes for me. Quieter. "The clone got her by the throat. She couldn't breathe. She was still trying to get to Riley when the box came down."

The woman absorbs that without reaction. Looks toward the counter.

"You're with Stillglass."

"Reading variables," Marisol says. "I was at the fight. I'm here for the aftermath."

Her eyes track to the window — to the thin shimmer of particles. If she finds it strange she does not show it.

"You're the one they call Weave."

__I was present for all engagement phases. Threat assessment and containment support. I will provide a full tactical chronology when you're ready to file.__

Her voice is steady in the back of my head. The words arrive clean and whole — the way they always do when she is reporting.

Her expression flickers — someone processing something new and choosing professionalism.

"Appreciated."

I hear her close her notebook and tuck it away.

"Thank you," she says. "I'll file it later. Right now I just needed to understand." She puts her wide-brimmed hat back on with a practiced motion. "You've got my number."

She leaves. The door closes soft.
