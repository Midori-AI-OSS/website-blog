Then Lady Light stepped into the edge of my vision.

She came from the direction of the state cop cars. Moving carefully — the same deliberate economy from the house, but worse now. The working left side of her face was gray with exhaustion. The other side — the right, the side that had always been still — was the same as it had been. Frozen. But there was fresh blood across her cheek, new damage painted over old stillness. Her left arm hung dead at her side — the way it always had, but now with fresh bruising dark along the forearm. She had been through something hard. Harder than us, maybe.

Lady Darkness was in her shadow. Quieter. Her eyes moved across the scene — Riley, Echo, the body down the road — and settled on Luna and me.

They stopped a few feet away. Close enough to see the cost in their faces. Close enough to see her working eye move across the scene — Riley, Echo, the caster's body down the road — checking the same things mine did.

She looked past us. Toward Echo. Toward Riley.

"She did it." Lady Light's voice was rough — the kind of rough that came from using the only side of your face that still worked. "The plan — the crystal, the box, him. It worked. But it was supposed to be cleaner than this."

Lady Darkness did not look at her sister. "It never is."

I did not say anything. My wrist was throbbing. The exhaustion was a dull weight I had stopped tracking. Luna had straightened from Riley and was standing a few feet to my left — her right arm tucked, her weight on the staff Marisol had returned to her.

Lady Light looked at us. At Luna. At me. Her working eye tracked the wrist cradled against my chest and held there for half a second.

"He was the source. The crystals in the cities — the copies — all of it. He was the hand behind it. You stopped the spread. Tonight. Right here."

Luna's voice came from my left. Low with exhaustion. "All of them. Every city."

Pain pulled through my wrist. Deep. Spreading. I waited for it to pass. It did not pass. I spoke through it.

"Then it's done. Tonight."

Lady Darkness shifted. Her attention moved past us — toward Echo and Riley, the iridescent gold-green shimmer still wrapped around them. Her voice was soft. Direct.

"What happened to her?"

Luna glanced toward the field. "Weave was inside with her. Weave — can you join us?"

__Acknowledged. Shifting focus.__

A thin ribbon of bismuth particles detached from the main field — iridescent violet to teal to rose cycling slow in the headlights — and drifted toward us. The cops reacted. A state trooper near the Bolt flinched. Another one's hand went to his belt and stopped. The air around the group sharpened.

Weave's voice came through in the back of my mind. Clean. Precise. The way she always sounded when she was delivering a report.

__Riley deployed the Lacuna Crystalli inside the sealed box. The caster's sequence trust fractured — his spells failed, backfired. She fought him hand-to-hand. The Fracture blade made contact — shoulder, ribs, then the neck. Decapitation. The crystal was returned to the safety case and sealed. She collapsed immediately after. The through-and-through wound from the earlier clone encounter was sealed by Luna. Riley is stable but unconscious. Exhaustion, not injury.__

Lady Darkness stilled. Lady Light's working eye sharpened. Echo — barely conscious — lifted her head a fraction at the sound of Riley's name. Barely more than a shift of weight, but I caught it.

Lady Darkness looked at Echo. Then at me. At the wrist cradled against my chest. "And Echo? What happened to her? And him —"

__Echo: cervical compression injury from the false Lady Darkness clone — limited vocal output. Blunt cranial impact against the driver-side window. Residue exposure from the destroyed clone — visible age progression. Approximately five years. Cause: Lady Darkness clone residue — exposure from the clone's destruction at the containment site. Leo: right wrist — multiple fracture. Sustained during the initial clone engagement.__

I did not react to the report. It was accurate. I had been there for all of it.

Lady Darkness looked back at Riley. Her delivery was soft. "She's still breathing. That's more than the plan owed us."

Lady Light glanced at the body down the road — the shoulders, the chest, the absence where the neck had been. The head separate, near a blood smear. The wrong-light still hanging in the neck wound — a slash of not-color suspended in the cavity.

"We had him cornered. Before he got away." She looked back at us. "This time — she made sure."

Luna's voice was quiet. "She made sure."

I looked at Lady Light. At the damage she was carrying. At the way she and Lady Darkness were still standing after whatever had hit them before they reached us.

"Was there anyone else? Working with him."

Lady Light shook her head. The motion was small — the working side of her face grim. "No. He was alone tonight. But there were nearly forty clones at the crystal before we arrived. We killed them all before he got away."

Forty. I took the number in. Let it settle. Lady Light and Lady Darkness had walked out of a fight that size and come here to close the loop.

Lady Light looked at us again — at Luna's tucked shoulder, my cradled wrist, the exhaustion in our eyes.

"That seal you held — we felt it from outside. That was not a small thing."

Luna did not lean on the staff. Her voice was flat with tired. "It held. That's what mattered."

The pain in my wrist pulled again — deep, spreading, the bone shifting with every pulse. I waited for it to pass. It did not pass.

"He's done."

Lady Light held my eyes for a long beat. The working side of her face did not soften. But something in the way she looked at me — at both of us — changed. It was recognition — the look of someone who understood exactly what standing here had cost.

She did not say anything else. There was nothing else to say.

A man stepped from the shadows from the cliff side, as if out of the air. He moved to Lady Light first and lifted her. Her weight settled against him.

Magic flared. A doorway opened — not a portal, not a tear. A door. Hinges. Frame. Light spilling through from somewhere that was not the road. He carried Lady Light through. Lady Darkness followed. She did not look back.

The doorway closed. The light went with it. The road was a road again.

I stood there for a beat. Let the quiet settle. My wrist throbbed. The exhaustion was bone-deep. Crickets filled the spaces between the distant cop radios and the idling engines.

Marisol's voice pulled me back. She moved from beside us to Echo — knelt, hand steady on her back. "Echo. Let me see you. You're fading."

Echo's voice came out ragged. Barely there. "Riley—"

"She's stable. Weave has her. Breathe."

I did not move toward them. Marisol had Echo. Weave had Riley. They were covered.

Hicks was approaching from the direction of the cop cars. His stride was direct. Professional. The look of a man who had been doing aftermath work all night and was not done yet.

"The body." He stopped a few feet from Luna and me. "Some of us saw something on it — magic, residue. What do I tell them?"

Luna answered first. Her voice was tired but clear. "Two things. One: the crystal. Lacuna Crystalli. She used it on him — it breaks your trust in sequence. What just happened, what you just did. His spells couldn't route. They started failing, backfiring. That's why he couldn't fight back at the end." She paused. Glanced toward Riley. "Two: the sword. The light on the blade left something behind. Wrong-light. Under her skin, and in the wound. His neck. Wrong color. Like it can't close."

My voice was hoarse but steady.
"The crystal's sealed. The case is in her bag. There's nothing active on him. But the neck — the wound's still got that wrong-light in it. Tell them not to touch the edges."

Hicks nodded. Already translating it into orders. "Anything else they need to stay clear of?"

Luna's answer was immediate. "Just the body. The head's separate. Keep them back from both."

A cop approached from the side — younger, flashlight on. His beam swept across Echo and Riley and stopped. Flicked back. His voice was cautious.

"What's on her arms? That light — is that—"

Luna glanced toward Riley. "Residual. From the fight. She's stable."

The cop did not look convinced. He kept his distance. Smart.

Hicks was already turning away — toward the cop whose cruiser was idling at the edge of the scene. "I'm taking your vehicle. Washington Regional." Then to the rest of us. Decisive. "Washington Regional. Two hours. I'll take point. Weave — you've got the cruiser."

__Acknowledged. Cruiser interface ready. Route confirmed.__

"And the Malibu — bring Leo's car behind us."

__Malibu interface queued. Will follow the cruiser.__

My car. Parked on the road between Hicks' cruiser and the Bolt. I had not thought about it since the clone. It was still there. Still intact. Weave would bring it. One less thing to track.

Marisol straightened from Echo. Her voice was practical. Steady. "I'll meet you there."

Hicks looked at Luna. Then at me. "You two, with me."

Luna's voice was thin. "Yeah."

My wrist throbbed. The body was running on empty. The cruiser was forty feet away. It might as well have been a mile.

"Let's go."

Marisol was already moving toward the commandeered cruiser. "Weave. Help me get them in the cruiser. Echo first — she's about to drop."

__Field adjusting. Lifting Echo.__

The iridescent gold-green shimmer tightened. Lifted. Riley first — she was limp in Echo's lap, completely drained, and lifting Echo first would have dropped Riley to the ground. Weave's field moved them together. Echo never lost contact. Her hand stayed on Riley's shoulder. Her other hand stayed on the back of Riley's head. The field carried them toward the cruiser's open back door.

Echo's voice came out as barely more than air. "...Riley."

Marisol's reply was steady. "I'll see you there. She's in good hands."

I turned toward Hicks' cruiser.

The walk was forty feet. It took longer than that.

Every step sent a spike through my wrist — the bone shifting, the joint grinding against itself in ways it was not supposed to. I held it cradled against my chest and let the rest of my body absorb the motion. My spellbook was clipped to my right side — I'd closed it at some point after the search, couldn't say when. My legs were working but they did not feel like mine. The pain came in deep spreading pulses. It stayed. I did not stop walking.

Behind me, the lightbar on Weave's cruiser held steady — blues, reds, and whites. Not flashing. No rush.

Luna walked beside me. She was not much steadier than I was. Her right shoulder stayed tucked. Her left hand was on the staff. But she was moving. Still moving. Still here.

We reached the cruiser. Hicks was at the door, holding it open. The interior light was on — warm, dim. The engine was idling. Headlights carved two cones across the dark road.

Luna climbed in first. Her shoulder first, then the rest of her — slow, careful. She settled into the seat on my left.

I lowered myself in beside her. The motion pulled at everything. My wrist pulled — a sharp deep pull through the break, the bone shifting hard against itself. I did not make a sound. I settled against the seat back and let the cruiser take my weight. I unclipped the spellbook. Set it down. The hard plastic seat held the day's leftover chill under the heater warmth. Close enough that her arm touched mine. Neither of us moved away.

The door closed. The cruiser's interior went quiet — engine hum, the low crackle of the radio, the sound of our breathing. Hicks got in the front. The car shifted under his weight.

Through the rear window, I watched Weave's cruiser fall in behind us. Echo and Riley in the back. The Malibu followed — my car, empty, headlights falling into line last.

The headlights swept across the scene one last time — the cops, the body, the wrong-light still hanging in the neck wound, the blood smear on the asphalt — and then the road ahead was dark. Tree-lined. Quiet.

The cruiser accelerated. The engine note steadied into a low hum. The road stretched ahead — Highway 62 north, two hours to Washington Regional.

Luna's weight was warm against my side. She was still. Still breathing. Still here. The bond hummed with the quiet note of her presence — too drained for more, but steady.

I closed my eyes. Not sleeping. Just not looking at anything for a while. The cruiser hummed. The road ran beneath us. The night was dark and quiet and we were still moving.

I kept my eyes shut and waited.

A minute passed. Maybe five. The pain in my wrist was a steady throb — the bone shifting with every bump, settling into a rhythm I could almost ignore. Luna's weight was warm against my side. Through the bond, the quiet hum of her presence — thin, tired, still there.

I opened my eyes.

The drive had settled into dark, engine noise, and pain.

Luna was sitting beside me in the dark of the cruiser — her staff braced between her knees, the crystal tip angled toward the ceiling. Every bump sent a faint flicker across the headliner. Her right arm stayed tucked. Riley's sword pressed against her hip.

I had been watching her since the convoy pulled out. The way her right arm stayed tucked. The angle of the shoulder that would not let her lean back. I shifted in my seat — slow, careful, right wrist cradled — and turned just enough to see her face.

"Shoulder. Not the short version."

Her eyes stayed forward, then flicked to the rearview and back. Amber light pulsed there in brief washes from the Malibu's hazards two cars behind us. "Weave braced it during the fight. Held through. It'll hold through the drive."

"That's still pretty short."

A breath that was almost a laugh. Not quite. "It's the version I've got, Leo. I can cast left-handed. I've been doing it. The shoulder's not going to get worse sitting still."

I looked at her for a moment longer. A jolt in the road sent a spike through my wrist — I felt it in my teeth. Faced forward again.

"I'll check in an hour."

"I know."

I sat beside her, behind the passenger seat. My right wrist was cradled in my lap. My good hand still held Riley's glasses — the bent temple resting against my palm.

Luna broke the silence. "Riley took a blade for Echo."

"I saw."

"She collapsed when the sigil dropped. I had her heartbeat in my hand and it was —" She stopped. Whatever she'd been about to say stayed behind her teeth. "She's alive. Echo's alive. Everyone's alive."

"Barely." Flat. Not cold. "Echo can barely talk. My wrist is done. Riley's still down. Your shoulder's wrecked. The caster's dead. Doesn't mean we're fixed."

"Everyone's alive." Quieter. Not arguing. Holding onto it.

I said nothing for a beat. Then: "Yeah."

The road hummed under the tires. The silence stretched, then broke.

"We need a story," she said. "For when we get there. What happened to us — the kind of shape we're in — someone's going to ask."

"We were attacked. That's all anyone needs to know right now."

"Echo."

"Friend. Same car."

Her hand tightened on the staff. "The weapons."

"Trunk of the Malibu. Weave can bring it around when we get there."

Hicks spoke without taking his eyes off the road. Quiet. Calm. "We'll figure it out when we get there."

"Thank you," Luna said.

She turned toward me — the motion small, the shoulder refusing more than a few degrees. "You. Talk to me."

"I'm fine."

"You pulled yourself out of a crater with a broken wrist. You're not fine."

My jaw tightened. I stared at the back of the passenger seat.

"Wrist is gone. Every bump in the road is a problem." Stopped. Voice lower. "I'm holding. That's what I've got."

She reached with her right hand — the shoulder must have flared, but the reach was short and she kept it low. Her fingers found my arm. Didn't squeeze. Just rested them there.

I didn't pull away. My left hand stayed cradled in my lap. The rest of me adjusted without permission — shoulder giving, spine settling, the tension I'd been running on bleeding out into the seat.

A mile passed.

She turned, looking past me through the rear window. The second cruiser was right behind us — Weave's cruiser, gold-green shimmer along the dash. Echo and Riley in the back. I couldn't see them clearly — just the shape of the monitoring field through the glass, holding steady. Two cars back, the Malibu followed — headlights on, driver's seat empty.

"She drives better than you." Not quite a smile.

I followed her gaze. A beat. "We're in the back of a cop car."

"Yeah."

"Just checking."

I didn't pull my arm away.

The cruiser pressed on through the dark. The highway opened into four lanes. The treeline on either side softened into scattered lights — houses set back from the shoulder, dark windows, the occasional porch light. Luna was leaning toward the window now, temple against the glass, eyes open and fixed on the road ahead. She didn't speak. Neither did I.

Hicks' hands stayed on the wheel. Another mile passed.

The convoy merged onto I-49 south. The darkness on the horizon softened into a faint orange glow. Fayetteville.
