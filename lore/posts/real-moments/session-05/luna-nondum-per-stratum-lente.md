My laptop was open to the scheduling dashboard. The kind of work that's mostly waiting for other people to answer emails. Which was fine. I hadn't answered any either.

I straightened and felt the pull in my shoulders — the visitor chair wasn't built for four days. Neither was I.

The monitor beeped. I looked up — bed, unchanged — and let my eyes follow Weave's drift (window frame, IV pole, bed rail, monitor edge) before they dropped back to the screen. The subject line hadn't changed. I'd read it maybe five times now. The words had stopped being words somewhere around three.

Leo was at the window. Coffee on the sill beside his spellbook, cold for at least an hour. He'd been drinking it anyway — a sip every so often, mechanical, like his body needed something to do that wasn't watching the bed. The way he stood — weight forward, shoulders set, the particular steadiness he uses when he's trying not to want something too hard — told me everything the bond only confirmed. I could feel him through it. Not words — just the rough shape of him holding still when holding still was costing him. The splinted wrist. The way he hadn't left the window since we got here, and wasn't going to.

Marisol was in the chair by the door. Coffee cooling in one hand, phone dark in the other, the same way she'd been for four days. One hand stayed on the door frame — not gripping, just resting there, like she needed to feel the threshold. Every so often her eyes moved: door, Riley, phone, back. Waiting for the phone to light up. Waiting for Stillglass to call. The glass rods in her hair caught the light when she turned her head. Watching the hall. Watching the room. Holding both. Four days of it, and she still hadn't left.

Echo wasn't back from tests yet.

Weave's gold-green particles traced their circuit: window frame, IV pole, bed rail, monitor edge. Two hours since she'd said contact established. Two hours of the particles doing exactly what they'd been doing, which was also nothing new, which was also everything. They never stopped. That was Weave. She'd been watching longer than any of us.

I shifted. The chair creaked.

Riley hadn't moved. The wrong-light on her arms hadn't changed either. I checked it. Habit.

The monitor beeped again. Same numbers. My eyes stayed on the bed — blanket, IV line, the face I'd memorized four different ways — then dropped back to the laptop.

The email was still there. I still hadn't answered it.

Footsteps in the hall. Flip-flops — Echo's, the particular slap of rubber on linoleum I'd learned to pick out of the hospital noise over four days. Slowing. Stopping outside.

I looked up. I hadn't realized how wrong the room felt until I heard her flip-flops in the hall.

The door opened, and there was Echo. Hospital sandwich in one hand. Fresh gauze on her right forearm where the IV had been — it pulled when she shifted her grip on the sandwich. The set of her shoulders: someone who'd been holding herself together by the edges, and the edges were getting thin.

The room was whole again. Everyone accounted for. Something in my chest settled — not relief, not while Riley was still in that bed, but close.

She stopped inside the door. Mapping the room the way she'd left it. Making sure nothing had broken while she was gone. I didn't move. Didn't speak. She needed to count, and I wasn't going to be the one who made her lose her place.

I could feel Leo through the bond. The tension he'd been carrying for four days didn't ease when Echo walked in. If anything it pulled tighter. Concern, with something sharper underneath. He was watching her too — and not just her. The bond reminded me of something I'd been too tired to name: Leo wasn't okay either. None of us were. I kept forgetting to check.

Echo lifted the sandwich half an inch. "Those tests were dumb. They put me in the loud machine, asked me what day it was like three different ways, and then gave me this like that was supposed to make any of that less dumb."

Her voice came out rough but intact. Hers. I hadn't realized how quiet the room had been until she filled it — and then I realized what the quiet had been hiding. Riley's voice wasn't there. Should have been. Echo was complaining about the sandwich and all I could hear was the person in the bed who couldn't answer.

She was holding the sandwich like she'd forgotten she had it. I slid my laptop over — one motion, making a corner of empty space on the tray table. My water bottle caught the light and didn't move. I didn't say anything. Just made room. My hands did it before I thought about it.

Echo was here. Breathing. Complaining. Alive.

The word snagged. My eyes cut to Riley and something in my chest pulled tight. I saw the blade enter her. Saw it go all the way through. I tried to stop it — didn't matter. The blade kept going. Riley stayed over Echo anyway. The blood. She took it so Echo wouldn't have to. She shouldn't have had to. A brief heat behind my eyes. My jaw set. I cut it off before it could become a thought I'd have to finish. Not now. I breathed.

Echo crossed to the bedside without hurrying. Her hand touched the back of the chair — checking it had stayed where she left it. It had. She sat. Her hand found Riley's wrist through the blanket without hesitating. Four days had taught her the geography. But something was off. I couldn't name it. The way she held herself — something in her hands, in the angles of her face — had shifted while she was gone. Not injured. Different. I set it aside. Nothing I could do about it right now.

I stayed where I was. Laptop shifted over. She hadn't looked at me. Her eyes were on Riley, on her own hand, on the blanket. That was better. If she'd looked at me, she might have asked how I was doing, and I didn't have an answer I was ready to give. Her focus was where it belonged. And I didn't have to pretend.

The fresh gauze on her arm pulled when she adjusted her grip — a small thing. In a room where almost nothing had changed for four days, even that registered.

"Hi," she said, quieter now. "Still here."

To Riley. Not to the room. I heard it. It wasn't for me. But I heard something else: that was love. Most people heard a routine. I knew the difference. I always had.

The monitor kept its rhythm. The gold-green along the rail steadied. Echo's thumb kept moving over the blanket, and the silence stretched — someone building toward something. I could see it in the way her jaw worked, the way her grip on the chair edge tightened. Whatever she was about to say, it was going to cost her to say it. I could see that too.

Whatever it was, I'd hold the room while she paid it.

Echo kept talking.

"I still don't want the food they keep giving me." She looked at the sandwich in her hand. "I keep bringing it back in here like maybe this time I'll eat it, and then I look at you and I just... don't."

She set the sandwich down without opening it. The wrapper crinkled — a small sound, the kind the room had learned to absorb. Her hand went straight back to the blanket near Riley's wrist.

"Leo's still letting his coffee go cold before he finishes half of it. I think at this point it's basically part of the room."

That almost wanted to be a joke. It didn't make it there. I watched it try and fail — the corner of her mouth twitching, the line landing in the space between us and dying there. Leo stayed at the window. Coffee at his elbow. He didn't even look over. Through the bond I caught the rough shape of him — not cold, just holding himself there. The way he did when laughing would've cost something.

"You'd be complaining about this room by now if you were up." Echo's voice shifted again — lighter, reaching. "You'd have a Coke Zero in one hand and be rearranging half this place with the other."

Her mouth twitched and failed. Her fingers curled a little tighter into the blanket before she looked away from them. The monitor kept its small green rhythm. Something moved in the hall and passed. Nobody filled the space after.

"Instead I keep coming back in here and you still don't— you just..."

She stopped. The word she'd almost said — *wake up*, probably, or *answer* — hung there without landing. She swallowed. Kept her thumb moving once over the blanket. Made herself keep going.

"...you're still not getting up, and I hate how quiet this gets when it's only me talking."

The last words scraped on the way out.

She'd named it. The thing I'd been living my own version of for four days — the emails I wasn't answering, the work I wasn't doing, the silence I kept almost filling and never did. Echo had put words on it. Out loud. In front of all of us.

I didn't look at her. She hadn't asked me to. This was between her and Riley, and the rest of us were just — witnesses. The room let the silence stretch. Nobody filled it.

Then Echo lifted her head. Her eyes found the gold-green near the bed rail — Weave's particles, steady in their circuit. She stared at them like they were the only thing in the room that might know how to carry what she was about to say.

"Weave... can you— can you tell her something for me?" Her voice was careful now. Each word placed like she was setting down something breakable. "Not— I don't mean, like, anything else. Just what I say. Just that."

My hand closed the laptop before I thought about it. The hinge caught — a small resistance, then gave. Not done working. Just — this was bigger than whatever I'd been pretending to do.

Echo took one shaky breath and pushed the next line through before she could lose it.

"Tell her… Tell her I'm not mad she couldn't answer."

My throat went tight. She'd thought about this. About what order to say things in. Forgiveness first. Before the love. Before the promise to stay. Echo had lined up these words in her head, and she'd put absolution at the front because she knew — she KNEW — Riley would think she was in trouble. The labor of that. The care.

"Tell her I'm here."

Her hand on the blanket. Same spot. Four days.

"Tell her I'm not going anywhere."

The monitor kept its rhythm behind me. Riley hadn't moved. But Echo was talking to her like she could hear. Like the words would find her wherever she was.

"And—"

Echo's hand stayed on the blanket. Her thumb still. The fresh gauze on her forearm pulled too — a small white shift at the edge of my vision.

"And tell her she doesn't have to come back fast to make me feel better."

"Just come back right. Or as right as she can."

My breath stopped mid-cycle. She was telling Riley to take her time. To not rush back for Echo's comfort. I'd have said something different. I'd have said *come back*. Echo said *come back right*.

Her thumb stayed on the blanket. Her voice raw and quiet and stripped of everything except what was true.

"I'll wait."

After all that — the careful construction, the absolution, the not-rushing, the come-back-right — she put it all down and just said the truth. Two words. The plainest thing she'd said all night. The heaviest.

The room went silent. Not the hospital kind — a different one. The kind with weight.

Echo's thumb stopped mid-stroke. Her grip stayed on the chair edge, knuckles sharp at the ridges.

I watched it. Didn't name it. Her jaw. Her breath, shallow now. Spent. I wanted to cross the room — my weight shifted forward before I caught it. Settled back. This wasn't mine to enter. Whatever she'd just given, it belonged to Riley. The rest of us were just holding the edges.

Leo didn't move from the window — his eyes stayed on Riley and the monitor. Marisol didn't shift at the door. Weave's particles steadied along the bed rail — gold-green, patient, carrying something none of us could see.

The monitor chirped.

I'd been closing the laptop. Echo's words still sitting in my chest. The chirp stopped me mid-motion.

Echo's head came up.

And her face.

The hope on it — too sharp, too bright. The same hope that had cracked her open this morning. That raw sound she'd made, doubled over. I'd held her face then. My hands had been the only thing keeping her upright. Now I was across the room with a laptop in my lap and the plastic edge biting into my palm.

Leo straightened at the window. The bond carried it before his voice — the bracing, pulling the room onto a tight leash. Not cold. Protective. Then the flat lecture register, one clinical fact after another. *Not waking. Contact, not proof.* He was slowing it down before it got where Echo couldn't follow.

The cost came through underneath — his words steady, him not.

Echo's fingers tightened in the blanket. "It moved."

The hope in her voice had nowhere to land.

Leo let the acknowledgment sit. *It matters. Just doesn't mean what we want it to mean yet.*

The monitor kept its new rhythm. Riley hadn't moved. I didn't look at Echo. Knew what I'd find. Knew Leo was right.

Weave's particles shimmered — a brief ripple of gold-green, there and gone.

"Thank you."

Echo's voice — quiet, aimed at the gold-green.

The phone buzzed from Marisol's chair.

She checked the screen, stood, and stepped into the hall. The door clicked shut behind her — soft, the way Marisol did everything. I looked up first. Habit. Four days of it. Leo glanced over from the window, then back to the monitor. Echo stayed with Riley, her hand on the blanket, her thumb still.

I watched the gap Marisol left. The chair by the door, empty. The threshold unheld. My chest registered it before my brain named it — one person lighter. Leo at the window. Echo at the bed. Me in the visitor chair. Riley in the bed. Still enough of us to hold her.

{{image: /lore/real-moments/nondum-per-stratum-lente/room/room-nondum-per-stratum-lente-8.png}}

The hall stayed quiet. Whatever Stillglass was telling her, I couldn't hear it. My hand on the laptop — still flat, still pressing. I breathed. Slow. Steady. The monitor kept its rhythm. Riley hadn't moved.

The door opened. Marisol didn't take the chair.

"Stillglass got back to me," she said. "This is worse than we hoped."

{{image: /lore/real-moments/nondum-per-stratum-lente/room/room-nondum-per-stratum-lente-2.png}}

My hand stopped on the laptop. *Worse than we hoped.* The words landed before I could brace for them.

She looked at Riley, then back at the room. "They think getting fully back to herself may take a while." Echo stared at her. Marisol didn't soften it. "Months. Could be three or more."

Months.

The word hit my chest like something cold and solid. Three months. Ninety more days of this chair, this monitor, this face unchanged. Riley wouldn't be fully herself until — I couldn't finish the math. The IV line. The blanket. The person in the bed who took a blade through her body and got back up, and now she wouldn't be getting back up for months.

The stillness hit. My body braced. My hand on the laptop pressed harder — I didn't ease up.

Nobody answered. Marisol gave us a beat. Then kept going.

"They think we may start seeing signs in three to five more days. Little ones. Not her waking up."

Three to five days. Signs. Not awake. Just — something. A monitor shift. A flutter under the eyelids. Proof that wherever she'd gone, she was fighting her way back.

"My read is sooner," Marisol said. "One to three." She tucked her phone away, voice even, the same register she'd been using for four days. "I read her file. And I saw her at the church. She steadies fast once she has something solid to catch."

I held both timelines. The bad math and the better one. Riley fighting. Riley steadying fast. Riley needing something solid to catch — and I didn't know if I was it. But someone in this room was.

I leaned forward. The chair creaked. My voice came out before I'd decided what to say.

"Can I Cash App or Venmo you or something?"

She took paid leave. She pulled Stillglass strings. She'd been sitting by the door for four days, watching the hall, giving us information we couldn't get anywhere else. I had a laptop I hadn't worked on in hours and a job I wasn't doing. Money was all I had to give. It wasn't enough. I knew it before the words left my mouth.

Marisol's face shifted by a fraction. Not a smile. Something close. "No, thank you. I've got it."

I sat with that. The weight behind my ribs — quiet, heavy. My eyes dropped to the laptop. The email I still hadn't answered. The work I wasn't doing. I couldn't hold Marisol's eyes. Couldn't find the words for what I owed her. There weren't any.

At the window, Leo was already past the timeline. I felt it through the bond — that shift in him, the way his attention broke from what we'd just heard and landed on something else in the room. He was looking at the corner chair. At Riley's bag.

"Was the box really sealed?"

The question landed in the quiet. Nobody answered it. I looked up. Leo's eyes were on the bag — Riley's black crossbody, sitting in the corner chair where it had been for four days, untouched. A Stillglass box. Leo knew about a box. A conversation I hadn't been part of. Something she'd told him. Something she was carrying.

The back of my neck went cold.

Weave's particles shifted along the bed rail. The gold-green pulled tight, the way it did when she was holding something the room couldn't see. A small flicker at the edges — not the smooth circuit she'd been tracing for days.

The voice that came through was not her usual clean address. It arrived broken. The words separating like they'd been cut mid-send.

__Box... es... are... sealed...__

My head came up. I'd heard Weave tired. I'd heard her scared. I'd heard her pull inward when the room got too loud. I had never — in four days of watching her trace her circuit, steady and patient — heard her sound like THAT. Like the words cost her.

__Sorry.__

Then, steadier. She'd found her grip again.

__The boxes are sealed.__

Boxes. Plural.

The word hung in the room. Leo caught it too. I stared at the gold-green near the monitor. Weave's particles, still tight, still pulled inward. Whatever she'd seen in that bag was worse than a single sealed box. Worse than one thing Riley had been carrying alone.

I didn't know what *boxes* meant. But I knew it wasn't good.

I pushed out of the visitor chair. My legs were stiff — four days of sitting, my body protesting the movement — but I was already on my feet.

"Let me just make sure."

I crossed to the corner chair. Riley's black crossbody bag. It hadn't moved since we got here. The strap was looped over the chair arm, and her key ring caught the light — small silver shapes I didn't stop to name.

I moved my laptop to the visitor chair and set Riley's bag on top of it.

My hand found the zipper. The rasp of it cut through the quiet.

My fingers brushed the phone before I saw it. The weight landed in my palm — familiar, the way a doorknob is familiar, or the handle of my own silverware drawer. I'd handed this phone back to Riley across a dozen ordinary moments. Car to table. Couch to kitchen. I knew its weight the way I knew the shape of my own spellbook against my hip.

I pulled it out. The black case, worn at the edges — the way everything Riley owned was worn at the edges. Not neglect. Just use.

The screen woke as I lifted it. I hadn't meant to look.

Riley. Facing away. The Ironwake Compact in her right hand, the barrel wrapped in pale gold-white light.

{{image: /lore/real-moments/nondum-per-stratum-lente/phone/riley-wallpaper-light-gun.png}}

I'd seen this photo before. I took it.

The range. My own phone in my hand that day — framing her from behind. She'd wanted Light rounds for the shot. *"The gold reads better against the grass."* Something practical. She always thought about how things would hold up, what they'd mean later. I pressed the shutter and she didn't turn around.

She never turned around. All her lock screens — every single one — Riley's back to the camera. Different weapons, different stances, same angle. I'd photographed half of them. I'd memorized the rest. She faced the danger so we didn't have to. I didn't need her to turn around.

My thumb moved over the edge of the phone, restless and automatic. The visitor chair creaked under me. Four days.

After the range, we'd gone to Steak 'n Shake. Fayetteville, next to the mall. Leo, Riley, and me. Her great-grandfather James had joined us — showed up in that quiet way old men do, like he'd been planning to be there all along. He told us stories about flying a plane. Burgers and milkshakes and an old man's voice carrying across the table while his great-granddaughter listened. I remembered the way Riley looked at him. Not checking her phone. Not scanning the room. Just there. James was gone now. The thought landed heavier than I expected — I'd known he passed, but holding this photo, remembering him alive at that table while Riley listened...

I breathed. The monitor beeped. Same numbers.

The light on the gun — pale gold-white running along the barrel. Light-element rounds. Riley had loaded them deliberately. She couldn't cast magic. The Ironwake Compact was what she had. Every single thing she owned was dark — the gun black. But the rounds were light. She chose light. I chose light too. Two people who looked at the same spectrum and both reached for the same thing. We'd never talked about it.

The photo was proof she'd been ready. Always.

I looked at her hand on the grip. I knew how her fingers sat on that gun — had photographed it, watched it a hundred times. Steady. Deliberate. The way everything Riley did was deliberate.

Then I looked at the bed.

Her hand was slack under the blanket. Just the shape of it — the ridge of knuckles, the curve of palm, the same geography doing nothing. Four days. The hand that held the gun, that killed the Luna-copy at the church, that pressed Lacuna Crystalli into her sword hilt and got back up anyway — couldn't close around the blanket.

My own hand tightened on the phone. I didn't tell it to.

The memory came without asking. My palm flat against her chest. Golden light pulsing from the contact — my heartbeat, not hers, because hers had felt like seconds from stopping. I'd pulled her back with `Radiant Anchor` and she hadn't even known how close it was. Then she'd gotten up. Wounded. Bleeding. She'd 1v1'd a caster with that fucking crystal in her sword, and she'd won, and now she was in this bed.

My jaw set. I couldn't `Radiant Anchor` my way out of this one. Whatever was keeping her under, it wasn't a normal wound.

But she'd used the time I gave her. Every second. For us. I couldn't fault her for that.

What if this was the last photo I ever took of her?

The thought finished before I could stop it. Cold pressure behind my ribs — the kind that sits and doesn't move. I let it sit for one second. Let it be true.

Then I pushed back. She'd come back before. Stillglass — dissociating from Lacuna, and she'd grounded. The blade through her body — seconds from death, and she'd gotten up. Marisol had said it this morning: *she steadies fast once she has something solid to catch.* The pattern was there.

I wished for a sign. Not prayed — wished, a raw private thing aimed at nothing and everything. Proof she'd found something to hold onto.

My thumb found the hinge of the Fold. I opened it. Closed it. The hinge caught — a small resistance, then gave, the way it always did. Riley's phone. Riley's wear pattern. My thumb found the worn spot on the edge where hers had rested. I held there. My thumb where hers had been. Grounding myself the way I always grounded her.

The monitor beeped.

That small electronic note. The same one I'd been hearing for four days. And for one irrational half-second — the kind you know is stupid and feel anyway — it felt like she was watching me. Like she knew I was holding her phone, touching the worn spot her thumb had made.

I knew she wasn't. The beep was just the monitor. Same numbers. Same rhythm.

I reached anyway.

"Hey... you..."

Quiet. The voice I used when her edges were fraying — the grounding phrase I'd said a hundred times before, in a hundred smaller crises, and it had always worked. I was using it now on a silence that had never been this deep, hoping it still meant something.

The room didn't answer. The monitor kept its rhythm. Riley didn't move.

I set the phone screen-down on the tray table. If I looked at it again, the screen would wake — the photo would reappear — and I couldn't do that. Not yet. The black case faced up, worn spot showing.

I pulled Riley's wallet from the bag. My thumb ran once over the leather. The leather was cold. It had been sitting in the bag for days, untouched.

I opened it just enough to see the ID in the little window. The tiny almost-smile. The photo Riley hated and I'd always thought looked exactly like her.

I stared at it.

And my body remembered the drive. Riley's Volt — before the Bolt — and she was driving, and she was mad about having to take her glasses off for the photo. Self-conscious. "I look weird without them." I was in the passenger seat, and I was just... happy. Getting out of the house. An ordinary day with her. I didn't have a name for it yet, but I was glad to be there. Glad she'd asked me.

She kept opening her wallet to look at it the whole drive back, making that face like the DMV had personally insulted her. She really did hate it.

Her whole face in the photo was caught between what she felt and what she wanted to show. The eyes without glasses — direct, unfiltered. The almost-smile — the seam where the truth leaked through before she could stop it. She looked at this and saw young and scared and hated it. I looked at it and thought: *there you are.*

And then the other memory hit. The weight of her head in my lap, six days ago back at the house, clean soft hair, my hand in it because we both needed grounding. The warmth of her through my clothes. The texture of her hair sliding under my fingers. The sound of her breathing evening out as she fell asleep. Peacetime. Trust.

Her head had been warm.

I pressed the wallet flat against my stomach. The leather was real. Solid. Present. Something unclenched in me. Not a lot. Just enough to breathe.

"Of course you did."

Quieter than before. To the photo. To her. I was there. I remember. You hated this photo, and of course you did. I looked at it and saw the person I'd been watching since before I knew what I was watching for.

I closed the wallet. The metal clip clicked against the tray table when I set it down, overlapping the phone.

My hand went back into the bag. My fingers found the battery pack before my eyes did. The shape. The weight. The scuffed surface I'd know anywhere.

I pulled it out. Weighed it once in my palm.

Scuffed black. Beat to hell. A dent in one corner — Riley used to joke she could throw it if she ran out of better options. She never did. It was always in her bag, always charged, always ready for someone else's phone to die.

The LED dots were dark. I passed my thumb over the button without pressing. Didn't need to. I knew this pack. It wasn't dead. She'd never let it run out. Not once. Not in all the time I'd known her.

The pack was still ready. She wasn't.

My jaw was already set — had been since the phone, since the wallet. But something gave. A fraction. Not a lot. Enough.

The night my phone died at the diner, she had the pack out before I finished the sentence. Still looking at her menu. Hadn't even glanced up. Slid it across the table like it was part of the order. I hadn't finished.

I breathed. The pack was still in my palm.

Months ago at Harrison, near the walking trail. My phone at five percent in the car and the cable was already plugged in. She was driving. Hadn't said anything. I went to grab my charger and found hers already there. Something unclenched. I didn't know what to do with it.

The trip to Little Rock. Both of us forgot chargers. One pack, two phones, the whole drive. She hadn't mentioned it until I asked what the cord in the center console was for. Quiet confirmation. I'd suspected. She confirmed. That was all.

Five years ago at the range. The same day I took the photo, her back to the camera, gun wrapped in light. Leo's phone was dying and she had the pack out before either of us said a word. The armed protector in the photo. The quiet caregiver with a battery pack. Both at once. The pattern was already there, five years ago. Before the church. Before Weave. Before any of it.

I blinked and the range was gone — just the hospital room, just the pack in my hand, just the weight.

The Harris family. Years of stories — ATF, One Thing, the things they did together at New Day Fellowship. Somewhere in all those stories was this: Riley always had a charger at church. For anyone who needed it. I'd kept it. Everyone who knew her knew this about her. Everyone except me, maybe — not that I didn't know. That I never said it.

My thumb was still over the button. I moved it. Didn't press.

"You knew." My voice caught. "Always ready."

I set it down heavier than the phone. Heavier than the wallet. It landed with a weight that wasn't just the pack.

My hand went back into the bag and found the cable — short, red, coiled tight.

The color hit before the shape did. Red. Bright and private against the dark interior of the bag, against the gray hospital walls.

I pulled it out slow. Short. Neatly coiled — she had coiled it herself. The rubber cool against my fingers, the weight lighter than the battery pack but somehow heavier now. Red. Riley almost never wore red. I couldn't remember a single time. The years of watching her and I couldn't find one.

NWACC. She'd asked me to come — or maybe I'd asked and she'd said yes. Either way, we were in her Volt, Highway 62 winding through Eureka Springs, and she had things to do on campus and I had a spell book to pick up from the library. A drive. That was all.

I breathed. The hospital room came back — monitor beeping, Weave's gold-green at the edge of my vision — and went away again.

I came back from the library with the spell book under my arm and found her in the computer lab. One of the quiet ones. Computer lab hum, rows of monitors. She was at a station near the window. Face tilted toward her laptop screen, washed in that pale monitor glow. Brow furrowed. The red cable bridging from her phone to the laptop — she'd been tethering for internet. Cyber security coursework. Something about network analysis. She was so deep in it she forgot I was there.

I sat down at the next station. Opened the spell book. Studied planar magic while she studied threats. Two people, learning side by side. Choosing to do it in the same room.

Her face in that monitor light. Unguarded. Absorbed. The face of someone who wasn't performing. Who didn't know she was being watched. Who was just... working. Becoming.

My jaw unlocked. I hadn't noticed it was set.

I didn't take a photo of that face.

The thought landed somewhere under my ribs and stayed there. I'd taken the lock screen photo — Riley the protector, facing away, gun wrapped in light. I'd documented that version. The armed one. The one everyone saw. I hadn't documented this one. The student. The person lit by a screen, building herself in code and concentration, private and fierce.

I shifted in the visitor chair. The plastic creaked — the same sound it had been making for four days.

Everyone knew her drive. Terrifying. Awe-inspiring. Lady Light had called her full of consequence. In every crisis, it was what she did. The color was beside the point.

Anyway. This cable was red.

That was the thought I landed on. She'd chosen this one thing in that color and kept it in her bag. Private. Hers.

My throat tightened. I knew what was coming — the next thing I'd find in this bag — and I wasn't ready. My hands weren't ready.

I coiled the cable once more around my fingers. Slow. She'd already coiled it perfectly. My hands needed one more thing to do. One more small task before whatever was sealed in the inner pocket. My body knew. My fingers wrapped the red around themselves, the rubber catching at my knuckles, and I let them work.

The magnetic ends found each other with a small click. Two ends. Finding each other.

"Of course it's red."

Quiet. Just... recognition. Of course this was yours. The one thing in the color of who you actually were.

I set it beside the battery pack. Neat. Deliberate. Then I looked at Riley.

Her face hadn't changed. I knew that — I'd been checking. But looking at her now, really looking, was different.

The monitor kept its rhythm behind her. The IV line dripped, steady and indifferent. Her eyelids were closed — not tight, not peaceful, just... closed. The same way they'd been since the seconds this morning when they'd opened and found nothing. Blank. Distant. Like she was in the room and somewhere else entirely, and neither place had her.

*If only you would open them and look at me.*

The thought hollowed my stomach before I had words for it.

*What were you carrying alone?*

Two months ago. Her voice at the door of my house, reaching for her bag on the hook by the door. *"Sorry I'll be right back... need to... go get something."* The hesitations. The flicker on her face I couldn't name. I'd thought soda, or something else mundane. Let it go.

*Why didn't you tell us?*

I hadn't let it go. I'd filed it under odd and never asked. And now the inner pocket was right there, and I knew what was coming, and I couldn't ask her about it. Couldn't explain. Couldn't soften it.

*What did you know that we missed? How long?*

My hand was still on the tray table. The plastic cool under my palm. My stomach was doing something I didn't have words for, and my throat was tight just below the point where speech would start. After four items with words for each one, I had nothing.

The monitor beeped. The IV dripped. Her face didn't move.

I looked down at the bag.

My hand left the tray table and found the inner pocket by feel — the zipper cool under my thumb, the weight inside pressing against the lining.

My fingers knew before my eyes did. The cold. The particular weight of a Stillglass safety case. I'd been in the room when Riley tested one. The weight was the same.

I pulled out the first box. Set it on the tray table, label facing away from me. The second. Then the third. The sealed boxes among Riley's things on the tray table. I didn't separate them. All of it was her.

Leo's spellbook was on the sill. He'd been reading. Now he wasn't.

"The fuck," he said. Very quiet.

My head came up. "Umm what?" His face had already changed.

I turned the nearest box toward me.

Celestia Crystalli.

Pale gold. Stillglass had said it made the celestial parts come up whether you wanted them or not. I'd felt it — surfacing at rest in the Stillglass room.

My hand went flat on the box. The cold of the safety case under my palm.

Marisol. And Leo. And me. And Riley had been carrying it in her bag. Inches from us.

I turned the next box and read the label.

Noctilux Crystalli.

My stomach dropped — the same sick feeling from the bar. Clear ice in Leo's water that wasn't ice. Wrong things showing up in the wrong hands — dark on my knuckles, light on his. The bond turned into a weapon.

My knees folded — everything leaving at once — and I went down. Leo caught me. Stillglass told us him being close made it worse. I could feel it through the bond. Rough. Wordless.

The box was a few inches from my hand. Sealed. Riley had been carrying it this whole time. Every day since the bar. The church. Thorncrown Road. Days in this room. Next to her phone.

I turned the last box.

Lacuna Crystalli.

The name landed behind my ribs. Heavier than the others.

I was in the room when Riley tested this one. She asked for me — "I want somebody I trust in there. And I want it to be Luna specifically." I sat behind her — close enough to reach, not close enough to crowd. I talked her through the sequence drift — "Your hand is on the table. The crystal is on the tray." She was scared and she asked for me.

After, I asked if she wanted a hug. She said yes. I stepped in. Held her. The weight of her against my chest. She leaned into me like standing had used up everything.

Now she was four feet away in a hospital bed. Unconscious. The crystal — the one she'd killed with, closed again, and kept carrying — was on the tray table.

My hand stayed flat on the Lacuna box. The cold of the case. My jaw tight.

"The rude mint."

"The evil ice." My eyes cut to Leo. His mouth went flat. His hand stopped moving on the spellbook. Just pressure through the bond.

"The beautiful jerk."

Echo's voice came from the bedside. I didn't turn my head.

"Those are the ones that target you. Right?" She paused. "You and Leo and..."

Her voice thinned before she got the name out.

"Marisol."

My eyes stayed on the boxes. The blanket went quiet near Riley's wrist.

I didn't mean to line them up. My brain did it anyway. Riley had prepared for all of us. She was right, and I hated that — hated it in the flat of my hand still pressed to the cold case.

Lacuna had opened. The Celestia and Noctilux boxes — still sealed. She'd carried them anyway. She never said a word.

"The fuck," I said. Quieter than he had.

My brain tried to make it homework. It didn't help.

Then I remembered her weight against my shoulder. I had held her then. I couldn't now.

The monitor chirped.

Not the steady beep I'd been hearing for four days. A different pitch. My breath didn't change. My eyes snapped to the screen — the monitor trace jumping, settling higher. Somewhere behind that, Leo's voice: One-thirty-two. Tight. The number landed in the room before I could decide whether I'd read it or heard it.

My heart kicked. Hard. Once. My chest went tight around it and held. I knew what the number meant. My body got there first.

I looked at Riley.

The blanket moved. A hitch — one short catch in the breathing that had been shallow and even for four days. Sweat at her hairline, catching the hospital light. My hope didn't ask permission. It just came — bright and sharp and so big I couldn't hold it all at once. She was surfacing. She was still in there.

Then her eyes opened.

Fully. No slow flutter, no gradual waking up. Just open. And her head turned — toward me, like she knew exactly where I was. I was still at the tray table, my hand flat beside her wallet, the only one in the room with a clean angle on her face.

The web was across her eye and she was looking at me and both things hit at once.

White lightning threaded through her eye — fine forks branching, holding. Wrong-pale and cold and somehow still wet, like current moving inside a living eye. And her gaze on mine. The web and the being-seen arriving together. I couldn't pull them apart. They landed in my chest as one thing: warmth and terror, her looking at me and something wrong inside her, both true, both now.

My hand didn't move from the table. My jaw did.

The hope didn't go anywhere. It was still there, bright and sharp and mine. My throat closed. Hands around it. Squeezing — like someone was there. She was still looking at me.

"Hey... you..."

I forced it out. The words had to push past the hands. They came out quiet. Almost under my breath. Thin. Reaching for someone I couldn't prove was there.

Her focus held. The warmth still in my chest. The terror alongside it.

Inside, the words ran too fast to become sound: *no no no no no no no please no don't fucking go*.

None of it came out. The scream filled everything. My jaw set so hard it hurt. The scream kept going.

Then her focus drifted. The thing behind her eyes dissolved while I watched. The web still branched — the forks still there, still wrong-pale — but she wasn't behind them anymore. She was still looking at me but she wasn't seeing me. She was losing me and I couldn't stop it.

Her eyes closed. The web was gone.

She lost the room again.

Somewhere behind me Echo's voice broke — her name, Weave's name, a question I couldn't make out. Weave's voice answered in the back of my mind, steady and quiet. I didn't catch the words. Nothing reached me. The room was gone. I was gone from it.

I didn't move from the tray table. Hand flat beside her wallet. Jaw locked.

And then — through the screaming, through the nothing — the words came. My own voice, quiet and clear, inside my head where nobody else could hear it.

*She carried all three of them alone. Never said a word.*

Her wallet was beside my hand. Her phone facedown. Her three boxes still on the tray table where I'd set them. Her battery pack. Her red cable.

*She saw me.*

For a few seconds she had been there. Looking at me. Her head turned toward me and her eyes on mine and the web in her eye and she was there. Not the armed protector in the lock screen. Not the tiny almost-smile on the ID. Her. Riley.

*Now she's gone again.*

My hand pressed flat against the tray table.

The surface was cold — institutional smooth plastic, the same surface I'd been touching for four days. I held it there. Counted one breath. In. Out. Steady. I could do one breath.

The next one didn't come.

My jaw unlocked. The sound came up through the scream that had filled everything after her eyes closed, and I couldn't stop it.

I was crying. Loud. The room heard it. I didn't try to be quiet.

My hand slid off the tray table. The cold plastic — the last thing I'd been anchored to — gone.

The words ran underneath everything: *what the fuck Riley, why, why, why*. Not questions. Not looking for answers. Just the raw shape of the thing I couldn't hold anymore. She'd carried all of it. All of us. Never said a word. She'd turned her head and looked at me and now she was gone again.

Marisol's voice cut through. Quiet. Direct. The same even register she'd used for the timeline — steady and factual and warm underneath in a way she never asked to be thanked for. A Private Message. Just for me.

Marisol's message arrived simple and direct: let it out.

Two words. Not a command. Not comfort. Permission. She'd been holding the door for four days and now she was telling me I didn't have to hold anything.

I couldn't answer. Couldn't. But the last muscle I'd been using to keep it together — the one I didn't know was still there — let go. The crying got louder. There was nothing in the room but the sound of me breaking.

Then warmth. Across my back. Pressure. The hard shape of the splint on my shoulder.

I didn't see him come. Didn't feel him through the bond. Didn't hear the coffee hit the sill or the spellbook stay behind. But the moment his arms were around me — his left hand flat against my back, his splinted wrist resting on my shoulder, the specific weight of him I'd known my whole life — my body knew. No flinch. No gap. I was already crying into his shoulder.

The words were still there — *what the fuck Riley, why* — quieter now, like something underwater. The question didn't go away. It just wasn't crushing me anymore. Leo's hand pressed harder against my back. The splint's hard edge stayed careful on my shoulder. I cried and didn't try to be quiet and didn't try to stop.

She'd carried all three of them alone. She'd seen me. She was gone. The truth of it was still there — the same weight, the same unanswered why — but I wasn't holding it by myself anymore.
