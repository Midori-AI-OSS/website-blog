A nurse I don't know stands in the doorway. She waits until I look up.

She knocks on the door frame — soft, two taps, like she doesn't want to startle anyone. Her badge says Grace. There's a faded sunflower on the reel — green, the print worn thin at the edges. She smells like lavender before I even see her move.

"Echo? It's time for your scan, hon."

I look at Riley. The monitors keep their rhythm. Luna glances up from her laptop on the other side of the bed. Leo looks over from the small table near the window. Marisol shifts at the door — checking, then settling when she sees it's just the nurse.

I don't want to go.

Grace doesn't step in. She stays in the doorway, giving me the choice.

"The shuttle runs every twenty minutes. We can catch this one or the next. Up to you."

Luna says something low. "I'll be right here. She won't be alone."

I push up from the visitor chair. My legs are stiff from sitting too long.

"Okay," I say. It comes out rough but it's there.

Grace nods once. "I'll be with you the whole way. There and back."

She walks me to a small room down the hall — not a patient room, a prep alcove with a chair and a counter and a box of gloves on the wall. She has me sit.

She pulls a syringe from the cart and uncaps it. The needle is thin. She swabs the IV port in my right forearm. Her hands are warm — warmer than I expected. The lavender hand cream, the silver band on her ring finger, worn thin.

"You want something to take the edge off before the machine? It'll make you relaxed. Floaty. You'll be awake, just... chill."

"Yeah."

She nods. "It'll come on slow. Last a while."

She pushes the syringe. The cold spreads up my arm. Heavy. Slow. My arm feels far away.

She pulls the curtain closed. I can hear her on the other side — footsteps, then still.

The dress comes off next. The fabric pools at my feet and I step out. The cold air hits my arms. My back.

The gown is folded on the bench. Pale blue. Thin. The kind that ties at the back with two little strings. I put it on. The fabric is wrong — not soft, not anything. Rough in the wrong way. The ties are cold plastic snaps. I do them up without looking. There's a gap down my spine I can feel the air in.

I set the dress on top of the cardigan in the locker. My own clothes, folded. Small pile. I press one palm flat against the wool for half a second.

The table slides in.

Pause.

A whine. High. Climbing. Then another pattern — faster banging, a different rhythm. I track the changes. The ceiling of the tube. The foam at my ears. The patterns switching.

Bang-bang-bang. Pause. Whine. Bang-bang-bang-bang. Different this time.

"Echo, do you know what day it is?"

The words take longer than I mean them to. "Saturday." Or... no. What day is it. I try again. "Day... four. In the hospital. Four days." That's not what they asked.

"You're doing great. Almost done with this sequence."

The machine switches rhythms. The banging gets faster, then slower, then stops. A different whine — lower, longer. The table vibrates differently. I notice when the lights dim between sequences. The tube is close. The sounds keep changing. I wait.

Time does the stretched-out thing. I can't tell how long I've been in here. The sequences blend. Bang-bang-bang. Pause. Whine. A new pattern I haven't heard yet. I can still feel the cold from the contrast in my arm. The metallic taste is fading but not gone.

The machine stops. The silence hits me. I didn't know how loud the noise was until it stopped.

The table slides me out. The room is cold after the tube. The lights are too bright — I blink at the ceiling and my eyes water. The foam blocks come off. The headphones come off. Sam says something — I catch "all done" and not much else. I'm slow from the meds and the noise.
