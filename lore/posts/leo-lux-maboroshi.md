The white hit the road ahead of us and I braked before I finished thinking about it.

Hicks had already stopped. Luna's staff hit the footwell as she braced. In the mirror, the Bolt's headlights swung wide for half a second, then Echo pulled it straight.

One figure in the road.

Lady Light. White hair. Black sparkles. The wrong side of her face still — same damage, unchanged since the house. But now there was blood on her, fresh damage layered over the pre-existing stillness. Her left arm — the one that had always been dead weight — hung heavier than before.

She limped forward.

Then the limp broke.

One step too clean. Left leg smooth. Left arm correcting under the sleeve. A body that had learned the shape but not the injury.

Weave went tight along our windshield.

I opened my door.

Hicks was already out, hand raised, shouting. Luna was slower on her side — staff first, then feet, right shoulder tucked. I tracked her movement while I moved forward. She was clear and armed, bad side protected.

The copy lifted one hand. Gold-white particles gathered there, denser than ambient light should be, building into a spiral.

Then the Bolt took a hit from above.

Metal report. Suspension drop. The kind of impact that travels through your jaw before you decide what to do with it.

I looked back. A dark shape was on the Bolt's roof, sliding forward. It dropped onto the hood — Lady Darkness's shape, but wrong. Too still in the way it settled. Too arranged.

The windshield cracked. The shape on the hood drove a palm through the glass and a hand went in after it. Echo jerked forward in the driver's seat — the copy had her by the throat.

The copy's shoulders squared. Not choking. Setting for a twist. I knew the motion.

A gunshot cracked from the Bolt behind me. Riley firing.

The twist broke. Echo's head snapped back against the headrest. The copy's hand came off her throat and the same motion swung sideways — she was on the hood and then she was off it. The copy threw her. Echo hit the pavement in front of the Bolt hard enough that I felt the impact through the road.

I did not stop moving.

Ahead of us, the fake Lady Light raised her right hand. Light blades started forming around it — thin, bright, edge-sharp. Spinning. Picking up speed. The air started humming with them.

She was looking at Hicks.

I kept moving. Bolt-side had a response now. Front-side still did not.

I kept moving toward Hicks.


Then a sound cut through everything.

Not an engine. Not anything I recognized. A tearing sound, like something splitting the air overhead.

The fake Lady Light in front of us went still. The blades kept spinning but her head turned — toward the road ahead, not toward Hicks anymore. Her face changed. The composure cracked.

Fear.

I looked up.

Dark wings — smoke and shadow — came in fast overhead.

They hit the Bolt behind me.

I did not turn. Luna was already casting — four photon blades cutting past me toward the fake Lady Light. I had my opening.

I put shadow on the blade.

I went in under the light. Low. Fast. The sword took the space Luna's spell opened.

The fake Lady Light was still looking up when I reached her.

She was fast.

She turned into the swing — not dodging, redirecting. The light blades around her hand swept low. I had to pull back or lose my footing. One of them cut past my shoulder. Didn't connect. Close enough to feel the heat.

She wasn't scared anymore. Or she was, but it had turned into something worse.

The blades spun tighter. She pressed forward — right hand leading with the light, left hand raising something behind it. A second spell forming. Gold-white particles gathered near her fingers.

I had to stay close. Longer reach meant her light blades had the advantage. I stepped in again, shadow trailing the sword edge.

The tearing sound came back overhead. Louder. Closer. A rip through the air I couldn't place.

I did not look up. She was right in front of me and I had no room for a mistake.

One of her blades caught my sword arm. Shallow. Across the forearm. I didn't feel it yet — the cold of the cut came first, then the sting.

Weave was trying to say something. I caught fragments between swings.

__Tracking: target ascending.__
__Velocity: 18.3—__
__Altitude: 200.__
__300.__
__400.__
__500+.__

__Projected impact—__
__Splash radius—__
__Echo within—__

Then something hit me from behind.

Wet. Wrong temperature. Not water. Not blood either — too cold, too thin, too fast. It splattered across my back and the side of my neck like something thrown from a height.

The smell hit a second later. Burnt sugar. Copper. Something else underneath I didn't have a name for.

The darkness on the blade flickered. Like a candle in wind. Then steadied.

I didn't know what had hit me. I didn't have time to find out.

The fake Lady Light changed.

Her face shifted — the composure from before was gone. Not fear now. Rage. Pure and personal and aimed at something behind me that she could feel and I couldn't see.

She screamed.

Not words. Just sound. The light blades around her hand tripled — faster, brighter, spinning so tight they blurred into a single ring of white.

She came at me like I had killed something she loved.

I blocked the first two. The third caught my blade and twisted it. The fourth came for my throat. I dropped my shoulder and let it pass over me — close enough that the heat dried the residue on my neck.

I tried to reach Weave.

__Echo—__

Fragment. Barely a whisper through the noise.

__...still—__

Static ripped across it.

__Front—__

Then nothing. Just the space where her voice should have been. Silence. Not even static. Just gone.

Pressure hit behind my eyes — sharp and wrong, a spike of noise inside my skull. I faltered. Half a step. Maybe less.

The ground shook. Not an impact — a landing. Something heavy enough to rattle the road through my boots.

I caught movement at the edge of my vision. Dark wings folding — smoke and shadow settling. Marisol — dark hair, dark feathers, crystal rods in her hair glowing low. Spell foci — the magic showed in her hands, but the rods were the source.

She didn't stop. The magic came forward — shadow-wrapped, fast, aimed past me at the fake Lady Light.

I didn't ask. I used the opening.

The shadow on my blade was steady now. The disorientation was still there — humming at the back of my skull, wrong and cold — but I could move through it.

Luna's casting lit the road to my left. Photon blades cutting arcs through the dark. The winged woman was on my right, shadow pooling from her hands.

We pressed the fake Lady Light together.

Then the residue burned.

Not surface heat. Not the sting of the cut on my arm. Something deeper — wrong heat, the kind that doesn't come from outside. It crawled through my back where the splatter had hit me, crawled up my neck, and then it found the wound on my forearm and concentrated there like it had been looking for the opening.

Luna's breath caught to my left. She felt it too. Weave's particles flared — a sharp brightening, then dim again. Echo, somewhere behind me — I heard her inhale wrong. The magic-touched only. The fake Lady Light didn't flinch.

The burning passed fast. Three seconds, maybe four. Left a cold aftertaste behind my teeth and a clarity I didn't want.

I pulled shadow over the arm wound while I was still moving. Quick field seal — not healing, just closing. The darkness wrapped tight around the cut and held. Functional. It would hold until I had time to do it properly.

I didn't have time now.

Luna's photon blades had carved an opening on the clone's left side. Marisol's shadow was pressing it on the right. I saw the gap and I took it.

I went for the kill.

The sword drove through — shadow-wrapped edge, full weight behind it. The clone's body took the hit and did not stop. The wound opened across its torso and it kept coming like the damage was someone else's problem. No stagger. No correction. Just forward motion with a hole in it.

It was already turning.

I tracked its eyes — wrong eyes, too arranged, too still in the face — and saw where they landed. Past me. Past Luna. Toward the Bolt behind us. Toward Echo. Toward Riley.

I was already sprinting before I thought about it.

The clone moved faster.

I got between it and them — or started to. It planted a foot and shoved me flat. Not a spell. Not a blade. Just physics and bad leverage. A kick or a shoulder, I couldn't tell which. I went down hard. Road met my back before my arms caught up.

The impact rang through my chest. Vision grayed at the edges for half a second.

Then something caught me.

Not the road. Not the impact stop I was braced for. A wing — dark smoke and shadow — swept under me and broke the fall. Marisol. She didn't stop fighting. The wing adjusted, righted me, and pulled back before I finished finding my feet. I was up again. No words. No check-in. Just the catch and the release.

I looked at the clone and my stomach turned.

Its head was half regrown.

I'd seen the face shift earlier — that moment when the composure cracked and something real from Lady Light surfaced underneath. That had been wrong enough. This was worse. The regrowth wasn't clean. Skin lay wrong over the reforming bone. Features didn't line up — one eye socket higher than the other, the mouth pulling at an angle that wasn't expression, like it was built wrong. The hair was filling in patchy, white with black sparkles but in the wrong places, like a drawing traced by someone who'd only seen the original once.

I faltered. Half a step. Maybe less. Long enough.

Luna didn't.

She dropped the photon blades and switched grip on her staff — both hands now, left taking weight, right braced through the shoulder wound. I knew that stance. She was about to cast something big.

I moved before she finished setting her feet. Put myself between her and the clone. Not a fighting stance. A wall. Shoulders square. Sword up. Nothing gets past me.

The clone's reforming face tracked toward us. The light blades around its hand were still spinning — slower now, but still edge-sharp — and the gold-white particles from its other hand had built into something denser. A new spell forming. I didn't know what. I didn't need to know. Luna was behind me, staff raised, spell-lines tightening in the air. The clone was not getting to her.

Then the clone turned its head again — that wrong, half-finished movement — and looked past me toward the treeline.

Riley was there. I don't know when she'd moved from the Bolt. I don't know how long she'd been visible. But the clone saw her.

The light blade in its hand lengthened — thin, bright, extending farther than I'd ever seen it reach. It drove the blade forward. Not at me. Not at Luna.

Riley took it through the body.

I saw it hit. Saw the light go through. Saw Riley's weight shift wrong as her body tried to process damage faster than pain could register.

I reached for her with healing. The spell left my hand — shadow-wrapped, the way my healing always was, a closing force, not a light — and crossed the distance toward her.

It never landed.

The clone's counterspell hit mine midair. I felt it as collision — not visual, not sound. A pressure against the working, then a tear. My spell came apart. The shadow I'd sent toward Riley scattered like ash in wind. The closing force I'd shaped for her wound dissolved before it touched skin.

Someone else might have described it as a wall. It wasn't a wall. A wall has a surface. This was absence — a place where my magic stopped being magic and became nothing.

I tried again. The clone's counterspell was already there. Already waiting. Already undoing what I hadn't finished sending.

Riley was still standing. Bleeding. The light blade had withdrawn. The clone's attention was already swinging back toward us, toward Luna, toward the spell she was building behind me.

I couldn't reach Riley. The wound stayed open. Nothing I tried —

The sigil sealed.

Luna's spell finished. A boundary of gold-white light snapped into place around us — clean radius, sharp edge, the air inside it suddenly still in a way the road hadn't been since we stopped. The light traced a ring in the pavement and held.

I was inside it. Luna was beside me, staff still up, breath coming hard. Marisol was beside us — dark wings folded, smoke and shadow at rest. Weave's particles were inside with us, a thin gold-green shimmer near the edge of the ring.

__Containment field detected.__
__Full operational capacity restored.__
__I am combat ready.__

The clone was inside too.

Echo and Riley were outside. I could see them through the boundary — the light was translucent, gold-white, solid enough to hold but clear enough to track what was on the other side. Riley was down on one knee. Echo's hand was on her shoulder. They were on the other side of the line. Protected. Separated.

The clone was still here. Still reforming. Still turning that half-finished face toward us.

I stood beside Luna. Sword ready. The shadow on the blade held steady.

The clone was still here. So were we.

I saw the particles move.

Not the thin gold-green shimmer that had been near the sigil's edge — something denser. A ribbon of iridescence peeling away from the core swarm and crossing the sigil toward Luna. Violet to teal to rose as it caught the light from the barrier.

It went to her right shoulder.

The same shoulder I had been watching all night — the one she kept tucked, the one that made her cast left-handed, the one I put myself in front of when she set her feet for the sigil. The particles wrapped it in a close lattice. Not a grip. A brace. Structural. Taking the weight the injury had been making her carry alone.

Then a second ring of particles snapped outward into a defensive orbit around her — circling at arm's reach, densest at the plane an attack would cross first. Shield pattern. I knew what I was looking at.

Her voice reached me — calm, precise, the same register she had used from the convoy to the sigil.

__Luna — shield active.__

I did not say anything. The clone was still standing half-regrown in front of us and my sword arm was still up.

I looked at Luna. At the brace on her shoulder. At the orbit around her. The particles were holding what I had been trying to hold all night with my body between her and the threat.

Weave had put armor where my healing could not reach.

I stayed where I was. Sword ready. Between Luna and the clone. But the space beside me felt different. Fuller.

I did not have time to be grateful.

The clone turned its head — away from the sigil boundary, away from wherever it had been looking — and its eyes found me. The half-regrown face tracked toward me with that wrong assembly-error movement, and the light blades around its hand were already spinning again.

I moved to meet it. Sword up. Shadow on the blade. Two steps.

It covered the distance in one.

Its right hand closed on my sword wrist. Not a block — a grab. Gold-white particles flared around the grip and something in my forearm gave. The sound was inside me before it was in the air. Bone. I felt the break travel up through my elbow before the sword hit the ground.

I did not have time to process the pain. Its other hand was already at my throat.

Then the ground was not under my feet.

It lifted me. One hand around my neck, the other fisting the front of my coat. Not magic. Physical. The kind of strength that did not care about leverage or form or the shadow still flickering weakly at my fingers. My boots cleared the pavement. My hands grabbed at its wrist on reflex, and the darkness at my fingertips — the closing force I had been holding since the fight started — guttered and went out.

It brought me down.

The road met my back with a sound that came through my spine before it reached my ears. The impact split the pavement. I felt it crack under me — felt the crater open like the road had been waiting for permission to break.

I was on my back in the bottom of a crater that had not been there three seconds ago. One arm was bent wrong beneath me. The other was the one the clone had snapped at the wrist, and it was not answering. My sword was somewhere in the dark past the edge of the hole. The staff and spellbook were still on me. Didn't matter. Couldn't reach either one.

My vision grayed at the edges. Tried to pull it back. Could not.

I could hear. That was all I had left for a long few seconds. The clone's light was still humming somewhere above me. Marisol's wings — I heard them beat once, twice, repositioning. And Luna. I could not see her clearly through the gray, but I could hear her breathing change. The way it changed when she was about to do something that cost.

I forced my eyes to focus.

Luna had her glimmersteel longsword out. It was in her right hand — the shoulder Weave had braced, the arm that had been useless all night — and raw light was climbing the blade. Not the clean gold of her halo. Not the sharp blue of her photon blades. Something hotter. Something that did not spiral or shape or follow a spell. It just burned.

Marisol came in from the clone's blind side. Dark wings opening. Shadow pouring ahead of her. They hit the clone together — not twins, not practiced, just two people who had decided the same thing at the same time. Shadow held the clone's left side. Light drove the right.

The clone's particle-wall came up — that flat disc of gold-white static. The light on Luna's sword touched it.

The wall melted.

Not shattered. Melted. Like wax. The clone's face changed. I saw it through the gray. The rage dropped. Something else — something that looked almost like recognition — crossed what was left of its features.

Luna drove the blade through its chest.

The clone came apart. Not like a body. Like light that had been holding a shape it was never meant to keep. Gold-white particles scattered upward. The silhouette thinned. The edges blurred. The face was the last thing to go — both sides of it finally matching, finally still.

Then nothing. Just the road. Just the dark. Just Luna standing where it had been with the sword still hot in her hand.

She had killed it. My sister had killed it.

I tried to get up. My body told me no. I stayed where I was and let the gray take the edges for a while longer.

The sigil fell.

I felt it more than I saw it — a change in the air pressure, the way the road stopped humming with that low gold frequency Luna's wards always put into the ground. Through the gray at the edges of my vision, I saw the warm disc dissolve into streamers of light and then nothing.

Weave's particles went past me like a river. The whole swarm — gold-green iridescence that had been pressed against the inside of the barrier — flooded toward the treeline. Toward Riley. I tracked them as far as my eyes would focus, which was not far.

Luna was already moving. Two steps past the sigil boundary. Then she was on her knees beside Riley, and I could not see what she was doing, but I knew. Riley had been stabbed. The light blade had gone through her. I had seen it happen. I had tried to close the wound and the clone had stopped me. Now Luna was doing what I could not.

I stayed on my back for another three seconds. Maybe four. The gray was still there. My left arm was still not answering. My right wrist was broken and I could feel the bone shift every time my pulse pushed through it.

Fuck you logic.

I planted my good hand — the left one, the one that still worked — against the broken edge of the crater and pushed. My chest told me to stop. I did not stop. I got one knee under me, then the other. The road tilted. I waited for it to settle. It did not settle. I stood up anyway.

The pavement was still warm from the particle-storm. My sword was somewhere in the dark and I could not bend to look for it. Blood was in my hair, down the back of my neck, soaking through the coat where the impact had opened skin.

Marisol was there.

She had been at the crater's edge without me noticing — wings folded, one of the crystal rods in her hair glowing a weak lavender. Her hand was already pressed to my chest. Triage magic. Barely more than a stopgap. I felt it working at the edges of the damage, taking the sharpest corners off, leaving the rest for later.

"He's stable," she said to someone behind me. Her voice was rough. "Focus on Riley."

That was meant for Luna. I did not turn to see if Luna heard it. I was busy staying vertical.

Marisol did not try to stop me. She shifted her hand to my shoulder and let the weak lavender light keep running while I found my balance. Smart. She knew I was getting up whether she approved or not.

I looked toward the treeline. Luna was on the ground beside Riley, her hand on Riley's chest. Golden light there — steady, pulsing, the rhythm of it matching a heartbeat I knew better than my own. Radiant Anchor. I had seen her cast it once before. It cost her.

Then her staff was up. White light. Fracturing. Color bands I could not resolve from this distance. She was closing the wound.

Riley was still alive. If she were not, Luna would not be casting like that.

Good. That was one thing I did not have to carry.

I turned to face the road — the dark beyond the headlights, whatever was still out there waiting. My body was a list of things that were not working, but my eyes were clear and my legs were holding. That was enough to start with.

Hicks was at the treeline.

I had not seen him arrive. He must have been there the whole time. Through the sigil, the crater — whatever had gone down, he had stayed on his feet near where Riley was lying. When he saw me standing he started toward me without being called.

I met him halfway. Or close enough. My steps were not straight and I was favoring my good side, but I was moving. That was what mattered.

"Perimeter," I said. One word. My voice came out wrong — hoarse, thin, the kind of sound a body makes when it has used up all its air on impact and has not gotten around to replacing it.

Hicks nodded. He did not ask if I was okay. He did not tell me to sit down. He just fell in beside me and started scanning the dark past the headlights the same way I was.

Good man.

We moved along the treeline — slow, careful, the way you move when you are down one functional arm and the rest of your body is crater-battered. I could not hold a sword. Did not matter. My eyes tracked fine and my head was clear. I was still between whatever was out there and the people on the ground behind me.

The road stretched dark in both directions. Headlights from the Malibu and Hicks' cruiser carved two pools of light that did not reach far enough. Past them, nothing but trees and the idea of movement. I watched the spaces between the trunks. The shadows that were too still. The shadows that were not.

Behind me, Luna was still working.

I did not need to turn around to know. I could feel it — the twin-bond hum when her magic shifted registers, the focused note of her working. Marisol was with her. She was healing. I knew that without seeing it. The light on the ground in front of me kept changing color — white fracturing, red and blue and gold bands bleeding across the dirt — and that told me enough. The hum through the bond was thinning. She would be hollowed out and still casting, because that was what she did.

Her staff was in Echo's hands. I did not have to look to know that. Luna would not have put it anywhere else.

I kept scanning. The dark did not change. The trees did not move. The road stayed empty.

Then Weave's voice came into the back of my mind. Calm. Precise. The way she always sounded when she was about to say something I did not want to hear.

__Movement detected.__

__Bearing: northeast.__

__Distance: closing.__

__Estimate: one contact. Classification: caster. Inbound.__

Then Riley moved.

Not the kind of movement you check on. The kind you recognize. Weave's particles shifted around her — the gold-green shimmer that had been pressed against her wound. Not healing now. Building. Flat planes of light beneath her hands. A brace forming around her ribs where the clone's blade had gone through. She must have asked Weave for help. Weave was answering — not with words I could hear, but with structure.

Riley forced herself upright. One knee. Then the other. The brace held. The platform shifted under her weight. She was broken and bleeding and using everything she had left to get vertical, and I knew exactly what that cost because I had just paid the same price from the bottom of a crater.

Fuck you logic. She had it too.

I heard her say it. Out loud. Not loud enough to carry past the treeline, but I was close enough and the air was still.

"I am not done."

She was on her feet. Her shirt was bloody front and back where the blade had passed through. Her hair was loose, tangled, catching what light there was. She looked like she had already died once tonight and decided it did not count. She was unsteady, bleeding through the brace, but standing. The sword at her hip caught what light there was. Her arm found Echo's shoulders — not hard, not urgent, just there.

That was the thing I was protecting. The two of them. The reason I was standing between the dark and my people.

We were all still here, barely.

I did not stop scanning. My eyes stayed on the tree line. My body stayed between the dark and the people behind me.

The fight was not over.

It had not even been a fight. It had been the first wave. The clone was dead, but whatever had sent it was still out there. Still moving. Still coming.

I stood where I was, broken and unarmed, watching the road.

It would have to go through me first.

Slow clapping started out beyond the headlights.

Once. Then again. Unhurried.

Hicks and I shifted just enough to let Riley move between us when she stepped forward. Weave's brace held around her ribs. She only came a little past our line. Her hand stayed off the sword.

I stayed where I was. Hicks stayed with me. We let her pass and kept the space tight around her.

Luna did not leave Echo. Echo's hands closed harder around Luna's staff. She turned toward the sound before I did. When I looked back, her eyes were open all the way. A thin light had started along the staff. Faint. Not enough for a real cast.

Then the man stepped into the headlights.

White. Short gray hair. Work boots. Jeans. Plaid shirt under a work jacket. Rough hands. Sun-lined face. Clean. Plain. Farm-country ordinary.

He kept clapping while he walked toward us. Slowed when he got close enough to see us clearly, but did not fully stop.

"Good job," he said. "Those were my best clones yet. Be a shame not to use the others too."

Riley's voice cut across the road. Raw. Scraped. Still standing.

"Maybe we try something better. One-on-one. Me and you."

I saw her hand go into her bag. Not reaching for the sword. The inner pocket. She did not pull anything out. Her hand just settled there, resting on something I could not see. I did not know what it was. I did not have time to wonder.

She leaned her head toward the gold-green shimmer still wrapped around her shoulder. Her mouth moved. Whispered something I could not catch from my angle. The bismuth particles near her collarbone brightened once and stilled. Whatever it meant, I didn't understand the flicker. I saw it and stayed on the perimeter.

The caster cocked his head. Made a show of it. His eyes traveled down Riley's body the way someone sizes up livestock, not a threat. His eyebrow lifted. His mouth twisted into something that was not quite a smile.

"Well now." He dragged the words out. "That's... real cute."

One step closer. Too casual for a man standing in the middle of the damage he had made.

"Little thing like you. All tore up. Barely standin'. Got blood soakin' through your front and your back both." Farm-country plain. The kind of voice that should have been harmless. "And you want to go one-on-one?"

He laughed. Not a chuckle. A real laugh. Deep and long enough that it stopped being about what Riley had said and started being about him enjoying the sound of it. When it faded, he wiped at the corner of his eye with his thumb.

"Alright," he said. "Sure. Why not. Been a while since I got to play with my food."

Riley did not answer him. She turned her head toward Luna and me.

"Luna. Leo. Please keep us locked together."

I knew what she was asking. I did not need a second word.

Luna was already in motion — spellbook open, the pages catching light that was not from the headlights. Dawnbreak Sigil. I recognized the framework before the gold finished forming under her tracing fingers. Her one-directional barrier. I knew that spell. I knew what it cost her. I knew she was going to cast it anyway.

My right wrist was broken — wrong angle, the bone shifting every time my pulse pushed through it. The coat was torn across the back. Blood was still drying in my hair from the crater impact. Did not matter. Riley had asked. Luna was already casting. I was not going to make her hold a full containment alone.

I reached back with my left hand. The one that still worked. My staff came free from the loop on my back — the crystal tip already catching what light there was. Dark tendrils unspooled from it — smoke-like shadow, threading across the road. Eclipsing Sigil. My spell. I knew the shape of it without thinking. The containment geometry. The seal-weight. The way the shadow would need to thread through Luna's gold to make something harder than either of us could build alone.

The twin-bond hummed at the back of my skull. I could feel Luna's magic — the focused note of her working, the drain already opening behind her ribs. My shadow reached for her light before the gold was halfway formed. Not fighting. Threading.

Her Dawnbreak framework absorbed my dark threads like ink soaking into paper. The gold stretched toward the shadow. The two spells wove alongside each other — her one-directional barrier, my full-seal containment — and pulled into walls of black glass around the road.

The air changed. Pressure dropped. I felt it in my ears, in my bones, in the place behind my eyes that was still raw from the signal burst. The cast pulled through my palm — the tendrils kept coming, smoke and shadow threading into the gold, and every second of it wrenched the arm the clone had wrecked. The pain was not sharp. It was deep and spreading and wrong in a way that meant the break was worse than a simple snap. I did not stop. The box needed a ceiling. The spell needed to finish.

The seal closed overhead. The hum settled into something steady. Holding.

I let my hand drop. My left arm was shaking from the cast. The right one was useless. The twin-bond was still humming Luna's drain alongside my own. I stayed on my feet. That was all I needed to do.

Echo was at the glass.

I did not see her move. One second the treeline was empty. The next she was there — pressed against the black-glass wall, close enough to touch it, Luna's staff still in her grip. The crystal tip flared once and dimmed.

She had not walked there. Misty Step. Had to be. Nothing else put someone at a box edge between one blink and the next. She was supposed to be on the ground. She was supposed to be recovering. She was here.

I did not say anything. The glass was between us. I stayed on the perimeter and watched.

Inside the box, Riley pulled her hand from the bag.

Not empty. A small sealed case. Safety casing — clear, compact. Inside it I could see something white. Crystalline. Red lines tracing its edges like cracks in something that should not be cracked. Ring-sized. Sealed.

I dropped.

One knee hit the road before I decided to kneel. My staff bounced off the asphalt. The dark threading guttered — flickered — for one long second it pulled thin, almost gone.

Then the twin-bond caught.

Luna's hit came through the dark threading in her gold — a weight that was not mine settling into the space behind my ribs where her magic always sat. I felt the Dawnbreak Sigil dim. Felt her light flicker and pull thin. For a moment neither of us was holding — my shadow and her light both on the wrong side of the line — and then her light steadied. Found the seal.

So did mine.

The box held.

I stayed on one knee for another second. My right arm was useless. The bone shifted every time my pulse pushed through it. My left hand stayed on the staff, still channeling, the dark threading running through the wood like cold current. I did not let go.

The cruiser had stopped dead against the glass. Steam rose from the crumpled hood in thin white threads. The driver's door opened.

A cop got out. Local uniform. The way he moved was not local. Gun drawn but not aimed. Held low and ready. His eyes went to the box first — the impossible black-glass walls sealing the road shut — and then to us. His radio came up. Steady. Professional. I could not hear the words, but I knew the rhythm. Calling it in.

More headlights rounded the bend behind him. Slower. State cop cars — two, then a third — pulling off to the shoulder in a clean line. Doors opened. Boots on asphalt.

The third car opened last. Lady Light got out first — moving carefully, the same deliberate economy from the house. Lady Darkness followed. They did not look like prisoners.

Hicks was already out of his car and working the scene — assembling pieces, routing information. He caught my eye for half a second.

Then I saw it.

The cops approaching the box. One slowed. Stopped walking. Looked down at his hands and stood there staring. Another — younger, state cop — blinked twice and stared at his radio. A third one's hand went to his belt and stopped halfway.

I knew that look. Stillglass. The pause. The double-check. The body checking for a fact that kept slipping. The crystal's effect was bleeding outside. The box was containing the fight but not the reach.

Marisol went still.

The left glass pin in her hair flared — the same cold color from before.

"They need to get back." Not to me. To the air. Then, louder, toward Hicks: "Radio in. Anyone acting strange near the box — they need distance. Now. The crystal's reaching outside."

She knew. House Reader. Of course.

Hicks did not question her. His radio was already up.

I watched the pieces move. Cops pulling back. The ones who had gone strange being guided away. The rest giving the box wider berth.

Through the smoked glass, I checked on the fight.

Riley was still there. Still standing. Still swinging. The crystal was chewing on her sequence and the caster was backfiring and she had not stopped. Her jaw was set. Her feet were planted. She didn't look up. Kept swinging.

Good. Let her hold it. We would hold this.

My left hand stayed on the staff, channeling. The dark threading held. Luna was still beside me — I could feel her through the bond, her magic holding, the familiar note of her working steady but thin. Marisol was still watching the perimeter. Echo was at the glass.

None of us had dropped. None of us were going to.

The fight was still moving inside the box.

I could see the shapes through the smoked glass — Riley swinging, the caster responding. The exchanges were too fast and the glass too dark for fine detail. I tracked what I could. Position. Motion. The dark threading in the staff told me the seal was holding. That was enough.

The twin-bond pulled tight. Luna's tension came through sharp and focused, the way it did when she was watching something she could not look away from. The Dawnbreak light flickered at the edge of the framework. She had seen something inside the box. I did not know what. The bond gave me the shift — the tightening — but not the visual.

Then I saw it.

Riley's blade finished its arc. Through the smoked glass I saw the shape of what happened next — the head coming away from the shoulders, the body still upright a half-second too long. The Fracture light did not leave with the steel.

It stayed.

In the wound. In the air where the neck had been and then was not. A slash of wrong-light hanging suspended — white bleeding into red bleeding into something that was not a color I had a name for. The dark threading in the Eclipsing Sigil jumped once, hard, before I steadied it. The wrong-light was wrong in a way that registered through the spell framework — a note that did not belong in the chord the sigil was holding.

Through the twin-bond, her gold threading through my dark tightened like a fist closing around something fragile. Then held. She was still there. I was still there. We were both still there.

The head rolled.

The tilt of the road carried it — three feet, four — toward the glass wall. Toward where Echo was pressed against the pane. It struck the glass with a dull impact I felt through the spell framework more than I heard — a soft wet thud that traveled into my hand on the staff. A smear of blood appeared on the inside of the pane, dark against the smoked surface.

The body folded. Knees first. Chest. The rest of him. The Fracture light in the wound stayed standing a beat after he was down — a slash of wrong-color in the shape of a neck that was not there anymore, suspended in air like the memory of violence refusing to leave the room. Then it guttered. Faded. Left nothing but the body and the head and the blood pooling slowly on the floor of the box.

I breathed. One breath. That was all I could afford. The Eclipsing Sigil was still flickering at the edges — thin, barely there — and my left hand was locked on the staff and the dark threading was stretched to the point where I could feel the cost in my body the way I felt the cost in Luna's. I was near passing out. Had been for a while now. The only thing keeping me upright was the seal itself.

Riley was still standing.

Sword in both hands. I could see her knuckles through the smoked glass — pale, locked, the grip of someone whose hands did not remember how to let go. The Fracture wrong-light was still crawling under her skin. I could see it through the smoked glass — the faint crawl of not-right under the surface of her arms, bright enough to register even from my distance. She was looking at her own hands like they belonged to someone else. Checking and rechecking — like the answer wouldn't stay.

She had done it. She had finished it. She was still standing.

Her hands opened.

One finger at a time. Slow. Mechanical. Like she was operating a body she did not have the manual for. The sword dropped — and did not fall. Gold-green shimmer wrapped around the blade, violet to teal to rose cycling slow across the steel. The sword hung suspended at Riley's side. Safe. Waiting.

She reached for the hilt with her left hand. The hand I had watched her check and recheck since the crystal seated. Her fingers found the white shape with the red edge-lines. She pulled it free.

The wrong-light under her skin did not vanish. I could see that much — the crawl did not stop, but it stopped climbing. Settled at her shoulders. Waiting.

She moved her hand toward the safety case. The motion was clumsy and slow — her hand wobbling, the distance between her palm and the case shifting like she could not tell where the opening was. The bismuth shimmer widened around the case. The lid angled toward her approach. Her fingers found the gap. The crystal slipped inside. The red lines dimmed against the white as the case closed. The latch sealed.

Riley's knees gave.

Not a decision. Not something she chose. Her body reached a limit it had been ignoring and the limit decided it was done being ignored. She went down — not hard, not dramatic. Just her legs not being legs anymore and the floor of the box rising to meet her. The gold-green shimmer was already there — wrapping around her shoulders, her back, her hips. Not gripping. Not restraining. Just present. The shimmer caught her descent and eased it — momentum absorbed in increments, the fall slowed until it was not a fall anymore.

She settled on her side against the box floor. The sword lowered beside her — plain steel now, resting within reach. The safety case stayed sealed. She was still breathing.
