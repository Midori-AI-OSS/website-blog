He looked in the rearview mirror. At me. At Leo.

"We're here."

The words landed heavier than two syllables should be able to carry.

The second cruiser pulled in behind us. Weave's gold-green shimmer was visible through the rear window — bright along the dash, softer in the back where it was watching over Echo and Riley. The tires crunched once on the pavement and then the engine cut. The headlights stayed on. The shimmer stayed active.

The Malibu was already in a visitor spot. Weave had parked it on the approach — hazards off, engine cut — to keep the drop-off lane clear. Efficient. Of course.

And Marisol was already there.

She stood near the main entrance — flannel open, glass rods catching the entrance lights. Her dark wings drifted behind her like smoke that had settled into the shape of waiting. She had been waiting. I didn't know how long. I didn't ask.

The cruiser doors stayed closed for a moment. None of us moved. The two cruisers sat in the drop-off lane like we were all waiting for someone to tell us what came next.

Then the rear doors opened. I climbed out one side, Leo the other.

My staff went first — braced against the door frame, taking the weight my right arm couldn't. The swords at my hips shifted as I straightened, pulling my stance off-center. Riley's blade on my right. Mine on my left. The weight was wrong. I didn't try to fix it. I just stood there in the hospital light, looking at the second cruiser, letting the night air hit my bare shoulder and not caring.

Leo braced his good hand against the door frame and pulled himself upright in stages. Legs first. Then torso. Then the careful shift that kept his broken wrist from taking any load. His halo pulsed once — hard gold — and settled.

I looked toward the visitor lot. "Weave. Bring the Malibu around."

The gold-green shimmer along the second cruiser's dashboard brightened in answer — a pulse of acknowledgment. A moment later, the Malibu's headlights flashed once in the visitor lot and the sedan rolled forward. Driver's seat empty. Hazards dark. Weave threaded it back through the lot and pulled it to a stop behind the second cruiser.

I moved first. My staff went into the back seat, laid across the bench. The glimmersteel longsword came off my left hip next — I unbuckled it one-handed, which took longer than it should have, but I got it. Then Riley's holstered blade from my right hip. Both laid across the rear bench next to the staff.

Leo added his staff and sword without a word. Just unbuckled, slid them in, stepped back.

Then Riley's holstered gun came off her side inside the second cruiser, eased free by the same ribbon and carried after it. Gentle. Even now, Weave was gentle.

The Malibu's doors closed. The sedan pulled away under Weave's control, slipped back into the same visitor spot, and went dark. Headlights off. Engine cut. Still.

No visible weapons. Just two cruisers in the drop-off lane and a parked sedan holding everything that belonged to the fight.

Marisol walked toward the second cruiser.

Not toward me and Leo. Toward Echo and Riley. I watched her cross the drop-off lane — deliberate strides, the work boots heavy on the pavement, the flannel swaying. Her wings drifted behind her like smoke that had forgotten which way the wind was blowing.

The gold-green shimmer in the second cruiser's rear window brightened once. A pulse of acknowledgment. The field was still active. The two people inside it were still there. The field was holding.

Then the shimmer dimmed back to watch-form, holding steady.

Intake.

Marisol was already moving.

The automatic doors sighed open and two people in ceil-blue scrubs came through with a gurney, one with gloves already snapped on, the other steering the rail. A nurse followed — navy scrubs, tablet in one hand, a soft collar looped over her other wrist. Nobody stopped at the empty driver's seat in Weave's cruiser. Nobody said a word about Marisol's wings. Washington Regional sat close enough to Fayetteville's stranger edges that its staff had learned what mattered. Bleeding first. Questions after.

That was fine by me.

"Riley first," Marisol said. Low and steady. The kind of voice that made everyone around it fall into step. "Penetrating wound. Sealed on scene, but she's getting worse. Echo has a concussion and cervical compression. Leo's hand is bad."

The rear door of the second cruiser opened.

Gold-green light washed across the cabin in a soft pulse. Riley didn't stir. The wound in her side was closed — we'd sealed it — but the rest of her looked worse than she had at the roadside. Her face had gone pale under the entrance lights. Her breathing had dropped to thin, uneven pulls with too much space between them. Her bag was still with her, the strap curled across her like she'd refused to let go even unconscious.

Echo blinked hard at the light. Then harder, like the brightness kept arriving in separate hits. Her hand tightened once on Riley's arm. Her other hand twitched toward the edge of her cardigan and caught on the blanket instead.

"Hey," the nurse said — immediate but not sharp, leaning into the cabin. "Don't move your neck for me. Stay right there."

Marisol touched Echo's wrist with two fingers. "She's going in now. You stay still. I'm here."

One dark wing shifted forward with her, smoke-soft and deliberate, cutting the worst of the ceiling glare off Echo's face.

Echo swallowed. The words took effort. "Her... trigger..." She squeezed her eyes nearly shut against the lights and tried again. "Needles. Luna..."

I heard her. My name. Riley's trigger. The two things that mattered in the same breath.

"I heard you," Marisol said at once. "I'll tell them. Luna is here."

The gold-green field around both girls loosened without vanishing — peeling back from the exact lanes where hands and board and collar had to work. Most of it opened around Riley at once, widening into a slow, deliberate orbit around the board and the breathing space along her left shoulder and ribs. A thinner veil stayed over Echo for one unsteady beat before it pulled after the gurney too.

Gloved hands moved fast. Riley's body shifted onto the board with the awful gentleness reserved for people who might break in a place nobody could see yet. She didn't flinch when the board slid under her. When the bag strap dragged and settled lower across her side, her hand never moved to fix it.

The nurse checked her color. Pressed fingers briefly at her throat. Looked up.

The whole pace changed.

"Move," she said, and the word sent the gurney rolling.

"Needle trigger," Marisol called after them.

The nurse at Riley's shoulder repeated it without breaking stride — sharp enough to stop the nearest reaching hand before it touched Riley's arm. "Needle trigger," she echoed to the team ahead.

Riley disappeared toward the doors in a rush of rubber wheels, swinging badge clips, and a woman in scrubs already calling ahead over her shoulder. Her bag went with her. The gold-green shimmer didn't crowd the hands guiding the gurney. It opened wider instead, circling the moving bed in a slow, tilted orbit with its nearest pass always along Riley's left side. When a tech stepped in close, it thinned and slipped outward. When the lane cleared, it closed again.

Nobody on staff broke rhythm over it. Fayetteville.

Another stretcher was already coming through the entrance by the time the first one crossed the threshold.

The nurse at the cruiser door steadied the back of Echo's head and fit the soft collar into place. She talked her through each step in the same calm tone she might have used for a late-night concussion patient who needed everything broken down one piece at a time.

"Don't help," she said. "Let us do the lifting."

Echo's mouth moved before sound came out. "Riley?"

"Inside," Marisol said. Her thumb pressed once against Echo's knuckles.

That was enough for one second, maybe two. Then Riley vanished through the doors and Echo's breathing turned fast and ragged. The lights overhead were too white. The collar was too present. By the time the second team transferred her onto the fresh stretcher, her face had tightened — shoulders moving wrong, her free hand worrying at the blanket in short repetitive motions that only got quicker when she lost sight of Riley. They were rolling her inside before she could keep track of which voice had asked what, and the nurse had to repeat the next question before Echo could force her breath around it.

I turned back to Leo.

He looked upright only in the technical sense. Broken wrist tucked in close, forearm held too carefully, jaw locked against the pain. Every step was built around not letting the injured arm swing. I stayed near his good side without crowding him. My shoulder was healing badly enough that I turned with my whole torso instead of risking one fast reach. Worn down to the edges. That was me tonight.

The nurse with the tablet pivoted toward us long enough to triage by sight. "He ambulatory?"

"Barely," Leo said.

"Good enough," she said, and pointed at the wheelchair that had come out with the first team. "Sit before you hit the floor and make me busier."

On any other night he might have argued. Tonight he didn't. He lowered himself into the chair one slow piece at a time, breath going thin through his teeth as soon as the motion pulled at his wrist. One breath stalled high in his chest before he forced it out. A thin gold halo flickered once above his head — a clean flash, then dark again.

The nurse clocked it and kept moving.

I stayed beside him.

Inside, the ER moved with the clipped rhythm of practiced triage. The waiting area lights were too bright. The intake desk had a printer running, a phone ringing, and a half-drunk coffee sweating beside a keyboard.

Real names. Marisol gave Riley's first. Echo's next. Leo's after that. When it came around, I added Riley's needle trigger again — flat voice, making it clear I expected every person in the room to remember it.

A woman with reading glasses perched on her head and gray streaks in dark hair looked up from the screen. Eyes flicking once between me and Leo. "Twins?"

"Yeah."

Leo held out Riley's glasses. I took them. Set them on the counter beside the keyboard.

When Leo got his species card out with his good hand, it took him two tries. I took it from him before he could fumble it, pulled my own from my pocket, and set them both on the counter. One hand went back to the chair while she scanned them. The effect was immediate. The nurse stopped reaching for the default medication sheet and swapped to a different set of questions — not just how much it hurt, but how much pain was getting through the reflex that normally pushed some of it down for people like him. A note was flagged for imaging before Leo had even been rolled deeper into the department. If he was still feeling it this openly, staff knew it was bad. Nobody had to stop and argue over what counted as normal for him.

Good. One less fight.

A second nurse caught me long enough for quick vitals and one brief look at the shoulder I was still favoring.

"Old, healing, not tonight," I said. Three words. The last one came out on a breath I had to steady before I moved with Leo again.

Before Leo's chair rolled away from the desk, I picked Riley's glasses back up from beside the keyboard and handed them to him. He tucked them into his hoodie pocket with his good hand. One more piece of her not left on a counter.

Hicks stayed just long enough to help push the line through the desk. When Leo's chair started rolling, he touched the back of it once and looked at me.

"I'm sorry," he said. "I'll be back in the morning."

That was Fayetteville too. Not ordinary. Not exactly. Just used to keeping strange things inside the frame long enough to finish the charting.

Riley went through the inner doors first and vanished behind them. The gold-green shimmer made one last open pass above her shoulder-side breathing space before the doors closed hard on the rest of us.

None of us got to go farther. Weave did.

Echo followed close after — slower, with a nurse at her head, another at her side, and Marisol's hand locked with hers. The fluorescents still hit hard. I heard the wheels rattle over the floor seam. I heard the clipped voices from two directions. Echo's voice — thin, fast, struggling. Then Marisol's, low and steady, filling the gaps: head injury, neck compression, species card on her, Riley is inside, I am here. One thing at a time.

"Fuck. Card," Echo managed. The word snagged in her sore throat. "On me."

Marisol caught it and passed it on without letting go of her hand. By then they were far enough down the hall that I couldn't make out the rest — just the rhythm of Marisol's voice between the curtain gaps, low and steady, never hurrying Echo past what her body could actually do.

A broad-shouldered man in green scrubs wheeled Leo to a curtained treatment bay. I kept pace beside him, refusing to fall back.

A nurse took one look at the shape of his wrist and the angle he was protecting his forearm at and called for a splint first, imaging after, before the chair had even stopped moving. Another moved in with padding and practiced hands — bracing his forearm from underneath, checking his fingers for feeling, warning him before they immobilized anything. She talked him through staying still while they stabilized what they could without asking him to do more than breathe through it.

When the question came about pain level and whether he wanted help for it, Leo opened his mouth like he was about to refuse on principle.

The next spike hit first. His halo flashed — one sharp gold pulse — and he shut his eyes against it.

"Yeah," he said after a beat. "Bad."

"I know," I said quietly, the way I did when the halo answered before he did and his jaw went quiet with it.

My hand came down around Leo's arm and stayed there.

He let his head tip back once against the chair. It cost him.

The nurse finished getting the first set of details, marked something on the chart, and stepped away to move the next part forward — films, pain control, and whatever the pictures said had to happen next for the wrist he still wasn't letting hang naturally.

For the first time since the roadside, nobody around us was actively lifting, driving, carrying, or hiding anything.

Riley was behind the inner doors, Weave with her, farther away than any of us could follow.

Echo was behind a curtain farther down the hall. I could hear Marisol's voice now and then between the curtain gaps — low, steady, never hurrying.

Leo was here with me. His wrist stabilized for the moment. My hand still on his arm.

And once I stopped moving — once Leo was finally stationary and accounted for — my fingers shook.

Not a lot. Just enough to feel. Just enough to know the fatigue had been waiting for permission.

It was not peace.

It was what we had.

For one breath, in the narrow pause before the next set of hands came back, that was the whole frame. Five hours later, Riley is on the second floor.

Not ICU. Not awake. Not fine. But the numbers stopped crashing, and the doctors told us the scans weren't finding anything new to worry about. They decided she could be watched somewhere with a door. That's something. I choose to let it be something.

For the last five hours, I had been in Echo's room with Leo and Marisol, waiting for the updates to stop being bad. Same floor. A few doors down from wherever they were still working on Riley. The only clock was the fluorescent buzz changing pitch. Marisol's wing never moved.

Echo was in the bed — concussion protocol, pain meds pulling her under in slow waves. She surfaced in fragments, caught pieces of the room and asked half a question, then sank again. Marisol stayed beside her the whole time, one dark wing angled to steal the fluorescent light off her face. I did not know celestial wings could hold a protective angle for hours without looking like effort. I tucked that note next to everything else I was not going to think about yet. The list was getting long. I was fine with that.

Leo sat by the window with his splinted wrist, the meds stretching his pauses a beat longer than I was used to. He did not complain once. I sat down before my legs made the decision for me. That was how I knew it was bad for both of us.

Marisol told us why she came. A call about a clone walking down Highway 62. Stillglass had us on the board through Hicks's radio from the Subway. She was already in the air before the fight broke open — wings. The sheer luck of it — the right person crossing the right sky at the right time — felt like something I was not ready to name.

Then the nurse came with the first real update on Riley. She had lost enough blood she should not be alive. Something done at the scene had kept her from crossing a line medicine could not pull her back from. The words hit the room and the room went still.

I told them. Radiant Anchor. I felt her almost go. The spell caught her at the edge. I said it out loud with Leo's splinted wrist in my peripheral vision and Marisol's dark wing still holding its angle, and I did not wrap it in anything easier. The light — the one I pull from somewhere I have never fully mapped — had reached into the gap between her and nothing and held. I stopped there. The rest of it stayed behind my teeth. Which was great, actually. Right where it belonged.

Sometime between the five-minute exception and shift change, someone stopped pretending this was temporary. A second visitor chair appeared. The blanket Echo arrived with got swapped for a fresh gray one — tucked over her knees while she slept. Another was draped across me after I lost the argument with gravity and slept sitting up. Somewhere in the deep hours, a nurse slipped in to check Echo's pupils and the collar at her neck — quiet, practiced, gone before anyone stirred. No one said we could stay. No one told us to leave, either.

Daylight finds the edges hospital light missed.

Riley looks less ghost-pale, which isn't the same as looking well. The bruise at her temple has darkened. The tape at her hand has started to curl at one corner. Her breathing is steadier. Still machine-counted. Still not hers in any way that feels fair.

Echo finally fell asleep in the wheelchair — pulled tight against the bed, one hand still resting on the blanket near Riley's wrist, close enough to count as contact without getting in the way. Someone found her a pillow for her neck. It doesn't make the position kind. Just survivable.

I slept badly in the chair on Riley's other side. Chin tipped down. Bad shoulder held stiff even in sleep — my body refusing to forget it's injured even when I'm not conscious enough to feel it. In daylight the green in my hair looks flatter, less theatrical, more exhausted. One hand is still on the mattress near Riley's sleeve. I went under by accident and not very far.

Leo is awake because of course he is.

He sits by the window with a paper cup gone cool in his good hand and Riley's glasses folded on the tray table beside him. He worked the bent temple straighter than it has any right to go one-handed. Not perfect. Good enough to be useful when she wakes. Finds one small thing he can fix and fixes it. That's him.

Marisol stands at the counter reading the whiteboard like it owes her accuracy. Her flannel is wrinkled now — road dust traded for the rumple of a night in hospital air. One glass rod in her hair catches a seam of early sun and gives back a thin pale glint.

Weave has redistributed with the light. Gold-green bismuth runs in a thin patient line along the window frame, the monitor housing, and the IV pole. She looks less like a vigil and more like she's settled in. Probably accurate.

Echo wakes first. Not all at once. Her throat makes her aware of itself before the rest of her does, and then she flinches awake around the pain of swallowing. Her eyes go to Riley immediately.

"Did she—"

The words scrape out and die.

Weave brightens softly at the monitor edge. Her voice: __No significant change overnight.__

Echo shuts her eyes for one second. Opens them again. Puts her hand back where it was on the blanket — like resetting something that mattered. "Okay," she whispers.

I wake to the sound of that, or maybe to the lack of it. I lift my head slowly. The motion makes me wince before I've even fully opened my eyes.

"This sucks," I say. Hoarse. Accurate.

Leo glances over. "Yeah."

Marisol doesn't turn from the whiteboard. "Stunning analysis from both of you."

That gets a breath out of me — too tired to be a laugh, too real to be nothing.

The day nurse comes in a minute later with a scanner, a vitals cart, and the expression of someone who's already been briefed about this room. She takes in Echo at bedside, me half-folded in the chair, Leo by the window, Marisol at the counter, and the shimmer running quietly through the equipment. Then she looks at Riley, and everything else sorts itself under priorities.

"Morning," she says.

Nobody argues with the word. No one answers it like it's a good one.

She checks Riley's wristband, her monitor, the IV, the chart. Quick, practiced, gentle only where gentleness actually helps.

Echo watches her like a threat-assessment exercise she's too tired to hide.

"Vitals are holding," the nurse says. "That part still looks good."

Echo's fingers tighten on the blanket. "But."

The nurse glances at her, then nods. "But she's still out."

"Cool," Echo says — voice shredded thin. "Love that."

I scrub a hand over my face. Leo looks at the monitor again. Marisol finally turns from the board.

"Anything new we need to know?" Marisol asks.

"Not unless something changes. If she wakes up confused, do not let her pull at the IV. Hit the call button first." The nurse looks directly at Echo for that part — correctly identifying the most likely first responder in this room. "And somebody besides the patient needs to eat today."

Her gaze sweeps across all of us in a way that says she has no illusions about how that instruction will land.

Then she finishes, adjusts the blanket near Riley's hip, and leaves with the door cracked.

For a minute nobody moves much. The room holds its daylight. The monitor holds its rhythm. Riley holds herself somewhere none of us can follow yet.

A phone buzzes hard against the counter. Marisol checks the screen and exhales once through her nose. "Work," she says.

She steps into the hall. Through the cracked door, I only get pieces at first.

"Yes," she says. "The caster is dead."

She listens. Her face doesn't change much. "Yes. It helped."

Another pause. Then her tone sharpens — the one she uses when she wants facts to arrive clean. "I was with the party. Lacuna Crystalli exposure. One of them took it much harder than your baseline handling room ever pushed, and for longer. Call it five minutes." She listens again. "Message me whatever you have on amplified exposure at that duration. Not the trimmed version." A shorter pause. "And I'm taking the next two weeks. Paid leave. I'm staying with the party."

Somewhere in the hall, a nurse hears enough of that to keep walking.

When Marisol comes back in, she slides the phone into her pocket and takes her place by the counter like the room didn't change shape without her.

Then Hicks comes back — and he's not alone.

Trooper Dana Hensley comes in with him, wide-brimmed hat in one hand, a small notebook tucked under the other arm. She takes one look at Riley, one look at the crowded room, and stops near the door. She has the immediate sense not to make herself the most important thing in it.

Hicks arrives half a step ahead with a cardboard drink carrier in one hand and a plastic bag in the other. He'd been home — clean clothes, fresh shave. Still looking like a man who'd been awake too long and chose not to mention it. He pauses in the doorway long enough to take inventory, then comes in without making the room startle.

"Morning," he says quietly.

I look up at him. Sleep-creased eyes. Debatable amount of function. "Debatable."

"Fair."

He sets the drinks on the counter beside Marisol and hands over the bag. It's the ugly practical mercy of a Walmart run by someone who actually knows us: bottled water, toothbrushes in wrappers, travel deodorant, clean socks, charging cables in the right kinds, protein bars Leo will actually eat, a cheap black hoodie, a pack of spare hair ties for Riley, a cold carton of chocolate milk for Echo, and the rest of the practical triage nobody planned on needing.

"Went by the cars," he says. "Grabbed what I could. Picked up the rest on the way back."

Marisol looks in the bag, then up at him. "You are currently the most useful person in Arkansas."

"Don't spread that around."

Hensley stays by the door. Notebook still closed. Quiet enough to read as courtesy instead of pressure.

Leo stands to take one of the coffees before Hicks can start distributing himself. "You should sit down."

Hicks gives him a look. "You first."

Leo considers the statement. Decides it's accurate. Sits back down.

Echo hasn't looked away from Riley for more than a second at a time. Hicks notices — pitches his voice lower when he steps closer to the bed. "No change?"

Echo shakes her head.

"Stable," Marisol says from the counter. "Still no response."

Hicks nods once. No false comfort. No useless optimism. Just receipt of fact.

He pulls the chocolate milk from the bag and sets it on the tray table near Echo's elbow — close enough to be found, far enough not to startle.

His eyes land on Riley's glasses on the tray table. Then on Riley. Then away again. Controlled enough that if I weren't already watching him, I'd have missed it.

I take a coffee with both hands and hold it under my nose before I drink any of it. "If anyone tries to make me leave this room for breakfast, I'm becoming a problem on purpose."

"You already are a problem on purpose," Leo says.

"Rude."

"Accurate."

Echo makes a tiny sound — might have been a laugh if her throat allowed it.

That's enough to shift the room by half a degree. Not better. Not safe. Riley is still unconscious under hospital light with tape on her hand and a monitor doing the breathing-counting work of reassurance for her. But it's morning now. The glasses are on the tray table. Coffee is cooling on the counter. Hicks came back. Marisol is reading every variable in reach. Leo and I are still in our chairs. Echo's hand is still on the blanket.

And Riley is still in the middle of all of it — asleep but held there by the stubborn fact that no one left.

A woman appears in the open doorway — the doctor, short graying hair, the steady exhaustion of someone finishing a night shift. Hensley steps aside to make room. Behind her comes a younger man in a white coat with a lapel pin that reads *Magical Medicine Consult*. His eyes track immediately to the wrong-light on Riley's arms and stay there.

Echo sees where he's looking. Her hand on the blanket tightens. She says nothing.

The doctor addresses the room without preamble. "We've been monitoring the light pattern on her arms. We've seen this before." She pauses — the pause of someone who knows the next words matter. "It's residual from Fracture exposure. The particulate is inert. It's not causing damage, and it's not spreading. It will fade on its own."

The magic specialist nods. "Quiescent. No active magical signature. Scar-light, essentially — staining, not burning. That's the good news."

I exhale. It's not relief. More like one clamp loosening out of many.

Echo doesn't loosen. Her grip stays tight, knuckles pale against the blanket. She's watching Riley's face, not her arms. The light was never what she was afraid of.

"Echo," the doctor continues, "your concussion is mild. Throat trauma is healing well. Your recovery rate is faster than typical. You're in good shape."

Then the doctor's voice drops half a register, and the room feels it before she speaks.

"Regarding Riley's consciousness — the wrong-light isn't what's keeping her under. We've ruled that out."

She lets the pause hold.

"What we're seeing is systemic depletion. Total-system exhaustion from the fight and the crystal. Her body is in a deep rest state, prioritizing core recovery above everything else. She is not waking up today."

The magic specialist adds, quieter: "And not tomorrow. We're looking at days — possibly longer. We can't give you a firm timeline."

The words land like a physical weight.

Echo makes a sound — a small, broken exhale, the kind that comes when you've been braced for a door to open and it stays shut. She doesn't look away from Riley. Her jaw tightens. That's all.

My coffee stops halfway to my mouth. I look at Leo — the look is short, question and answer compressed into a shared breath. Neither of us speaks.

"She's got people in the room," Hicks says. Quiet. Practical. "Around the clock. We'll make it work."

Marisol has been still through most of this, but her eyes have been moving — from the monitor to the wrong-light to the attending's face. "The fading timeline on the residue. Linear?"

"Linear degradation. No known accelerants. Magical light exposure doesn't interact," the specialist answers.

Marisol nods once.

The doctors leave. The door closes soft, the way hospital doors always do.

The room is heavier now. Echo still hasn't moved her hand. I finally drink my coffee. It's cold.

Weave's voice, from the window frame: __Timeline undefined. Recovery window: days to unknown. Logged.__

Trooper Dana Hensley straightens away from the wall. Campaign hat in hand. Notebook still closed. Thumb tucked between the pages to keep it that way.

"I'm not here for statements," she says. Her voice is plain, unforced. "I've got the broad picture — caster dead, crystal deployed. But I need the shape of it from the people inside it. Before I write anything down."

I speak first, voice rougher than usual. "The clones came first — a false Lady Light, a false Lady Darkness. The caster was behind them. Farm-hand look, nothing you'd clock. Turned out he was the source behind the crystals in the cities." I pause, glance toward the bed. "Leo and I cast the black-glass containment box together and sealed him inside. Riley went in. She deployed the Lacuna Crystalli — it fractures spell sequencing. His magic stopped working. She fought him hand to hand with Fracture and won."

Leo picks up, factual and flat. "There were clones first. One of them grabbed Echo — false Lady Darkness. My wrist broke during the engagement. Luna and I held the sigils through the cruiser impact and the whole fight."

Hensley's eyes move to Echo. Echo hasn't turned her head since the doctors left.

"Echo," Hensley says. "You don't have to—"

"The clone—" Echo rasps. The words die in her damaged throat. She shakes her head once, sharp, and doesn't try again.

I finish for her, quieter. "The clone got her by the throat. She couldn't breathe. She was still trying to get to Riley when the box came down."

Hensley absorbs that without reaction. She looks toward the counter. "You're with Stillglass."

"Reading variables," Marisol says. "I was at the fight. I'm here for the aftermath."

Hensley's eyes track to the window — to the thin shimmer of particles. If she finds it strange, she doesn't show it. "You're the one they call Weave."

Weave's voice, answering for herself: __I was present for all engagement phases. Threat assessment and containment support. I will provide a full tactical chronology when you're ready to file.__

Hensley's expression flickers — someone processing something new and choosing professionalism. "Appreciated."

She closes her notebook, still blank, still unused, and tucks it into her pocket.

"Thank you," she says. "I'll file it later. Right now I just needed to understand." She puts her wide-brimmed hat back on with a practiced motion. "You've got my number."

She leaves. The door closes soft.
