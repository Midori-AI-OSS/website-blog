Echo hit the glass.

The impact traveled through the spell framework and into my hand on the staff before I could place it. Not a knock. A body throwing itself at a wall that would not open. Her palms slammed the pane. Her fists followed. Then her forehead — hard, desperate. Her mouth opened around Riley's name and the sound that came out was wrecked — her voice used up past the point of screaming.

"Let me in—" The words tore out of her wrecked throat. "Let me in— the box— the box is still up— Riley is in there and she is down and I need— I need to—"

Her fists kept pounding. Her forehead pressed harder against the glass. The blood smear from the caster's head was inches from her face and she did not seem to see it. Her body was done — her knees buckling, the glass the only thing holding her up — and she kept pushing anyway. Marisol's arm tightened under her shoulder. Her dark wings folded in closer around Echo — bracing, shielding, not constraining. Marisol's lips were moving. Private words. For Echo or for Riley or for both. I could not hear them.

One of the cops said something behind me. I did not catch the words. His partner was staring at my head. I kept my eyes on the box. A third voice — younger, state trooper — cut through clearer: "Jesus Christ. Look at them. Look at their heads. They're glowing."

A rougher voice answered. Lower. Older. "Both of them. Gold rings. Right above their — what the hell is that."

I did not turn. Did not answer. My right wrist was broken and my left hand was on the staff and the dark threading was getting thinner by the second. The seal was still up. The box was still standing. But the fight was over. Riley was down. The crystal was sealed. The caster was dead. And Echo was pounding on the glass with everything she had left and the glass was not moving and Riley was on the floor inside and I was on this side holding a seal that was keeping her out.

Through our bond, I felt the Dawnbreak Sigil go.

Not slowly. Not carefully. Her gold light pulled back from the framework — the flat disc of the barrier dissolving into nothing. I saw it from the corner of my eye — her light guttering once, brightening for half a breath, and then gone. The box came down. The black-glass walls rippled once — a distortion in the air, a shiver of wrong-light at the edges — and vanished. The road was a road again. The bodies and the blood and the sword and Riley were not inside a box anymore. They were just there. On the asphalt. In the open air.

The Eclipsing Sigil went with it.

I did not choose to drop it. The sigils were linked — her Dawnbreak and my Eclipsing, two halves of the same seal — and when her light went my dark followed. The shadow threading pulled back into the staff and then into my body, fast and cold and final, and the absence of the working hit me harder than the cruiser had.

My legs gave.

No decision. No choice. The sigil was gone and my body had been borrowing from reserves I did not have and the debt came due all at once. I dropped the rest of the way — not controlled, not catching myself. My staff hit the dirt beside me. My right arm folded useless against my chest and my left hand lost the channeling and the dark threading scattered into nothing. The dirt came up hard against my back.

I stared at the sky. The stars were still there — exactly where they had been before the headlights and the box and the blood. My lungs burned. The pain in my wrist was a dull throb I had been sitting on since the cruiser impact and now there was no sigil to focus through and the pain was just pain. The magic had pulled back into my body and gone quiet — no dark threading, no seal, no working I needed to hold. Just me on the ground with a broken wrist and empty reserves and the cold of the ground seeping through my jacket.

Through our bond, Luna's presence flickered once. Thin. Tired. Still there. Then her left arm slid under my shoulders — I felt her shift beside me, her weight settling into the dirt on my right, her breath close and warm. She pulled me a few inches toward her, enough to brace my back against her side. We were not far apart before. Now we were not apart at all. I turned my head. The stars were still there — cold and distant — and the trees stood dark at the edge of the road, still. She was on the ground beside me. I was on my back. We were both on the ground. And we were still here. Still breathing. Still together.

I did not try to get up.

Somewhere in the blur, I heard Echo's voice — raw and scraped, the sound of someone who had been at the glass the whole time and was now where the glass was no longer. Marisol's voice was saying something I could not track. Hicks was already barking orders. The night was full of motion and noise and the aftermath of the thing we had just survived.

I closed my eyes. The ground was cold beneath my back and Luna was still there on the other side of the bond and her arm was still under my shoulders and Echo was with Riley.

I kept them closed for a while. Not sleeping. Not even close. Just not looking at anything for a few minutes while my body reminded me of everything it was currently failing at.

The ground stayed cold. The stars were still there — I did not need to see them to know. Luna's arm stayed under my shoulders — I could feel her breathing, shallow and steady. The bond gave me nothing but the quiet hum of her presence. Too drained for more.

The crickets had come back. I had not noticed when. Sometime after the box came down and the fight noise stopped. They were filling the quiet — steady, mindless.

Cop radios crackled somewhere in the dark. Distant voices. An engine idling — low, steady, the sound of a vehicle that was not going anywhere. Hicks was somewhere down the road. I could pick out his voice in the noise — the rhythm of it, not the words. Coordinating. He was always coordinating.

My right wrist throbbed. Not sharp anymore — the pain had settled into something deeper, duller, the kind of hurt that lived in the bone and stayed there. Every time my pulse pushed through it the bone shifted. I had been holding it loose against my chest since I went down. I had not moved it since.

Luna shifted beside me. Small motion. Her arm tightened under my shoulders — not much, just enough to adjust her own position. The weight of her against my side was warm. I opened my eyes.

She was looking at me. I could see her face in the dark — gray with exhaustion, the way faces went when the body had spent everything and had not gotten around to replacing it. But her eyes were open. Still here.

"You still with me?"

A long pause. My voice was not where I had left it. I found it in the back of my throat — hoarse, thin, the kind of sound a body makes when it has used up all its air on impact.

"Unfortunately."

She laughed. It came out wrong — more breath than sound. Relief, mostly. I knew the sound. The church. Every time we made it through something we should not have made it through.

"Gross." She stared at the sky. I watched her watch it. "Riley did it."

"I saw."

"She's—" She stopped. I waited. "She's alive. I know she's alive."

Our bond told me nothing about Riley. It did not need to. I had seen Riley through the smoked glass — down but breathing, the crystal sealed, the sword beside her on the box floor. Echo had just run past us toward her. Echo did not run like that unless there was someone to run to.

"She is." My voice was steadier now. Still wrecked. Still thin. "Luna. She is."

She believed me. I could tell by the way her shoulders dropped — a fraction of an inch. She let her head fall back against the dirt and I watched the tension leave her face.

"The sheer audacity," she said.

I made a sound that might have been a laugh. "Of what."

"All of it. The clones. The box. The caster slow-clapping down the road like he was walking into his own third act. The fact that we are still alive and lying in the dirt on an Arkansas highway at—" A pause. "—whatever o'clock this is."

"The audacity of Arkansas."

"Don't." She was still looking at the stars. "Don't make me laugh. I am not in a laughing mood."

"Noted."

The crickets were still going. The engine was still idling. Hicks was shouting something — I recognized the rhythm.

She shifted her left arm under my shoulders. Not much. Just enough to feel me there.

"Your arm," she said.

I did not answer.

"Leo. Your arm."

"Broken." Flat. The way I said things when they were true and I did not want to talk about them. "Wrist. The clone. Before the crater."

She closed her eyes. Opened them again. "I saw her grab you. I saw you go down."

"I got up."

"You always get up. That is not the point."

I turned my head toward her. The motion pulled at the exhaustion in my bones. I ignored it harder. Her face was close — close enough to see the exhaustion in the lines around her eyes, the way her jaw was set against the pain in her shoulder. But she was still here. Still arguing with me in the dirt on a dark road. Still Luna.

"The point," I said, "is that you are also lying in the dirt with a shoulder that has not worked since the church."

"Fair."

"Luna."

"I know." She did not know what she was agreeing to. Neither did I. The whole thing, probably. That we were both wrecked. That we had both spent everything. That lying in the dirt was the best we could do right now and that was going to have to be enough. "I know."

We stopped talking. The night was filling back in around us — the crickets, the radios, the low idle of the engine somewhere down the road. Footsteps. Distant voices. People doing the slow work of aftermath. I did not have the strength to track it all.

"You should not have held the sigil that long," I said.

"Neither should you."

"Different."

"It is not different." She turned her head toward me. The motion pulled at her shoulder — I could see it in the way her jaw tightened. "You were in a crater. Your wrist was broken. And you got up and you held the sigil through the cruiser impact and the whole fight and you are going to tell me I should not have held mine?"

I did not answer.

"Yeah," she said. "That is what I thought."

"Don't say I'm being dramatic."

"I was not going to. I was going to say you are a hypocrite."

A beat of silence. Then, very quietly: "You are also a hypocrite."

"Yes, I know. I should have left."

"No." I made my voice firm. Not loud. Just firm. "You should not have."

She did not answer. The stars stayed where they were. The crickets kept at it. She was still there — her weight against my side, her breath shallow but steady, her heart stubborn and still beating.

She lifted her head. I did not see how much it cost her because I was looking at the stars and she was beside me and I was tracking the motion from the corner of my eye. But I heard the catch in her breath. Heard the small sound of muscle protesting. She was looking down the road. Toward the place where the box had been.

"She's okay," she said. "Riley. She's in Echo's lap. Against a cop car. She's okay."

I didn't ask how the cop car got there. Didn't have it in me. She didn't either.

I let out a breath. Felt something lift. "Good."

"Yeah."

The crickets were still out there. The night was still out there. The breathing took more work, but we were managing it.

Footsteps.

Not distant anymore. Close. Boots on dirt — two sets. The heavier boots moved with the weight of someone who had been on their feet all night and was not done yet. Hicks. The lighter boots were quicker, more careful. Marisol.

I heard her voice first. Low. Steady. "They are here. Both down."

Hicks said something I could not catch. The tone was calm and capable and utterly unflappable. The man had just watched a black-glass box seal a stretch of highway and he sounded like he was coordinating a traffic stop.

Marisol knelt beside us. I heard the rustle of her coat against the dirt. Her face came into my field of vision — sharp features, dark hair, the glass rods in her hair glowing low lavender.

Her hand found Luna's shoulder. The good one. "Luna. Can you move?"

"Define move."

The corner of Marisol's mouth twitched. "Fair."

I shifted. Not much. Just my good arm adjusting, my weight resettling against the dirt. The broken wrist stayed cradled against my chest. I had not moved it since I went down and I was not about to start now.

Hicks stepped into position on my other side. His boots first — practical, scuffed from the road and the night and everything that had happened on it. Then his hand. Extended toward me. Toward the uninjured side.

"Come on," he said. One word. Steady. No pity. No urgency. He knew what he was doing.

I looked at the hand. Then at Luna.

"I'm fine," she said. It was not true. "Go."

She pushed herself up. Not fast. Not clean. I watched her do it — left hand planted against the dirt, her body following in increments. Shoulder. Hip. The slow negotiation of muscle and gravity. Her right shoulder looked wrong — I had been tracking it all night and I could read the cost in the way it stayed tucked, the way it pulled at her neck and her ribs and everything connected. Marisol's hand stayed on her left shoulder. Not lifting. Just there. In her other hand, Marisol held Luna's staff — the silver quarterstaff Echo had dropped earlier. She pressed it into Luna's left hand as Luna steadied.

She got her knees under her. Then one foot. Then the other. The world tilted for her — I could see it in the pause, the way she stopped and breathed through something I could not feel. But the ground was still there when she steadied. She was standing. Barely. But standing.

I took Hicks's hand.

My weight shifted. The broken arm moved — a small motion, a pull through the wrist that should not have mattered but did. The bone shifted. The pain spiked hard — not sharp, deeper than sharp, the kind of hurt that lives in the place where the break was. My jaw locked. I rode through it.

Hicks's grip was steady. He took the weight on my good side — my left, the arm that still worked — and he did not rush. I rose in increments. One knee. Then the other. Then the long pull toward vertical. The broken wrist stayed cradled against my chest. The body protested every inch of it. I did not listen.

Hicks held my arm. Not tight. Just steady. The way you hold something that is not quite sure it wants to stay vertical.

"You good?" he asked.

I did not answer. I looked at Luna.

She was standing a few feet away — Marisol's hand still hovering near her shoulder. Her face was gray. Her right arm was tucked the way it had been all night. But she was up. Still standing. Still here.

The bond gave me nothing but the quiet hum of her presence. Her eyes were open.

I looked back at Hicks. I did not say anything.

Luna answered for both of us. "Yeah. We're good."

It was not true. It didn't need to be.

Riley's glasses were missing.

I had seen them come off. Inside the box — the caster's fist connected with the side of her head. The glasses went one way. She went the other. But the box dissolved. The reference point was gone. Somewhere on the road now. In the dark. In the debris.

The cruiser's headlights threw white down the road. I scanned what I could see. My spellbook rested open at my side.

The asphalt was rough with containment debris — glass from the cruiser that had hit the box, scattered gravel, the crater I had been at the bottom of. Blood smears. Boot prints. The caster's body down the road, head separate. The wrong-light still standing in the neck wound. I did not look at it long. I was looking for a glint. Small lenses. Wire frames. The kind of thing that caught light wrong when you were not aiming at it.

Hicks swept his flashlight left. I followed the beam.

Marisol's voice drifted over from the direction of the cruiser. Low. Steady. She was near Echo — her light spell sweeping the asphalt, cooler than the headlights. Echo was still on the ground with Riley across her lap. The gold-green shimmer was wrapped around both of them.

"Sweep left." Hicks's voice, calm. "Got something over here."

I looked.

The glint came back at me — two small circles of reflected white against the dark asphalt. Near where the box had dissolved. Half-buried in containment dust. Intact.

I walked toward them. Short distance. Not far. The broken wrist shifted in its cradle against my chest. The pain was deep and spreading and wrong in a way that meant the break was worse than a simple snap. I rode through it.

My left hand closed around the frames. I straightened. Held them up. The headlights caught the lenses.

The lenses were intact. The frames were intact. But the glass was whole. The arms were attached. They would rest on Riley's face and do what they were supposed to do when she woke up.

"Got it. Here."

"Good." Marisol's voice, from somewhere near Echo. "Bring them."

I folded the glasses carefully — lenses inward, the bent temple tucked against the frame. Left them in my good hand. Riley would need them when she woke up.

The road was scattered with debris, the tree line dark beyond it. Stars overhead. Crickets had come back — steady, mindless. The air was cooler than it had been inside the box.

Luna moved toward the gold-green shimmer. Her voice was low. "I've got it. Release."

__Releasing.__

Weave's voice in the back of my mind. She was releasing something from her field. The sword. Riley's. Luna was handling it.

Luna was kneeling beside Riley. She had the sword — Riley's glimmersteel longsword. Weave's field had released it. Luna unclipped the holster from Riley's side — careful, right shoulder limited but both arms working. She clipped the holster and sheathed sword onto her own belt. She did not look up.

Echo moved. Barely. Her voice was wrecked. "...the case. Her bag."

__Sealing. Case secured.__

The safety case. Back in Riley's bag. The crystal contained. Another thing I did not have to track.

Riley was in Echo's lap against the cruiser door. Unconscious. Breathing — I could see the rise and fall of her chest. The through-and-through wound was sealed, but her shirt was dark with blood front and back. The wrong-light moved under the skin of her arms — white bleeding into red. Stopped at her shoulders. Not advancing. Not receded.

The cops near her were giving it a wide berth. I did not blame them.

Echo looked changed. The clone residue had done something to her. Her face was sharper, her hands longer. I stayed quiet.

Marisol was beside them. One hand steady on Echo's back. Echo was barely holding herself up. But her hands stayed on Riley.
