The sound comes first.

I don't know that's what it is. Not yet. Just—something. Something wrong and loud and then the world tilts and there's white at the edges of everything, white eating the corners of my vision, and I feel the blood before I understand what it is.

Warm. Wrong-warm. Running down.

Riley's hands are on the wheel. The car lurches. She's trying to drift us out of the line of fire and I can see her knuckles white and her jaw set and I want to tell her I'm okay but I'm not okay and my mouth won't make the words.

The pain is a bright line across my skull.

Everything is too loud. The shot. The tires. My own breathing, ragged and wrong in my chest. I try to reach for my staff and my hands are slow, so slow, like they belong to someone else.

We're behind a house now. Riley pulled us into the apartment complex, got us behind cover, and I'm conscious—barely—and my head hurts and I can't make my thoughts line up right.

She picks me up.

I hate that she has to pick me up.

I can feel her arms under me, her breathing hard, and we're moving under fire and I'm being carried again. Always carried. Always the one who needs saving. Luna gets the door and we're inside and Riley tears cloth from her outfit and hands it to Luna for my wound and her gun is up and she's—

She keeps moving between me and the door.

Every time the pressure shifts outside, her stance changes. She's putting herself in the line because she won't let me be hit again. I watch her do it. I watch her body move like it's nothing, like it's obvious, like the answer to *someone is shooting at us* is *put Echo behind me*.

And I can't stop her.

Something is out there.

Not a person. I can feel it before I see it. Ghostly. Purple-violet. Pressing against the walls like it's tasting them. Something hungry and wrong and stronger than an elemental. The air tastes static and spoiled.

Luna casts Sending. Her voice stays calm but I can see the tension in her shoulders. *"Leo. South of SHBC. House by the apartments. Echo is hurt. Bound violet entity. Come armed."*

Leo comes through a magic door.

Sword already out. Spellbook visible. Staff on his back. My ribs loosen when he steps through. I don't realize until then how hard I've been holding my breath.

The entity hits the room a second later.

Riley keeps herself between it and me. It hits her again and again and I watch her body jolt each time, but she doesn't move aside. She doesn't let it reach me. The light round does nothing. She swaps to the darkness clip and I see the moment she realizes it.

Then her legs give out.

She collapses onto me—her weight sudden and heavy across my thighs—and I feel her hands shaking against mine as she tries to steady the gun. Can't. Her grip fails. The darkness clip is loaded but she can't hold the aim and the weapon slips from her fingers, clatters to the floor beside my hip.

The gun is right there. Close enough to reach if I don't think about how much it hurts to move.

My hand is shaking when I grab it. Everything is shaking. I don't know if I can hit anything. I don't know if I can help. The gun is heavier than I remember—or maybe my hands are weaker, maybe the blood loss is making everything feel wrong, maybe I should just lie down and stop pretending I can be useful.

But Riley is still here. Still alive. Still putting herself between me and everything that wants to hurt me. And that matters more than my hands being steady.

I fire.

Darkness round. Clean hit. The gun kicks in my hand and the round connects with the thing's center. Just ammo. Just physics. No magic. No cost.

The thing weakens. Something in my chest loosens—just a little.

Then Leo kills it.

Sword through the core. The violet presence collapses in on itself and the air clears.

Everything goes black.

Not sudden. Slow. My head goes limp first, then the feelings drain out of me like water through a broken cup. The noise of the room fades. The weight of my own body disappears. The last thing I register is cold—cold and dark and then nothing.

Later they tell me I was out for hours.

The blackout feels like bad stitching in the day. I'm bleeding and then I'm not, the thing is there and then it's dead, and there is nothing in between that I can reach. Just dark. Just gone.

I wake at Riley's house.

No. Wait. This isn't my house. This isn't my room. My staff is in my hand and I don't remember reaching for it. My clothes feel wrong—too loose in some places, too tight in others. My face doesn't sit right on my skull. My hands look different. Smaller. The feeling is immediate and overwhelming: something is off. Something has changed.

"Where is James...?"

The words come out before I know I'm speaking.

Fear moves faster than thought. My body responds before I can stop it. `Ice Knife` into the ceiling—the staff doing what it knows how to do when I'm afraid. Then cold rain starts falling. Ice fragments. When I look up, there's a hole in the ceiling, and I hate that I cast without choosing. I hate that fear has a shape in my hands. I hate that my body knows violence before my mind knows where I am.

The cost settles.

Younger. Again.

*Fuck... again... James is going to get so... mad...*

I feel it in my bones first. A settling. A compression. Like someone turned a dial I can't reach and took something from me. Another piece gone somewhere I can't follow. The body collects the bill eventually. The arithmetic keeps without me asking it to.

Leo and Luna are here.

It takes me a second to place the room. Riley is in her bed. I'm in my cot in Riley's room. The door is open to the living room, and Luna and Leo had been trying to sleep on the couch nearby. They're both up now. Leo's hands are on my shoulders—holding me still, grounding me. I hadn't realized I was shaking.

The ice is still falling. Cold rain melting on my face. My head throbs where the bullet went through—still healing, still tender. I can feel the pulse of it under my palm when I reach up to touch.

Luna cleans it with Prestidigitation. Music playing. Soft. Her hands are gentle and she doesn't ask questions about the ice or the name I called or the staff still locked in my grip. She just fixes the room like it's normal. Like I'm normal. Like waking up afraid and putting a hole in the ceiling is something that happens and then gets cleaned up.

Leo's grip stays firm on my shoulders. Not restraining—just present. Reminding me where I am. Reminding me I'm safe.

They know me. They've always known me. That's why they're here—that's why they stayed.

The weight of the day lives in my muscles. Every breath costs something.

Then the doorbell rings and I jump. The sound cuts through the quiet and my heart spikes before I can stop it. Old reflex. Bad reflex.

Deputy Mercer. Officer Hicks. Questions about the house fight. Their words come fast and I can feel the noise of them pressing against my skull. Too much input. Too much to process. But I tell them what I saw. What I did. What it felt like when the darkness round connected.

Riley is too hurt to carry the full statement. I watch her try and fail to sit up properly. She asks if the kid from the house is okay. It was Nick. Even hurting, even shot full of holes, she thinks about the person who wasn't part of the fight.

That's who she is.

That's who I'm trying to keep alive.

Night settles. Leo goes home to sleep. Luna stays beside Riley. I get a glass of milk and drink it slow. The cold helps.

Sleep comes in pieces.

Morning comes. Riley already wants to go back. I look at her and see the determination in her face—the refusal to let her body decide what happens next. She's still injured. She should rest.

But I know that look.

I know her.

Her phone floods with messages.

BPD. Pastor. Twenty-seven missed messages. Her voice is tight when she says it: "We need to get to the church..."

I can feel the urgency rolling off her in waves.

We drive to SHBC.

Fifteen police cars with lights on. The lights hurt my eyes. Too much visual noise. Everything is too bright and too loud and too much.

Riley cracks the back door. There's someone inside. A Luna-shaped figure. Light swords drawn.

"Duck," Riley says.

Three normal rounds hit the clone.

The body responds like clay. Not flesh. Not a person. I watch it take the bullets and keep moving—and I understand. This is something wearing a person's shape. Something that looks like Luna but isn't.

But there's no relief. Officers are already shouting that more copies are still active. Leo-shaped. Echo-shaped. Riley-shaped. And the Riley-shaped one has a gun—the same gun Riley carries, copied along with everything else.

A shot rings out. The Riley-clone fired. An officer goes down.

Riley moves fast. "Sword—please—"

An officer hands her a blade. Loaned. Heavy. Real.

She goes for the Luna-clone first.

The thing wearing Luna's face hesitates. Just for a heartbeat. Not conscience—something else. Pattern leakage. Whatever made it copied Luna's memory along with her form, and that memory includes something about not hurting Riley.

It lets her kill it.

The loaned sword comes down. The Luna-clone falls.

Riley doesn't stop. She pivots—same blade, same motion—to the thing wearing her own face.

Her copy doesn't hesitate the same way. It fights back. But Riley is faster. Angrier. The sword swings once, twice, and the Riley-clone goes down.

Two down. Same sword. Same hands.

Riley's breathing hard. She's hurt—still hurt from the house, from everything—but she's standing. The sword drips something that isn't quite blood.

My stomach drops.

An Echo-shaped thing.

Something that looks like me.

The mirror matches begin.

I fight something with my own face.

It moves like me. Holds its staff like me. The same grip. The same stance. The same angle of shoulders I've seen in mirrors and photographs and Riley's careful glances.

But the eyes are wrong.

There's nothing behind them. Just playback. Just teeth. Something that learned my shape from somewhere—some memory, some echo I didn't consent to—and is wearing it like a costume.

The first swing comes fast.

Too fast. I know this angle. I know this reach. It's my reach—it knows how long my arms are, how I pivot, how I overextend when I'm scared. Every movement it makes is something I've done. Every opening it shows is a trap I've set.

And I can see all my own openings in the way it moves.

The worst part isn't that it's stronger than me. The worst part is recognizing myself in it. My grip. My feints. The way I shift my weight before I commit. It keeps reading me because it's built out of the same pattern.

We circle.

My staff feels wrong in my hands. Too familiar. Every little shift in my stance feels visible.

Then it pivots—turning toward Riley, like it's going to strike her while she's down.

No.

I change the pattern. Drop the staff. Pull my dagger.

Something I wouldn't do. Something it wouldn't expect.

The blade catches its neck mid-turn. The copy staggers. I twist. Pull.

The head comes off.

Clay and memory and wrong-wrong-wrong dissolving into something that doesn't hold together, doesn't bleed right, doesn't die like a person should because it wasn't a person. It was a thing. A shell. Something that looked at me from behind my own eyes.

I killed something that looked like me. I watched my own face come apart.

Hollow.

That's what I feel. Hollow.

I turn.

Luna and Leo are fighting the Leo-copy together. Twin sword work—synchronized, practiced, the kind of movement that comes from a lifetime of moving in parallel. They don't need to speak. They just know.

Leo keeps the copy busy while Luna charges her beam. Staff up. Light building. The same gold glow I've seen a hundred times.

The beam burns through the final copy.

Then both twins crash from the backlash. Halos gone. Eyes dim. They hit the floor hard, and I know that kind of drop. Too much power all at once. The body pays for it. They look spent—all of them, drained down to the dregs.

Four clones down. The church goes quiet for a heartbeat.

Then W.E.A.V.E. arrives.

Bismuth dust rises outside the church windows, catching the light. Gold-green particles pulling together into readable form. I recognize the pattern immediately.

*Help is here.*

Her voice in my mind is flat and steady. *"I came to help when I was informed."*

The steadiness helps. Something in me unclenches.

Then the second wave walks out.

Pastor clone. Healed Riley clone.

Two more. Mocking.

Riley gets up with W.E.A.V.E.'s help. The bismuth particles form around her like a second skeleton—holding her weight, keeping her upright. She's still injured. Still shouldn't be standing.

But she's Riley. She stands anyway.

"Sword—please—"

She can't stand on her own and she's still asking for a weapon. An officer hands her a blade—different from the one she dropped after the first wave.

The pastor clone starts building a death spell.

I can feel the air turn wrong. The book glows. The spell is aimed at all of us—me, Riley, Luna.

Riley cuts its arms off. Left arm. Right arm. The spellbook hits the ground with both hands still clamped around it.

She grabs the spellbook.

She's limping when she says it: "WEAVE go to... the others... keep this party... safe."

Then she blacks out against the cop car. The sword drops from her hand.

I lunge and still don't catch her in time.

The healed Riley clone looks at me with my friend's face. And it's whole. Perfect. No bullet holes. No blackouts. No cost. Like nothing happened. Like the fight didn't matter.

That's the horror.

Not that it wears her shape. But that it wears her shape *healed*.

Like the real Riley—the one who protected me, who took the hits, who keeps putting herself in front of every threat—like her wounds are the wrong part. Like the cost of loving someone is something that can be erased.

I want to destroy it.

I want to destroy it so badly my hands shake.

The healed Riley-copy breaks toward Luna.

No—

The light saber is in its hands. A copy of the copy—wrong-gold, wrong-cold—and it's moving fast and I see the blade pass through Luna's shoulder. A clean arc. Luna staggers but doesn't fall. Blood on her shirt. The wrong-gold light sizzling.

Impact.

Something hits my face. Hard. My head snaps sideways and there's blood in my mouth and I didn't see where it came from but I feel it. The pastor clone. Its arms are back—the stumps filled in while I was watching the wrong thing—and it's still moving, whole again, and I don't understand because Riley cut them off clean and they should have stayed gone, but they're back and everything is happening at once.

Luna casts Slow on the healed Riley clone.

She's been holding this since the house. Since the drive-by, since the car, since every cast and every hit and every time she stayed level and kept moving through it. The shoulder wound is just the last weight on a stack that's been building all day. I can see it in the way she stands—everything at once now, nowhere left to hold it.

Then the church fills with photon blades. I stop trying to count. The light is blinding—blue where Luna's light should be gold, cold where it should be warm. Each blade that forms takes something from her. I can see it from where I'm standing. The color draining from her face. The trembling. The way her stance collapses inward like she's trying to hold herself together with nothing left to hold with.

When the light clears, both remaining clones are down. Shredded. Ended.

Luna collapses.

I've seen her tired before. I've seen her drained. This is different. Her halo is gone—not flickering, just absent. Her skin is gray. Her eyes are dim, like someone turned off the light behind them. She looks scooped out. Hollow. Like something essential got spent and didn't come back.

Leo catches her. Heals what he can. But the blood loss is real, and you can't heal what someone chose to pour out.

I watch her halo flicker once—weak, wrong—then die completely.

The officers search the church.

They find the crystal and smash it with a hammer.

The sound is plain and ugly. Just metal on crystal until the thing breaks. That is the last violence of the night.

W.E.A.V.E. drove Riley's Bolt and Leo's car to COX Health before the ambulances left—party still at the church, preparing for transport.

Ambulance convoy to COX Health ER.

Officer Hicks leads with lights on.

I ride with Riley. Leo and W.E.A.V.E. ride with Luna. The ambulance is loud and small—the siren cuts through everything, the road bumps jolt through the gurney, the paramedic's voice is too close and too fast. I keep my hand near Riley's arm without touching. Just close enough to feel the warmth. Just close enough to know she's still breathing.

The fluorescent light inside is harsh. Everything is harsh. The smell of antiseptic and old sweat and something metallic underneath. I count her breaths. One. Two. Three. Don't stop.

Intake.

The nurses reach for lines and needles—smooth motion, practiced hands, reaching for Riley's arm to start the IV.

I remember.

Riley has a needle trigger.

I know this about her because I've watched her flinch. Because I've seen the way her breath catches when something sharp comes near. Because she told me once, quiet, like a confession she didn't want to make. Because I know her.

The needles are coming for her arm and she can't consent and she can't move away and she's unconscious and the nurses are reaching and I have maybe two seconds to stop something that will hurt her even though she won't know it's happening—

"Wait. Needles are hard for her."

My voice comes out faster than I want it to. Sharp at the edges. The nurses pause. Look at me. Look at Riley. Look at me again.

"She has a needle trigger. Please—can you warn her when she wakes up? Can you go slow?"

The staff adjust. They change how they move. Slower. More verbal. Explaining each step to Riley's unconscious body, just in case she can hear.

Riley is unconscious and I'm still protecting her edges.

I hand her off as blood loss. Repeat blackout. Spine caution. No seizure activity. I have to be exact so the room hears her body first. So they know what to fix. So there's no confusion.

The words come out clinical. Detached. The part of me that knows how to triage doesn't shake. It just speaks.

Riley and Luna moved into the same room once stable.

The room expands. More chairs. The walls are pale green and the light through the window is gray-morning and I can hear machines beeping in rhythm. Riley's breath. Luna's breath. The soft hiss of something pressurized.

I sit.

Watch.

Wait.

The chair is uncomfortable. I don't care. I'm not here for the chair.

Leo doesn't move from his chair by the window. He was already in it when we arrived and he'll still be in it hours from now—watching, present, doing what he does when people he loves are hurt. I don't say anything about it. Neither does he.

W.E.A.V.E. asks for an empty box. "Reduced presence," she says. "Not gone. Just quiet until you need me."

Bismuth dust falls into the box.

The room gets quieter.

I watch the dust settle. W.E.A.V.E. doesn't breathe. Doesn't bleed. She is just there. I don't know if that would feel easier or if it would feel like losing something important.

Leo notices my face healed.

The head wound from the house—gone. The blood that was there, the bright line across my skull, the wrong-warm running down—it's not there anymore. My skin is smooth.

"Your face..." Leo says. Then, quieter: "That doesn't mean it was free."

He gives a small nod.

He understands. The cost doesn't show on the outside. The cut closed because I age faster. The skin is smooth again, but the rest of me still feels wrong—deep in my bones, in my teeth, in the tightness under my ribs. I feel a little off in my own body.

I don't know where the edge is. I don't know what happens if I keep spending from a place that doesn't refill. I try not to sit with that too long.

I watch Riley sleep.

The monitors keep time for her a little. The IV drips something pale and slow. Her face is relaxed in a way it never is when she's awake—no determination, no stubborn set to her jaw, no protective stance. Just rest.

I don't know if she can hear me.

I don't know if I should talk.

I stay close anyway. Hand near her arm. Not touching—she might flinch—but close. Present. A shape she'll see when she opens her eyes, if she opens her eyes, when she opens her eyes.

She will open her eyes.

She has to.

Luna surfaces in fragments.

Halo flickers. Combat reflexes.

"die fuckers."

"Do not touch her."

She's still in the church. Still fighting.

I tell her: "There is nothing here to kill."

My voice is soft. I don't want to startle her back into violence.

Leo grounds her. "No one is touching her. Riley is safe."

His voice is the anchor she needs.

She surfaces more than once before she's really back. Each time a little more present, a little less church. Somewhere in there she crosses the line from surfacing to awake. I watch it happen. I don't have words for the exact moment.

Seven in the morning. Riley's phone rings.

Grandma Gustin.

I answer. "Hello, this is Riley's phone but this is Echo."

She asks to come. I say yes.

Family should be near.

Hicks comes back with an Arkansas State officer.

Big gray-tan hat. He fills the doorway for a second before the briefing starts.

I look at the box on the table—the quiet pile of bismuth dust, still and dark.

I reach over and tap the cardboard. Gentle. Just enough motion to shift the particles.

"Wake up," I say quietly. "There's something you need to hear."

W.E.A.V.E. wakes.

The particles rise, condense, reform into readable shape. Gold-green light returns. She asks to take readable form. We give her constraints—no looming, no sudden movement, away from the beds. She shifts into something the room can parse. Not human, but present.

She asks for four more chairs.

We make space. More chairs. More people who don't want to be far from the beds.

Leo remembers: the first Luna-faced clone let Riley kill it.

A hesitation.

Not conscience. Pattern leakage. Something carrying Luna's memory but not her choice.

Luna surfaces. Barely.

"I would never hurt Riley."

Her voice is raw. Hoarse. Like she's been screaming in her sleep and maybe she has. The clone may have hesitated because it carried her memory. But memory isn't choice.

Memory isn't love.

She needs to say it. She needs me to hear that the thing wearing her face wasn't her. Even though we both already know.

Crystal incidents showed up overnight across the U.S. and in parts of England. Coordinated. Targeted. Somebody powerful behind them.

The scale of it lands in my chest like another hit.

This wasn't just our fight.

It was happening in other places while we were still trying to keep blood inside the people in front of us.

Hicks apologizes for letting Riley go.

I say: "You didn't let Riley become Riley."

He didn't make her stubborn. He didn't make her brave. Those things were already true. She was going to fight no matter what—he just got to watch it happen.

I explain the Berryville crystal.

"It made copies of persons. Clay-wrong. Memory-linked. They talked like playback with teeth."

I try to make him understand without making it sound impossible.

It was impossible.

It still happened.

The wider attack starts to come into focus.

I force the last two days into order so I can say them out loud.

Investigation. Drive-by. House fight. Blackout. Waking at Riley's house. Back to the church. Copy fight. More copies. Crystal destroyed. Convoy. Hospital. Briefing.

Saying it as a list makes it sound cleaner than it felt.

The words still don't get close to what it felt like. Seeing my own face come at me still sits wrong in my stomach. So does watching someone I love take hits meant for me.

I don't know if this had a beginning we missed or if I'm only looking for one because I want the night to make sense.

Maybe there were earlier signs and we brushed past them. Maybe this was the first clear one. I can't tell from here.

I think about the days before this. The ordinary days. The days when nothing happened and we thought that meant we were safe.

We weren't safe. We were just early.

When the shape of what happened settles into the room—when the briefing finds its edges—W.E.A.V.E. asks for an empty box.

Not shutdown. Reduced presence. A way to be here without being present to everything at once.

The bismuth dust falls.

The room gets quieter.

Then the door opens.

Grandma Gustin.

She fills the doorway the way some people do when they've been needed before—solid, unhurried, like she knew this was coming before the phone rang. The room adjusts around her. There's no confusion about why she's here. She looks at the beds. Then at me.

"She's stable," I say.

She nods. That's enough for now.

She wakes around quarter to ten.

Her eyes opened but she wasn't all there—still caught in something. Black void and jelly, she'd say later. Like her brain was rebooting wrong.

Gold-green light flickered near her. W.E.A.V.E. Layer 3 contact—not talking, just anchoring. I watched Riley's breathing slow, something settle in her that had been scrambled.

Her hand twitched toward the IV line—instinct, trigger response—and I caught her fingers before she could grab it. Held on. She didn't fight me. Just... waited.

Riley owes me seven years.

She said so before the fight. *"I think I can repay them today hopefully..."*

She was joking. Kind of. A way of naming the thing between us without making it heavy. A way of saying *I know what you gave up for me* without having to say the whole truth out loud.

But I didn't spend those years so she could pay me back by getting shot somewhere else.

The debt isn't a ledger. It's not something I'm counting. It's not something I want back.

It's love.

I gave those years because I wanted her alive. Not so she could spend herself proving something back to me.

She keeps stepping in front of the hit. Bullets. Claws. Whatever is there. Like my body is the one that has to come first. Like bleeding for me balances anything.

That's not—

That's not what I meant.

Every cast costs me.

I can feel the change after it happens. My body sits a little wrong inside itself, like something in me got pushed backward and I don't have good words for it.

I don't know where the edge is.

What happens when there's nothing left to give? Do I just—stop? Wake up feeling even less like the person who started this?

I don't know.

I try not to think about it.

But I would pay it again.

For them.

For her.

Every time.

Without asking. Without counting. Without stopping to measure whether the cost is worth it, because of course it's worth it. She's worth it. They're worth it. The years I have are less important than the years they get to have.

I don't know what comes next.

I don't know how many more fights like this we can survive. I don't know if the crystal was the end or the beginning. I don't know if Riley's body will hold together through another choice to protect me at her own cost.

But I know this:

I'll be there.

Whatever it costs.

Even if the arithmetic runs out. Even if I give everything I have left. Even if the last cast takes the last year and I wake up somewhere I don't recognize with a face I don't remember earning.

I'll be there.
