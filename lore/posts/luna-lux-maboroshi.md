The road ahead went white.

Not headlights. Not lightning. Light, pouring from a single point in the middle of Highway 62.

Hicks braked. I felt Leo's hands react on the wheel before the Malibu's nose dipped, and I braced my staff against the footwell with my left hand.

The tires grabbed. The car stopped.

There was a woman in the road.

White hair. Black sparkles. Purple coat over gold pants. Lady Light's silhouette, sharp against the headlight glare — every edge catching the light like cut paper.

For half a second I thought it was her.

Then she took one step and the limp failed.

Left leg smooth. Left arm correcting under the sleeve without thinking. A small motion, a tiny tell, the kind of thing you only catch if you have been watching someone's face for damage long enough to know how damage behaves.

Weave's voice touched the back of my mind, thin and immediate.

__Roadwatch active.__

The message underneath it landed a beat later: not her. The limp was wrong. The body in the road kept forgetting which side was supposed to be broken.

The doors opened.

Leo was out first — driver side, moving toward the cruiser. I pushed my door open slower, staff in my left hand, feet finding pavement, right shoulder staying out of the high range because it still would not lift that far.

Hicks was already out of the cruiser, one hand up, shouting something I could not understand — the cruiser's radio was bleeding and the air had started to hum low and wrong.

The copy lifted one hand. Gold-white particles gathered around her fingers — not body-glow, spell-light, brightening in clean concentric spirals that made the shadows on the road sharpen and stretch behind her.

Then the Bolt got hit.

The sound was wrong. Metal report. Roof impact. Suspension drop. The kind of noise that travels through pavement and into your boots before your brain finishes classifying it.

I turned.

The Bolt's roof had dented. A dark shape was dragging across it toward the front. The shape dropped onto the hood — Lady Darkness's silhouette, wrong in the way the body settled like it had rehearsed the landing.

The windshield cracked under a flat palm. Glass punched inward. A hand went through after it, and I saw Echo jerk forward in the driver's seat — the copy had her by the throat.

Leo was still moving forward. Hicks had his weapon up but the copy's light was already rising, feeding off the attention we could not pull away from both directions at once.

Behind me, the Bolt shook under the motion — the copy's shoulders squaring. I knew what those shoulders meant for the neck beneath them. The angle would be wrong.

I lifted my staff.

Left-handed. Awkward angle. My spellbook pressed against my hip as I shifted — no time for pages, the staff was already in my hand. The shoulder line I had been working around all week was there — healed enough for normal range, but the high lift still would not answer. But the left side worked. The spell came when I called it.

Not a big answer. A quick ward — informal, a buy-time hold — something to keep us breathing while we figured out which emergency to meet first.

Weave's voice shoved into the back of my head and broke apart on the way in.

Front threat. Rear breach. Echo—

Then nothing.

I did not need the rest to know Echo was in it.

A gunshot cracked from the Bolt. Riley's gun.

I looked back. The copy's grip on Echo had broken — Echo's head snapped against the headrest, not sideways, not broken, just released. The copy swung the failed motion into a throw and Echo was off the hood, hitting the pavement in front of the Bolt with a sound I felt through my boots.

I did not have time to process what I was seeing.

The air in front of me started humming.

The fake Lady Light's head turned. Toward Hicks. Toward the cruiser.

Her right hand went up.

Light blades started spinning around it. Thin. Bright. Edge-sharp. She did not throw them. She let them build, gold-white edges catching the dark and throwing it in streamers, faster and faster until the air around her hand began to hum.

She was looking at Hicks.

The blades kept accelerating.

The sound of the gunshot was still ringing in my ears. Riley was engaging. Echo was on the ground. Hicks still had no cover and the blades were still spinning.

I started moving. Not toward one emergency — toward the space between them, hoping I could still reach both.


Then a sound cut through everything.

Not an engine. Not a spell I knew. A tearing sound, like something splitting the air overhead.

The fake Lady Light in the road went still.

Not tactical still. Her face changed. The smile — the one that had been wrong since she appeared — dropped. Something else replaced it.

Fear.

I looked up the road.

Two wings of smoke and darkness cut across the sky toward the Bolt.

They came down on the Bolt's hood.

Lady Darkness's copy was gone — driven off by the impact. Echo was already on the pavement where the copy had thrown her. I heard her scream.

I did not stop.

I brought my staff up, left hand taking the full weight, and cast.

Four `Photon Blades`. Not a quick ward. Not a buy-time answer. Four lines of light cut across the dark toward the fake Lady Light.

She did not have time to turn.

Beside me, Leo was already moving — sword low, shadow wrapping the blade. His thin gold halo flickered into sight with the shadow, then brightened. He did not hesitate.

I held the spell line and let him pass.

The first blade struck road.

Not the clone. The road. My left-hand aim had pulled two inches right, and the clone's dodge was faster than I had budgeted for.

The second caught her sleeve. Particle-light scattered where cloth tore — gold-white motes spiraling out from the impact like startled fireflies. Not body-light. Spell-light. The clone was bleeding illumination, not blood.

The third and fourth she met with a wall.

Her hands came together and a disc of light spun out from the space between them — flat, bright, edged in the same sharp white that had been building around her fingers at the cruiser. My blades hit it and shattered. The sound was glass on glass. The light did not go out. It broke into pieces and hung there, turning slow.

The shoulder line screamed.

Not the wound. The compensation. My left arm had been holding the spell line open and the four-blade cast had cost more than I let myself tally. The muscles along my ribs pulled tight. My neck burned. The staff in my left hand was still channeling and I could feel every strand of the ward vibrating back through the wood into my palm.

Leo passed under my spell line and kept going. Sword low. Shadow wrapping the blade so dark it drank the light off the road.

Then I heard it.

The tearing sound. Same as before. Not engine. Not spell I knew. Something splitting the air overhead — that same high wrong seam I had heard seconds ago when the fake Lady Light's face had changed and the wings of smoke had come down on the Bolt.

I did not look up.

I could not. The clone had recovered from the blade-strike and her attention had sharpened past survival into something else. Her left hand came up trailing gold-white particles that gathered and narrowed. The air between us started humming again — higher now. Meaner.

Weave was still trying. Numbers kept breaking apart before I could use them.

Something was going up behind me. Fast. Higher, still higher, and whatever came back down was going to land where Echo was.

My stomach dropped.

Behind me, a wet impact.

Not metal. Not stone. Something hitting the road with a sound my brain refused to classify on the first pass. Weight. Wrongness. I felt it through my boots before the noise finished traveling.

Then I felt it on my skin.

Cold first. Then wrong. The residue hit the back of my neck like a thrown liquid — thick, dark, crawling with that smoke-texture that belonged to shadow magic. It soaked through the collar of my coat. It was in my hair. On my jaw. Under my sleeve where the cuff had ridden up during the cast.

The air changed.

Not the road air. The residue air. It smelled like the bar — like the ice that was not ice, like the bass in the floorboards the night everything crossed. Like the quiet in Stillglass House where the crystals sat under glass domes and the red lines tried to take your sequence apart one beat at a time. Like the cold stone air of the church where I had burned myself hollow.

My stomach dropped. My left hand shook against the staff. The spell line wavered — I felt the containment field try to oblong, pulled it back into shape through the disorientation with something closer to willpower than skill.

The clone in front of me screamed.

Not fury. Not pain. Rage. A sound that hit the air like it was already the first attack — not a warning, not a threat, just the opening move.

Her face had already changed once tonight — the wrong smile dropping into fear when the first tearing sound had come. Now the fear was gone. What was left was worse. The copy stopped fighting like something tactical and started fighting like something that had stopped caring whether it survived the road.

Gold-white particles erupted from both hands. Not spirals. Not discs. A storm of light-edges spinning outward in all directions.

Leo was in the middle of it.

Weave's voice pushed into my head — or tried to.

__Echo—__

Barely a syllable.

__Front—__

Static.

__Leo—posit—__

__—holding—__

Wrong-noise. A burst of noise where words should have been. Then the shape of silence after. Not the clean cutoff from before. Worse. The feeling of a voice reaching for you and disintegrating mid-reach.

The burst hit physically — pressure behind both eyes, a spike of noise that made my left hand twitch on the staff. I flinched but the spell line held.

I did not know why. I only knew the line in my head had gone dead again and the one in my staff was slipping.

Then something landed beside me.

Not behind. Beside. Boots on pavement. A body. A shape my eyes should not have been able to name through the residue-disorientation and the shoulder-burn and the clone-light bleaching the road white — but the face was familiar. I had seen it at Stillglass House. Dark hair. Sharp features. Lean-strong posture. The woman who had caught me when the linked room dropped me and checked my breathing against her wrist.

Marisol.

She did not speak. Her hands came up and shadow poured from them — not at the clone, but across the space Leo was fighting in. Three of the clone's edge-storm blades hit the shadow and stopped cold. Leo cut through the fourth himself — shadow wrapping the sword so dark it swallowed the gold-white impact without a sound.

I pulled the spell line tighter. Left hand burning. Residue crawling at my neck. The wrong air in my lungs. The clone rampaging ahead. Leo fighting beneath the particle-storm. Marisol beside me with her coat and her satchel and one glass rod already glowing lavender at the edge of my vision.

I did not know what she had been doing before she landed.

I did not ask.

I just held the line and let her work.

The residue changed.

Not gradual. One heartbeat it was cold and wrong and crawling at the back of my neck — the next it was fire. Not skin-fire. Deeper. The kind of burn that bypasses the surface and goes straight for whatever inside you holds magic. I felt it catch on mine — hook in and burn.

My left hand locked on the staff. My right arm — the shoulder that would not lift — seized with a pain that had nothing to do with the old wound.

Then it passed.

That fast. The burning portion spent itself. What was left was just the smell and the cold. The bar. The ice that was not ice. Stillglass House. The church. The residue was still there — I could feel it — but it had stopped reaching for my magic.

I had no time to feel relief.

The clone's attention shifted.

She had been focused on Leo — the gold-white storm centered on him, Marisol's shadow catching the edges. But something pulled her gaze past him. Toward the Bolt. Toward the back line.

Echo and Riley.

The clone's eyes locked. The particle-storm did not stop, but the axis of it tilted — the storm's center drifting away from Leo and toward the two people who could not take a direct hit from what she was throwing.

I dropped the ward.

Not a decision. The spell-thread snapped back into the staff. My right hand was already crossing my body to the hilt at my left hip. The glimmersteel came free with a sound like a drawn breath.

Leo saw me move. I saw him shift his weight.

Twins. Practiced. We did not speak.

He went low-left. Shadow-wrapped blade cutting toward her legs from the flank. I went high-right — glimmersteel coming across her shoulder line from the opposite angle. Two vectors. One target. The kind of strike you can only counter by being faster than both directions at once.

She was faster than both.

Her left hand caught my blade — not the edge. The flat. Gold-white particles flared around her palm and the impact stopped dead. My right shoulder screamed from the overextension. The feedback traveled down into my ribs like a stitch popping.

Her right foot came up and caught Leo in the chest. Not a stumble. A kick that put him on the pavement with his sword arm twisted under him and his breath gone.

I pulled my blade back. She was already turning toward the back line.

Leo was down. The storm was drifting. My sword hand was shaking and the right shoulder was finished — done, full stop, no more high work from that side tonight.

I sheathed the glimmersteel. Right hand. Last thing I asked of it.

Then I reached back over my shoulder with my left hand and pulled the silver quarterstaff free. The crystal tip caught the clone's light and threw it back in prismatic fragments.

Leo got to his feet between me and the clone. Not fighting. Standing. He put himself in the gap like a physical wall. No words. Just his body between mine and hers while I switched weapons.

Riley was over Echo — protecting her, covering her body with her own. The clone's light blade was already in motion. Not a thrown edge. A thrust. Gold-white light traveling in a line that ended at Riley.

It hit.

The blade went into her — punched through her body and out the other side. Blood followed in a dark spray, unmistakable against the clone's light. Riley's body jerked — not a recoil, not a fall, a silent lock of muscle as the light passed through. She stayed where she was. Still covering Echo. Even now.

Something locked in me. Not rage. Not fear. Clarity. Crystal-clear. The moment burned itself into place and every other question went quiet.

I swept my staff in a wide arc. Left-handed. The crystal tip traced a line through the air and the spell answered.

*Prismatic Cascade.*

The staff tip flashed. A fan-burst — hundreds of small facets of prismatic light exploding outward from the crystal, catching the clone's gold-light particles in midair. The angle of contact broke them apart in a single instant.

Refractive counter. Not a shield. Her own spell-edges met my prismatic shards and the light sheared against the light — every gold-white particle scattered, thrown back at her in a wash of shattered color.

The clone recoiled. Her offensive broke. Not dead, not even truly hurt, but her momentum reversed. The burst was already gone — flash and done.

My left arm ached from the shoulder wound more than the cast. The cascade was light. The compensation was not.

I did not stop.

The staff tip was still glowing. I traced a circle in the air in front of me. A large one. Wide as a room. Left-handed.

The circle was not perfect. The top arc flattened where my wrist would not roll. The bottom wobbled. The line thickness varied — heavier on the right side where my left hand had more control, thinner on the left where the awkward angle fought me.

Did not matter. The spell takes the shape you give it, not the shape you meant.

*Dawnbreak Sigil.*

Leo's sword came up — his glimmersteel blade lifting toward Riley. Dark warmth was gathering along the steel, shadow tendrils unspooling from the edge, that cool, familiar darkness I had seen him call a hundred times. The clone's hand came up faster. Her fingers snapped closed and the shadow around Leo's blade dissolved.

Counterspell. Clean. He had barely begun the cast and she had already shut it.

I saw his face. I saw the healing fail.

My left hand never stopped tracing. When the ring closed, a flat disc of warm gold light sealed across the space. A ward wide as a room. One-directional. Nothing passes through toward Echo and Riley.

I locked myself, Leo, Marisol, Weave, and the clone inside with us.

Echo and Riley on the other side — protected. Clone still here. Leo beside me. Marisol beside me on the arena floor — dark wings folded back behind her, smoke-like and still. Her shadow kept working the edges of the space, catching light-particles before they could reform. Weave's particles brightening inside the arena — gold-green iridescence sharpening from haze to full shimmer, and this time her voice came with it.

__Containment field detected.__

__Full operational capacity restored.__

__I am combat ready.__

Arena set.

Something touched my right shoulder.

Not skin. Not cloth. Cool at first, then warming — a weight settling against the joint like a hand that knew exactly where the hurt was. I looked down and found iridescent particles wrapping the shoulder in a close lattice, gold-green bleeding to violet at the edges where the Dawnbreak light caught them.

The ache unspooled.

Not gone. I could still feel the old wound underneath. But the weight the shoulder had been carrying — the constant pull, the compensation burning through my ribs — lifted. The brace took it. Held it. My right arm hung easier than it had all night.

Then the particles spread outward.

A ring of shimmer snapped into place around me — dense at the edges, hollow at center, circling at arm's reach like a second skin made of light. Shield geometry. I knew the spell-language even if I had never seen Weave build one.

Her voice was calm, precise — the same careful steadiness she had been using all night.

__Luna — shield active.__

I did not thank her. There was not time. The clone was still here and the light-blade was still up and my staff was still the only thing holding the sigil.

But my shoulder was not screaming. And the orbit around me was bright and waiting.

I tightened my left hand on the staff.

My right arm hung at my side — and for the first time in hours, it did not hurt.

The clone's head turned.

Not toward the back line — the sigil was in the way. Toward Leo.

He was already moving. Sword coming up, shadow wrapping the blade in that dense dark that drank light. But the clone was faster than both of us had been all night, and this time she was not throwing storms.

She was throwing herself.

Her right hand closed on his sword arm at the wrist. Gold-white particles flared around the grip and I saw the angle of his forearm change in a way forearms are not supposed to change. The sword spun out of his hand and hit the pavement three feet away.

Leo did not have time to register the break. The clone's other hand was already at his throat.

She lifted him.

Not magic. Physical. One hand around his neck, the other fisting the front of his coat, and she hauled him off his feet like weight that did not matter. Leo's boots left the ground. His hands grabbed at her wrist on instinct — the shadow at his fingers guttered and went out. His halo followed — the thin gold ring above his head flickering once, then dark.

Then she brought him down.

Not a throw. A slam. She drove him into the road surface with her whole body behind the motion, and the impact split the pavement in a spider-crack crater that threw dust and gravel across the arena floor.

Leo did not get up.

He was at the center of the crater, one arm bent wrong beneath him, his sword somewhere in the dark beyond. Not moving. His shadow — the dark that always wrapped him — had gone still. His halo was dark too, the gold ring vanished. Not gone. Still. The wrong kind of still. The kind that meant his body had stopped fighting and his magic had stopped answering.

Then his halo pulsed — just once, a weak flicker of gold that was barely there and then gone. I held onto that flicker. The light was still in him.

I saw it. All of it.

The break. The lift. The slam. The crater. My brother in the bottom of it, not moving.

Something in my chest that had been holding since the church came unmoored.

I did not wait for the unmoored thing to settle.

The clone was still standing. Leo was still in the crater. The distance between them was not enough.

My left hand stayed on the staff — the sigil could not drop — but light answered before I called it. Not a named spell. Just pressure. The same raw push I used when something I loved was on the line and the difference between casting and not casting stopped mattering.

Light shot from the crystal tip. Not blades. Bolts. Fast. Angry. The clone's head snapped toward me and her own light came up to meet mine — a particle-wall flaring between us in a sheet of bright static.

She was still fast. But she was not the only thing in the arena.

Marisol came in from the clone's blind side. No signal. No words. Just dark wings opening and shadow pouring ahead of her — a wave of smoke-thick darkness that hit the clone's flank and wrapped her left arm from shoulder to elbow. The clone's balance broke. Her particle-wall flickered.

The clone planted her feet. Her right arm came free of the shadow-grip with a sound like torn cloth and she threw a backhand of raw light at Marisol — not aimed, not tactical, just force. Marisol's wings beat once and she slid sideways through the air, the light passing under her.

I pushed again. Another bolt. Another. Driving the clone's attention back to me, giving Marisol space to reposition, giving the crater another foot of distance between the clone and my brother.

She resisted. Every time. But we were moving her.

But moving her was not enough.

Leo was still in the crater. Still not moving. And the clone was still here — fighting, resisting, refusing to end.

Something in me that the unmoored thing had been holding back shut off.

Not rage. Rage was clean. Rage had edges. This was something older. Something that did not care about named spells or proper form or how much the right shoulder was supposed to hurt.

My left hand stayed on the staff. The sigil held. My right hand closed on the glimmersteel at my left hip.

The shoulder brace from Weave tightened as I drew — particles compressing around the joint like borrowed tendons. The old wound screamed once and then the brace took the weight. The sword came free.

Raw light climbed the blade.

Not gold-white. Not the clone's light. Mine. Warm gold bleeding to white at the edges, the way a filament brightens past its rated heat. It did not spiral or shape or follow a spell matrix. It just answered. The way a heartbeat answers a sprint. The way breathing answers a scream.

I crossed the distance.

Marisol pulled her shadow back — not retreating, making space. She saw what was coming and she cleared the lane.

The clone turned. Her particle-wall came up — that flat disc of bright static she had used to stop my blades before. It met the light on my sword.

And it melted.

Not shattered. Not broke. Melted. The raw light ate through the particle-wall like it was wax, and the clone's face changed. The rage dropped. Something else took its place. Recognition. The kind that comes too late.

I drove the blade through her chest.

Gold-white particles erupted from the wound — not spray, not blood, just light evacuating a shape that could no longer hold it. The clone's mouth opened. No sound came out. Her arms came up — not to fight, not to grab, just the reflex of a body that did not know it was already gone.

Then she dissolved.

The particles scattered upward like embers from a fire someone had finally put out. Her shape thinned. Her edges blurred. Her face came apart last, both sides of it finally matching in the same expression: nothing.

She was gone. The arena floor where she had stood was clean. Not even residue. Just pavement.

I looked toward the sigil. Through the warm gold of the barrier, I could see the treeline — and Riley on the ground beyond it. The light blade that had been through her dissolved with the clone. What was left was only the wound. Open. Dark. Bleeding freely.

The sigil had to drop.

I did not think about it. My left hand released the staff and the warm gold disc dissolved — not breaking, just coming apart like light through water, the way it always did when I let a ward go.

Weave's particles surged past me the instant the barrier thinned. They had been pressed against the boundary, waiting — I felt them rush through the space where the sigil had been, a river of gold-green iridescence streaming toward the treeline, toward Riley.

I was already moving. Two steps. That was all. The sigil had been right here, right at the edge of the road, and Riley was just beyond it.

She was dying.

The wound was worse up close. The blade had gone through clean, but clean did not mean shallow. Blood had soaked through her clothes on both sides — front and back — and was spreading into the dirt beneath her. Her face was gray. Her eyes were open but the light behind them was pulling back, the way a tide pulls back before it does not return.

Echo was beside her on the ground — barely conscious, eyes too blurry to focus, but her hand was locked around Riley's. Hicks was there too, crouched on Riley's other side, one hand in her hair the way she liked. He looked up when I dropped to my knees and I did not have time to read his face.

The spellbook pressed against the road beneath me — I shifted my weight without looking, making room for what mattered.

I put my bare hand over Riley's heart.

*Radiant Anchor.*

It took more than light. It took pulse. It took presence. Golden light bloomed from the contact point and sank through her shirt, through her skin, into the faltering beat beneath, where the pulse was still there, still holding on. My heartbeat pushed into hers — once, twice, three times — and the light between us pulsed in time, visible through cloth and bone.

The gray in her face pulled back. Not gone. Arrested. Her chest rose under my palm. Her eyes found mine.

The cost hit like a wave. I felt the drain open behind my ribs — not pain exactly, but emptiness, the hollow place where the magic had been. A ripple of light, flickering — then gone. I could feel the drain. The light was low. I would pay for it later. Right now later did not matter.

I lifted my hand from her heart and my left hand found the staff where it had fallen beside me. The crystal tip was still warm.

*Prismatic Cure.*

White light fractured from the crystal — not the warm gold of the anchor, not the raw heat of the sword. Precise light. Careful light. It split into bands: deep red for the torn muscle beneath, cool blue for bone, warm gold for the skin that needed to close. Each band found its layer and worked, separate and simultaneous, the way a team of hands works a single body.

The gut wound began to close. Not fast enough. Fast enough. The red band sank into the wound's depth first — I felt muscle pulling together, flesh mending from the inside out. The blue band found what the blade had broken beneath, the places where the impact had spread past the wound itself, mending what the blade had cracked and torn. The gold band came last, pulling skin together from both sides, the edges meeting and holding.

I was not the only one healing her.

Weave's particles had wrapped Riley in a second skin of gold-green — not sutures, not anchors, something slower and deeper. A different kind of mending. I could feel the difference: my spells were emergency, stop-the-bleeding. Weave was working underneath that, at a layer I could not reach, in a rhythm I did not recognize.

Riley was breathing. The sutures were holding. The anchor was still pulsing faintly under her skin — I could see it, a slow gold rhythm that meant her heart had decided to keep going.

I turned to Echo.

She was still on the ground beside Riley — the same ground I was kneeling on. Her eyes were open but not tracking. Her head had hit the road. Hard. The residue had gotten to her, the same cold wrong that had hit me during the fight, and I could see what it had done. Her cheekbones were sharper than they should be. Her jaw. Her hands where they lay locked around Riley's — longer, the bones closer to the surface, like something had pulled her forward in a way she could not brace for.

Riley's shoulder was under Echo's head. Riley's arm was around her — not the arm that had been holding the gun, the other one, wrapped loose across Echo's back. Their hands were held together between them, weak, the kind of grip that stays only because neither person had the strength left to let go. Echo was partially on top of Riley, and neither of them had moved from that position since the blade went through. Close. Tender. Two people who really, really needed each other.

I reached for my spellbook.

Not the staff. The book. I unsnapped it from my side with my right hand — Weave's shoulder brace tightening around the joint as I moved — and opened it across my knee. The pages found the spell before I did. `Prismatic Cure.` The framework was there, the tracing, the light-path. I just had to hold it steady.

Marisol landed beside me. Not from the air this time. Just there — her coat brushing my shoulder, one of the glass rods in her hair glowing. I did not ask how she knew. She saw what I was doing and knelt beside me without a word.

I traced the spell through the book. White light rose from the page and fractured — not breaking, splitting, the way a prism splits sunlight. Deep red first, sinking toward Echo's head, toward the place where impact had rattled her brain against her skull. Cool blue next, spreading through her throat — the crushed place, the place the copy had squeezed. Warm gold last, wrapping her face and hands — not undoing what the residue had done, the effect felt permanent, but stopping the spread, sealing the edges where the cold wrong had been trying to dig deeper.

Marisol's hands came up beside mine. Shadow-light poured from her fingers — not dark the way her combat magic was dark, but cool, lavender-touched, the same muted color I had seen from her glass rods. She did not trace the spell. She channeled. She found the edges of my framework and pushed her own power through them, the way water follows the shape of a riverbed someone else has dug.

The light between us doubled.

It was not just my Prismatic Cure anymore. It was the same spell — my tracing, my book, my framework — but Marisol was feeding it. The bands deepened. The red dug further. The blue spread wider. The gold wrapped tighter. I felt the cost split — the drain that should have hit me alone pulling in two directions, Marisol taking half of what the spell demanded. Her breath caught once. Mine steadied.

The light worked through both of us. Her hands and mine. Shadow and prism. A second set of fingers closing wounds my light alone would have taken twice as long to reach.

Echo's eyes moved. Not tracking yet. But moving. Her fingers tightened once around Riley's hand — a reflex. A start.

I pulled my silver staff from where it lay beside me — the crystal tip still warm from the cascade, still humming with residual light. I pressed it into Echo's free hand and closed her fingers around the wood.

She could not cast right now. Neither of us expected her to. But the staff was a focus, and if she woke up scared and reached for magic that was not there, I wanted her to reach for something that would answer.

My spellbook stayed open on my knee. I had more casting to do before the night was over.

I looked toward the crater.

Leo was still in it. Marisol — who had moved back to his side after the shared cast — had one hand pressed to his chest, the glass rod in her hair glowing a weak, watery lavender. Not the bright transit-color from before. Not even the steady channeling she had just poured into Echo. Something thinner. Triage magic, barely more than a stopgap.

She caught my eyes.

"He's stable," she said. Her voice was rougher now — the cost of the shared spell catching up, layered on top of the fight. "Focus on Riley and Echo."

Leo answered before I could.

Not with words. With movement. His hand came up — the one that was not bent wrong — and planted against the crater's broken edge. # Flashback — The Spar Before Eureka

Morning sat hard on the yard, bright enough to read every mistake before it finished happening.

Packed dirt showed through the thin spring grass in pale, trampled patches. Last year's weeds still clung low along the fence line. The porch swing creaked once under Echo's weight and then settled into a small, lazy rock beside Luna, its chains ticking softly whenever the breeze bothered them. Out at the curb, a marked cop car lingered on the street like it had nowhere better to be. Nobody in the yard commented on it twice. The watched feeling had become part of the weather.

Leo came down into the yard with two practice blades and no speech ready. Riley followed with her cane in her left hand, bag still on her shoulder, gun riding her belt, the shape of the week still written plainly through the way she held herself. One week out of the ER meant her legs still worked, but the recovery was in everything else: the turn, the catch, the pull through her middle when she had to stop one motion and choose another, the moment after a hard demand when the body had to decide whether it could answer again.

W.E.A.V.E. was already there, spread thin and gold-green through the air and the edges of the grass, most of it nearest Riley. Watching. Measuring. Ready.

Leo offered Riley one of the practice blades.

"No ceremony," he said.

Riley took it in her right hand. "Good."

That was enough.

They stepped into the open space between the porch and the street. Leo's stance stayed compact and unshowy, blade easy in his right hand, weight balanced for reading instead of rushing. Riley set the cane just off her left side, not hidden, not explained. A third point of contact. A tool, same as the blade. Dust ghosted around the cane tip when she planted it.

On the porch, Luna stayed quiet. Echo leaned one elbow on the swing arm and watched the distance between shoulders, hips, and hands instead of the blades themselves.

Leo opened exactly the way he meant to: slow enough to study, fast enough to be honest. A short entry. A measured cut. Nothing flashy. He tested her range first, then her recovery. Riley met him clean on the first exchange, planted, pivoted, answered with a straight return that would have landed on a less disciplined body. The blade line was still hers when she had time to claim it.

"Again," Leo said.

He shifted left the next time. Not much. Just enough to ask for a turn.

Riley made it. The blade line stayed good. The cost showed a beat later, in the catch. Her cane tapped down harder than before as she borrowed it for balance and reset.

Leo saw it. Pressed there.

Every exchange after that tightened around the same truth. When Riley had a second to set her feet, the form still held. Her right arm stayed sure. Her cuts stayed practical and direct. But the moment the fight asked for a fast angle change, or a hard recover under pressure, her body lagged behind the decision she had already made. The hitch never looked dramatic. It looked worse than that. It looked small, real, and expensive.

The cane kept becoming part of the answer.

Plant. Pivot. Catch weight. Stay vertical.

Leo kept testing the left side because the left side forced the turn. Riley knew he was doing it. She didn't complain. She adjusted, set harder, used the cane as a spacer once, a brace the next time, a post to ride through a recovery that came back slower than she wanted. Once she got the blade where it needed to be and still had to wait a fraction for the rest of herself to arrive.

The porch swing went still.

Luna had stopped rocking without seeming to notice. Echo had not moved at all.

Then Leo changed the spacing by less than a foot, and that was enough.

Riley committed to the turn. The idea was right. Her body missed the timing by a fraction. The practice blade hit dirt first. The cane clattered away in the opposite direction. Then Riley was down on one knee and hand, breath cut short by the loss of balance more than the impact itself.

Leo immediately stepped out instead of in.

He did not offer a hand before she wanted one.

Riley stayed there for one beat, jaw set, looking not angry but exact.

Then, without looking up, she said, "W.E.A.V.E., help me up."

The bismuth swarm gathered at her left and behind her back, not carrying her, not lifting her like she weighed nothing, just giving her something stable and deliberate to push against. Iridescent plates slid and recomposed in the air until they made a brace with no human shape in it at all, only structure. Riley got one foot under herself, then the other. The swarm steadied. She rose with the careful, practical grimace of somebody spending pain on purpose. The cane skated once across the dirt toward her and tipped upright in reach.

"Thank you," Riley said, already taking it.

__Support applied.__ __Recovery interruption reduced.__

On the porch, Luna's fingers tightened once around the chain of the swing. Echo's eyes tracked the spot where Riley had lost the turn, then returned to Riley's face.

Leo waited until Riley was fully vertical again.

"Set, you're fine," he said. "Transition's the problem. Turn, recover, pressure after contact. That's where your body falls behind."

Riley rolled one shoulder and planted the cane again. "Yeah," she said. "Thanks. I needed that."

Leo gave a short nod. He did not soften the read, and he did not press the point after she took it.

They ran two more exchanges with the practice blades, enough to prove the same map twice. Riley could still fight. That was not the question. The question was what kind of fight her body could survive without lying to everyone involved. Each reset cost her a little more to hide: a slower breath out through her nose, a firmer plant of the cane, shoulders squaring before the next demand because squaring them now was cheaper than catching herself later.

Riley followed, slower, cane clicking once on the threshold before she reached for her own blade — the normal-looking glimmersteel longsword Officer Hicks had put in her hands, plain until it wasn't.

Riley's hand tightened around the hilt trigger.

Echo sat forward on the porch swing for the first time.

Luna's head came up, eyes fixed on the blade.

That was all.

The shape in the yard became a triangle.

## Round One

Riley and Luna took opposite sides of Leo. Riley's sword stayed on Darkness, edge drinking the morning in a way that made every motion look a fraction later than it was. Luna's blade remained plain steel. Leo held center and read the group before he read any one person.

The first pass belonged to spacing.

Riley came in from one angle with direct pressure, not fast enough to overreach, just hard enough to demand an answer. Luna moved on the other side with less force and cleaner patience, testing how much of left-handed swordwork she could trust before committing it. Leo gave ground only where he chose to, turning his shoulders instead of crossing his feet, never letting either of them close cleanly at the same time. Dirt rasped under boots. The triangle narrowed, opened, narrowed again.

"Too wide," he said once, and it wasn't clear which of them he meant until both adjusted.

Riley's Darkness edge hissed faintly when it met his glimmersteel. Luna's plain blade came in unlit and quiet, forcing Leo to account for two tempos at once. He did. Compact. Efficient. No wasted chase. Riley's stronger entries still came when she could drive straight through the line. When Leo made her turn to stay in it, the recovery took just long enough to show.

On the porch, Echo's gaze kept cutting back to Riley's recovery after each redirect, not the strikes themselves. Out at the street, the cruiser still sat there, patient as a held breath.

Riley clicked Darkness out. The wrongness left the air all at once.
