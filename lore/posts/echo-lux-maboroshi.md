I laughed. It came out as a scrape. My throat did not approve of laughter any more than it approved of speech.

Inside the box, the caster was breathing hard. His magic had sparked twice and failed both times. The pale threads of light guttered out against his palms and left tiny scorch marks on his skin. He looked at the marks like they were evidence of a betrayal he hadn't seen coming.

His composure was gone. The slow-clapping man was gone. What was left was raw and ugly and dangerous in the way cornered things were dangerous — no plan left except to hurt whatever was closest.

But Riley was still standing. The crystal was still in her hand. And I was still at the glass, held up by a woman I still didn't know, watching the fight turn in a direction the caster had not written into his third act.

Riley's free hand moved to the hilt.

The crystal followed — the white shape with its red edge-lines dropping toward the crossguard. She pressed it into the hilt. I couldn't hear the click through the glass but I saw the crystal settle into the steel and stay.

The Fracture light changed.

Not brighter. Not darker. Wronger. The white fracture-lines along the blade deepened — red bleeding into them, the crystal's edge-lines running up the steel like veins finding a heartbeat. The blade hummed at a frequency I felt through the glass. Through my palms. Through my teeth.

Then Riley's body language changed.

Not the way it changes in a fight. Different. Worse. Her shoulders locked. Her jaw went slack for half a beat and then tightened again — but her eyes stayed unfocused, the tightness not reaching them. Her hands stayed on the hilt but she looked at them. Then looked again. My throat gave. The rest died behind my teeth.

What do you say into that? Nothing. You press your forehead to the glass and you keep talking anyway because it's the only thing your body can do that matters.

"Riley—" my voice broke on the first syllable, scraped out of a throat that had nothing left. My palms pressed harder, fingers spreading against the cold. "You're— you're still there. The box. The—" the caster's hand came up, magic sparking at his knuckles, "—he's casting— his hand is up— right hand— belt flickering— Riley—"

He could still fight. Through the fracture. Through the crystal chewing on his sequence. He could still hurt her.

My voice broke. I let it.

The caster's hand twitched. Magic sparked. Backfired. The light crawled up his arm and he screamed — raw, animal. I felt it through the glass.

Riley didn't stop.

She couldn't look at me — her eyes were full of the fight and the fracture. I didn't know if she could hear my voice through the glass. Didn't know if it was reaching her. But Marisol's hair-glass was still lit, her lips still moving, and Riley was still swinging.

So I used what I had.

I stayed.

The sound comes from behind me.

Not inside the box. Not Riley. Not the fight. Tires. Screaming on asphalt — the high wrong pitch of rubber losing to speed around a corner it does not have room for. I hear it before I understand it. My body knows first. The part of me that has been in cars and around cars and scared in cars for years — it recognizes the sound of something about to hit something and not stop.

I turn my head. The motion costs me. My spine remembers the pavement. My throat remembers the hand. But I turn.

Marisol's arm tightens under mine.

The cruiser hits the box.

The impact travels through the ground first. Then my bones. Then the glass I am pressed against and every part of me that was already hurting. The box holds. The black-glass walls shudder but do not crack. Do not buckle. Whatever is keeping those walls sealed, it holds.

My knees buckle.

Not from the impact. From my body running out of whatever it has been borrowing to stay upright since the pavement. Marisol catches me before I drop — her arm coming under mine, her shoulder bracing my weight, her dark wings folding tighter to keep from throwing me off balance.

I nod. Cannot speak. Do not need to.

The cruiser has stopped dead against the box. Steam rises from the crumpled hood in thin white threads. The driver's door opens.

A cop gets out. Eureka Springs — the uniform says local, the face says professional. Gun drawn but not aimed. Held low and ready, the way people who have been in situations hold things they do not plan to fire unless they have to. Not the stillness of surprise. Something else.
