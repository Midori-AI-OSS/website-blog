The next two days, the nurse kept trying to get at my shoulder. I'd been answering in one-word replies and keeping my right arm tucked so close it might as well have been stapled there. The shoulder was a grinding ache that had stopped being an injury and started being furniture — part of the room, part of my body's new default shape, something I'd learned to exist around the way you learn to exist around a loud refrigerator.

"Both of you," Marisol said.

She wasn't looking at either of us. She was looking at the space between me and Leo like we were a single problem she'd decided to solve before breakfast. Leo opened his mouth. Marisol's eyebrow moved half a millimeter. He closed it.

I laughed. One sharp breath. Not because it was funny. Because Marisol had us both clocked and I was too tired to be insulted about it.

The shoulder assessment was exactly as horrible as I'd been avoiding. The joint had more range than it felt like it had any right to have, and the tests still sent white fire up through it hard enough to leave my good hand gripping the chair arm until it ached. The nurse asked questions and I answered in single syllables, because breathing through the pain was taking most of my available bandwidth and the rest was going toward not crying in front of Leo — which was a stupid thing to be protecting but I was protecting it anyway.

When she finished, she handed me a sheet of exercises and a look that said she'd met me before in other bodies.

"Absolutely," I said. "I will definitely do these."

Leo made a sound that was almost a laugh. He knew I was lying.

At intake they'd slid an IV into Echo's arm. She'd been in the wheelchair and too depleted to argue about it — she just sat there, head tipped back, while they taped the line down and got the drip going. By morning it was running steady, and I knew because I'd been awake.

A man in a navy wool overcoat filled the doorway five minutes before Leo and I were supposed to leave. James Poindexter. Echo's brother. He crossed to the bedside without breaking stride — the kind of walk that meant he'd done the mental math of the room before his foot hit the threshold.

His sleeves were pulled down past his wrists, arms tucked close inside the coat. No hands visible. I knew why and didn't comment.

He pressed his arm against Echo's shoulder through the coat — brief, warm — and set a paper bag on the tray table that smelled better than cafeteria. Echo's eyes went to it once and straight back to Riley. James didn't push. He took the chair beside her and I caught the quiet exchange of two people who'd learned to talk around each other's silences a long time ago.

He looked up once. Told us to go. Said he had her.

We went.

Leo and I ran home mid-morning. The house was an hour and fifteen minutes from the hospital. I drove — left-handed on the wheel, right arm still tucked close against my ribs whenever I didn't absolutely need it, the staff laid across the back seat because I didn't want to look at it and didn't want it out of reach either. Leo's wrist sat cradled in his lap. Every pothole sent a thin gold pulse through the cabin — his halo flaring once, then dark. He didn't mention it. I didn't mention that I was counting.

The house was empty. No one was home.

I walked into the kitchen and stopped. Not for any good reason. The dishwasher was still half-full from before the church. The mail was still on the counter. Leo's jacket was draped over the back of a chair — just his jacket, a thing he'd taken off and not put back on — and for some reason that was the detail that landed. Not the blinking microwave clock. Not the cold coffee maker. His jacket. Still there. Still waiting for him to come home and put it on.

I didn't linger. I grabbed my laptop, my charger that actually worked, a few outfits and clean socks. Leo grabbed his lecture materials and a second shirt. He paused at the counter the same way I had — same spot, same beat of stillness — and through our bond I caught the tired pull of him stalling there too. Nothing clean. Just the weight of it.

"Ten minutes," I said.

"Ten minutes."

The house locked behind us. I'd spent maybe ninety seconds of it actually inside. That was fine. The house was going to be there when this was over. Right now there was only one building that mattered and it was an hour and fifteen minutes the other way.

We got back to the second floor a little after one. The room had changed while we were gone.

Echo's chair was empty. The blanket near Riley's wrist still held the crease where Echo's hand had been, but the hand itself was gone. James was in the visitor chair on the other side of the bed, coat still on, sleeves pulled down past his wrists. He'd been there for hours and looked like he hadn't moved except to breathe.

"She's in tests," he said when I stopped in the doorway. "Concussion. Throat. They came for her about twenty minutes ago."

I didn't ask how he'd handled the handoff. The way Echo's chair was still warm near the seat said he'd done it right.

Leo took the chair by the window. I sat down on the other side of the bed. And James — who'd gotten up early, driven here, brought food, and spent hours in a room with an unconscious Riley and a bismuth swarm — asked us to tell him what happened.

We did. Not the sanitized version. The church. The fight. The roadside. The wrong-light still fading slow on Riley's arms. Leo gave the facts in the steady professor-voice that carried through anything. I filled in what his facts couldn't carry — Echo at the roadside, the seal, the depletion, the needle in Riley's chart. James listened without interrupting. When we finished, he was quiet for a moment. Then he asked two practical questions about Echo's recovery and one about whether we'd eaten.

He helped with the room while we talked. Adjusted the blanket where Echo's hand had pulled it crooked. Refilled the water pitcher. Small things.

Weave was at the window — gold-green and steady. The clean cup was already on the corner chair beside Riley's bag. James had placed it during the morning vigil. Weave had briefed me on the plan.

He offered to stay in touch through `Sending`. He'd been handling the calls and logistics she'd been ignoring. He'd keep doing it, he said. He was sorry he couldn't bring her clothes. The food had to be enough.

I told him the food was enough. It was.

His sleeves stayed pulled down the whole time. Arms tucked inside the coat.

He left around two-thirty. Paused at the door and looked back at Riley one more time, then at the empty chair where Echo would be sitting in a few hours. Didn't say anything.

Then he was gone. The room felt smaller without him in a way that wasn't bad — just quieter.

Echo came back a little before four with a fresh dressing on her IV port and a sandwich in one hand she'd clearly forgotten about. She stopped in the doorway, eyes finding the side table — the paper bag James had left, still there — then me, then Riley, and the tension in her shoulders unwound by half a degree.

"James?" Her voice was still a scrape.

"He was here. Left about an hour ago. Food's on the tray table."

She looked at the paper bag. Her expression didn't change, but her hand found the edge of it and held there for a second. Then she crossed to the bedside, settled into her chair, and put her hand on the blanket in the exact spot it had been, her thumb finding Riley's knuckles through the fabric like it had never left. The look on her face said she would not be separated from that bedside again for anything short of the building itself catching fire, and even then she'd want to know which exit was closest to Riley's room.

I took the chair on the other side of Riley's bed and balanced my laptop on my knees. The angle was wrong and my neck was going to hate me by evening but I could see Riley's face from here and the monitor from here and Echo from here, which was the whole checklist. I wasn't being subtle about it.

Echo had graduated from the wheelchair sometime late on day one — her accelerated healing doing in hours what hospital rest was supposed to do in days. Her throat was still wrecked, her voice a rasp that hadn't found its floor, but she was on her feet with her hand back on the blanket near Riley's wrist like it had never left. Like gravity. Like a law.

My laptop glowed on my knees — light work, the kind I could pick up and set down without losing a thought. Emails, scheduling, nothing I couldn't handle from a visitor chair. Every few minutes my eyes went up: Riley's face, the monitor, Echo's shoulders, then back to the screen. Not a system. Just the rhythm of someone who couldn't stop checking and wasn't going to try.

Leo set up at the small table by the window. Laptop balanced on textbooks. Headphones plugged in. His lecture slides open to diagrams I didn't try to read. He told the students at the top of the hour: "I'm teaching from a hospital room. If I need to leave, I will leave. The recording will catch whatever I miss." His voice was the steady, factual register he used when he needed to be a professor instead of a person — the one that doesn't shake no matter what's shaking underneath. I knew the difference. I was proud of him for it and he didn't need me to say so, which was also the deal.

No one asked follow-up questions. Either they didn't want to or the professor-voice was good enough. Either way.

My phone buzzed on the tray table that evening. Mom and Dad.

I hit video and angled the phone so the room could see. For twenty minutes the screen filled the silence with English accents and careful questions and the particular helplessness of parents who were an ocean away from everything they wanted to fix.

Leo handled most of the facts. Wrist — broken, splinted, yes it hurts, no he wasn't going to argue about pain management today. Luna's shoulder — managing. Riley — stable, unconscious, systemic depletion, the wound is sealed, Luna sealed it at the roadside. Echo — concussion mild, throat healing, she's right here.

I filled in what his facts couldn't carry. That Riley was being watched around the clock — not just by monitors and nurses, but by people who loved her and weren't leaving. That the needle trigger was flagged in her chart. That Echo hadn't left except the shower and when I'd forced her to. That the wrong-light was fading. That Marisol had taken paid leave to stay. That Hicks was driving an hour each way just to show up. That we were okay. That we were all okay. That we were going to be okay.

The last one was a lie. I said it anyway. Some lies are load-bearing.

Echo listened from her spot at the bedside, not speaking — her throat wouldn't have let her anyway — but tears tracked slow down her cheeks, the kind she didn't seem aware of. Her eyes stayed on the phone like it was another person in the room.

When our mother said "Tell Weave we're thinking of her too," the gold-green shimmer at the window brightened half a shade. Not acknowledgment exactly. Just presence, made visible. Weave didn't speak. She didn't need to.

The call ended. Mom and Dad said they'd send money for food and whatever else. The offer hung in the air — practical, generous, completely inadequate in the way all long-distance love was.

I looked at Leo. He looked at me. The bond gave me the tired hum of him and nothing else, which was everything. Neither of us said the obvious thing — that an ocean was too far, that fine was a lie we'd gotten too good at telling, that we wanted them here.

I didn't say it. Neither did he.

The Gustins arrived a little after seven.

Grandma Gustin came through the door first — the same way she'd done at the hospital two weeks ago, surveying the space like someone already calculating what needed doing next. Kelsey stopped in the doorway and froze. I watched her take in the room — Riley's face, the monitor, the wrong-light still fading on her arms — and her expression pulled tighter instead of breaking. She managed herself. Needed a second. Took it.

Aunt Che came in last and went straight to the chat — light, steady, the kind of conversation that fills a room without asking anyone to feel anything they weren't ready to feel. Echo answered in monosyllables from the bedside, her hand still wrapped around Riley's. Aunt Che took it like a full sentence and moved on.

Kelsey crossed to the bed once she'd found her feet. She'd done Riley's color, the blue parts, the roots — and when she touched it now it was muscle memory. Fingers working through the tangles where the pillow had pressed it flat. Quiet. The same motion she'd been doing for years, just in a room that shouldn't need it.

Then she leaned down and whispered something against Riley's hair. Private. Her mouth close to Riley's ear, her hand still tangled in the strands she'd just fixed. Nobody else heard what she said. Echo was right there and didn't catch it. Weave's particles — still pressed into the cup on the corner chair — stopped for half a second and didn't speak. I didn't look. This wasn't for us.

She left a hair tie on the side table. Small, dark elastic, the kind that had probably been around her own wrist. Then she stepped back.

Grandma Gustin found me near the window. Not by accident. "You've been here the whole time."

I didn't deny it. "Someone has to be."

She nodded once. The kind of nod that meant she'd done the same math across a different set of years. We stood there for a moment — the two people who'd spent the most hours in hospital chairs for Riley — and didn't say anything else.

Then she asked about the gun.

Riley's gun. The one Leo had put in his car during intake when security ran their sweep. Grandma Gustin wanted to take it home. Lock it up until Riley could come get it herself. I told her yes. Leo said yes from the table before I could look at him. When Leo pushed back from the table, Grandma Gustin tracked the motion — the splinted wrist, the gold flicker from his halo as he stood up. She gave him the same surveying look she'd given the room. He walked out with them to move the gun from his car to hers.

Echo stayed planted at the bedside the whole time. Her thumb kept tracing Riley's knuckles and the family worked around her like a current around a stone.

Weave was in the cup on the corner chair beside Riley's bag. Particles dimmed to nearly nothing, barely drifting.

The Gustins left quiet and steady. Hugs at the door that were brief and practical and meant more for being understated. Grandma Gustin squeezed my good shoulder — the left one — and didn't say anything she didn't mean. Leo came back from the parking lot a few minutes later, the gun no longer in his car.

When the door closed, the room settled. Echo's thumb kept moving. Weave's particles stayed in the cup — waiting, maybe, to be sure the Gustins were really gone. Nobody spoke into the silence.

The Schaefers came after the Gustins had been gone long enough for the room to find its breathing again. Grandma and Grandpa Schaefer. Riley's other grandparents.

Grandma Schaefer came in first, and the look on her face when she saw Echo at the bedside wasn't surprise. Recognition. Riley had told them about us — photos, stories, names. She crossed to Echo and took her free hand in both of hers. "You're Echo. She talks about you constantly." Echo's thumb stopped on Riley's knuckles for one beat, then kept moving.

Then I asked if Riley had sent them photos. From bars. From outings. The group shots she was always pulling us into.

Grandma Schaefer nodded. "She did. She sends us everything." Then she paused. I watched the pause travel through her — the way someone does the math of a photograph and comes up one short. "There's five of you. Echo, Luna, Leo, Riley, and—" Another pause. "...dust in the pictures. She talked about Weave too."

I pointed to the cup on the corner chair beside Riley's bag.

Grandma Schaefer looked. The particles inside barely moved. Weave didn't speak. Didn't leave the cup. But after a moment — just long enough for the acknowledgment to land — the particles did a slow, deliberate spin. Then dimmed again.

Grandma Schaefer let out a breath. "Well. All right then." She looked at the cup for another second, then back at Riley. She didn't ask any more questions. She just knew, now, that Weave was in the room.

Grandpa Schaefer set a casserole dish on the tray table and a stack of blankets on the chair by the door. Then he pulled a folded envelope from his coat — money, gift cards, a scrap of paper with their landline — and put it next to the casserole like it was part of the same delivery. Food, warmth, resources.

They'd heard about Leo from Riley's stories — the professor, the one with the halo — and meeting him in person wasn't awkward. It was warm in the way meeting someone you already know from photos is warm. Grandpa Schaefer nodded at Leo's splinted wrist and didn't ask for the story.

They asked about the wrong-light on Riley's arms. I told them it was fading. That it wasn't active anymore. That the doctors said it could still be days before she was aware. Grandma Schaefer's mouth tightened and she didn't cry. She put her hand on Riley's ankle through the blanket and held it there.

The Schaefers stayed another half hour. Ate casserole with us. Made sure we had their numbers. Said they'd come back. Hugged each of us on the way out — even Echo, who let them, and even Leo, who looked startled by it and didn't pull away.

When the door closed, the room was fuller than it had been in days. The fullness was food and blankets and the knowledge that someone else was in this now. Echo's thumb kept moving. Weave's particles stayed in the cup a while longer, then peeled out slow and rejoined the window. The room found its shape one more time.

Day three was more of the same. Which meant it was harder.

The routine had calcified into something that felt less like endurance and more like a very boring hostage situation with better lighting. Leo taught his other class — different slides today, same steady voice, same gold flicker every time his wrist shifted wrong. I watched him from across the room the way I'd been watching him since we were kids: looking for the cracks. He was holding. Barely. The students probably didn't notice the two sharp halo pulses during the containment-theory section. I noticed.

I worked again. Emails. Schedule adjustments. A second co-worker had emailed — word travels, or the first co-worker talked — and I typed *family situation, taking a few days, updates when I have them* because the truth was not a thing you put in a work email and I'd learned that lesson the hard way years ago.

Hicks showed up between shifts. Drove an hour each way. Stayed forty minutes. Left a convenience-store bag on the counter — chips, pretzels, sports drinks, bottled coffee, and a second cold chocolate milk for Echo. Not the big practical Walmart mercy this time. Just enough sugar, salt, and caffeine to keep five exhausted idiots upright for one more shift. He looked exhausted and didn't mention it. I didn't thank him. He didn't need it. That was the deal with Hicks.

Marisol came back at dusk with good coffee and wings tucked to a near-nothing shadow at her shoulders. She took the chair by the door — the one nobody else sat in — and the room adjusted around her presence like someone had checked the locks and found them holding. It was one less thing for my brain to track and she knew it and that was also the deal.

Echo didn't leave the bedside. The nurses had stopped trying around lunch on day two. Her thumb was still tracing the same path across Riley's knuckles — a rhythm so steady it had become part of the room's ambient noise. Like a second monitor. Another count.

Riley didn't move. The green line traced her heart rate the same way it had since the morning the doctors said *days to unknown*. I'd stopped hoping for a different answer sometime around noon on day two. The hope was still there — it always is, infuriatingly stubborn — but it had learned to sit quietly and wait.

At the end of day three, I closed my laptop.

The click was small — two hands, the lid settling into place — but it landed like a door shutting. Work was done. Nothing left but the room and the monitor and the slow, heavy work of staying present.

Leo shut down his lecture slides a few minutes later. I felt the tired release of him through the bond and saw his shoulders drop half an inch now that the performance was over. The wrong-light on Riley's arms had faded by roughly half by now — no longer as stark as it had been. It looked less like a wound and more like a shadow. A thing that had been there and was slowly deciding not to be.

Weave had been passing through the room all day in a quiet, deliberate orbit: Riley's face, the monitor, Echo's hand on the blanket, a slow circuit past me, past Leo, collecting the shape of each of us before returning to the bed. She orbited because the room was full of people she refused to lose and the only thing she could do about it was stay.

I watched the particles draw inward around Riley's bed — gold to green to gold, cycling slow as breath — and let my head rest against the chair back.

The room was warm from five bodies in it and the quiet light at the window. The exhaustion was a shared weight now — not lonely, just heavy, the particular gravity of people who had learned how to wait and were still doing it.

Leo's presence hummed quiet through the bond. Echo's thumb was still moving. Marisol was still by the door. Riley was still gone wherever she'd gone.

But we hadn't gone anywhere. After three days that was starting to feel less like failure and more like a promise.

The room on the fourth morning was full of the same people and the same quiet and the same green line doing its small stubborn work. Nothing had changed. That was starting to be its own kind of wound.

Echo woke before the window finished deciding to be morning. I knew because I was already awake. The chair on Riley's other side had molded itself to my spine over three nights and I'd stopped pretending I was going to find a better position. Her thumb was already on the blanket near Riley's wrist — I'd watched her reach for it before her eyes were fully open, the motion automatic, the way you check a door you've been guarding.

She lifted her head. Swallowed. I heard the scrape in her throat before she spoke — still there, still wrecked, the clone's fingers still written into the cartilage three days later. She looked at Weave's shimmer at the window.

"Did she—"

The question broke. The sound of it before the words were finished told me she already knew the answer and was asking anyway because the asking was the only thing she could do.

Weave brightened once. Her voice came steady — the way she always sounded when the news was not good enough to dress up. __No significant change overnight.__

The words landed. Stayed there.

I did not move. I watched her thumb stop and her hand flatten against the blanket like she was testing whether the world was still solid.

It was. That was the problem.

Her next swallow hurt more than the first. I could hear it — the click in her throat, the way the muscles caught. The chair leg scraped when she shoved back from it. The sound cut through the room and nobody filled the space after.

"Four mornings." She was already on her feet. The blanket fell away from where her hand had been. "Four mornings and nothing."

Nobody answered. Leo was at the window with a paper cup gone cold at his elbow, his other wrist still splinted. Marisol was in the chair by the door, boots planted, attention already cutting between Echo and the bed. I did not move from my chair. I only watched Echo's thumb stop rubbing the blanket.

Her hand came off it. "You can't just—" Breath hitched. The sentence broke open on the way out. "You can't do this and then just stay gone. You can't."

Her palm cracked against the rail.

Metal rang. Sharp. The sound traveled through the bed frame and into my hand where it was resting on the mattress and I felt it — the impact, the vibration, the shape of a body throwing something it could not afford to lose against a surface that would not give. Echo. The person who hated loud noises and sudden movement and attention. Hitting a hospital bed rail with her bare hand because the alternative was worse.

I was already standing. The motion was fast, and I ignored it.

"I can't fix this," she said, louder now, tears already in it. "I can't help you. I can't do anything, and you are still not waking up."

She swung again. Sloppier. Weave got there before I did. Gold-green light bloomed between Echo and the bed — dense, bright, sealing off the IV line, the monitor leads, the tray table corners, anything that could turn sharp in a bad hand. The rail stayed exposed. Too close to Riley to wall off cleanly.

__Echo. Stop.__ A beat. Softer. __You are going to hurt yourself.__

Echo shoved at the light anyway. Her hand went through it and caught the rail on the follow-through hard enough to ring the room a second time. The sound caught her in the chest — I watched it travel through her the way a sob does before it breaks. She went with it, and her arm kept moving.

I caught her by the face with both hands.

Not hard. Just enough to stop the next wild turn. My palms against her jaw, my fingers wrapping toward her ears, my thumbs just below her cheekbones. I held her there until her gaze struck me instead of the bed beyond me. Her skin was warm under my hands. Her pulse was hammering in her temples. I could feel it through my thumbs.

"Echo."

That was all. Her name. Steady. Quiet. Not a command. Just a fact.

For one breath it worked. Her eyes held mine. Her breathing caught in the middle and stalled there. Then her gaze tore sideways — toward Weave, toward the bright knot of gold-green at the bed — and she twisted hard enough that her shoulder clipped me on the way through.

"No more updates." Her voice shredded on the way out. "No more calm voice. No more floating there like this is fine." She tried to wrench herself toward Riley and didn't get free. I had her. The next breath came out broken — the plea underneath the anger finally showing through. "If you can do more for her, do it. Please. Fucking do something."

She grabbed at me. Hard enough to leave marks. I did not pull back. I changed my grip instead of losing it — one hand staying at her jaw, the other sliding down to her shoulder and keeping her from pitching forward into the bed.

Then the fight ran out of her.

Not gradually. All at once. Her grip loosened. Her hands dropped. Her shoulders jerked once, twice, and then she folded into sobbing — not louder, not dramatic, the kind that stops pretending to be anything else.

"She's not waking up." Her voice was smaller now. Broken in a different way. "She's not waking up."

I stayed close. My hand still at her jaw, my other braced warm and solid at her shoulder. No speech about hope. No false promise. I was not going to lie to her in a room that had already held too many lies. I just kept my hands where they were and my body planted in front of her like something that would not move unless she wanted it to.

By the window, Leo did not come in. One hand on the table, the other cradled against his chest, his eyes moving once — Echo, me, monitor — and then settling on the green line. He knew the distance was useful right now. He was right.

Marisol crossed just far enough to ease the door most of the way shut. One hand on the handle. Holding the threshold without stepping into the middle of the room. Her wings were withdrawn — I could not see them — but the glass rod in her hair caught the room light and gave back a thin violet glint.

The force bled out of Echo once it had somewhere to go. Her breathing scraped less. Her shoulders stopped jerking. When she tried to talk, what came out first was a torn, useless apology.

My thumbs brushed once beneath her eyes. Just once. Grounding her to the touch.

"There you are."

Then I pulled her in. Not a rescue. Not a big motion. Just one brief, solid hold that gave her somewhere to drop her weight. When she sagged, I went with her — one hand sliding to the back of her neck, the other staying at her shoulder — until the two of us sank back into the visitor chair. My body took the extra weight without comment. I did not listen to the protest from three days of stillness.

Echo folded forward. For a few seconds she stayed bent over herself, breathing raggedly into the space between her knees, eyes shut, hands empty. Then one hand groped back toward the bed. Stopped at the blanket near Riley's wrist. Not skin. Not yet. The place she had been keeping for four days. Her fingers found the fabric and held.

Leo stepped in. Just far enough to set a glass of water on the side table. Close enough to take. Far enough not to feel like pressure. Then he went back to the window. I caught his eye for half a second — the bond gave me nothing but the tired hum of him, which was still everything.

Weave let the barrier peel back from the rail. The particles dimmed once, then folded into a tighter orbit at the foot of the bed.

__Echo friend.__ Her voice was different now — not clinical, not distant. __I have been running this problem in a loop. There are actions available to me beyond waiting. I have not taken them yet.__ A pause. __Fear entered the calculation. Not only yours. Mine.__ __I may have one way to help Riley, but I do not yet trust it to help more than harm. Let me turn it over a little longer.__

The last line went wider than the rest. Gold-green shimmer brushed the doorframe, flashed once against the far wall, and snapped inward — the orbit cinching tight above the bed.

__Apologies. Range corrected.__

Echo's fingers knotted the blanket. She stared at Weave. When she spoke again the anger was gone, leaving only the raw edge behind. "If you do try it, don't— don't lose her trying."

I did not take my hand off Echo. "Then take the extra time. Riley first."

Weave stayed where she was. The particles narrowed until the orbit sat close against the bed. Not yet.

Then the monitor chirped — one short bright note — and my hand went still against Echo's back.

Echo's fingers clenched in the blanket. Her head came up hard — I felt the pull travel through her shoulders, into my palm. Riley's eyes were open — and they weren't looking at any of us. Fixed on the ceiling — blank, distant, the way someone looks when they are in the room and also somewhere else entirely. But open. After four days. Open.

Echo's breath caught so hard it hurt. She pushed herself upright on her free hand before the rest of the room finished freezing around her. "Riley... Riley, it's me. It's Echo."

I was already leaning in. One hand stayed firm between Echo's shoulders, holding her in the chair — she would have launched herself at the bed if I hadn't, and the IV line was right there. My other hand found the call button by feel and pressed it without taking my eyes off Riley's face.

"Riley." Low. Steady. The way I'd said her name a hundred times before when she was waking up confused in the dark. "I'm here."

By the window, Leo straightened. One hand braced on the sill until he made himself let go of it. "Riley. It's Leo."

At the door, Marisol's hand tightened on the handle. Beside the bed, Weave drew into a smaller, brighter ring.
