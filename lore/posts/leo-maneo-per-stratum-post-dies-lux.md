The next two days flattened into watch shifts.

{{image: /lore/real-moments/maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux/room/room-maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux-1.png}}

By early day two, Echo had already been out of the wheelchair since late the day before. Her throat was still rough but she was on her feet.

Day two started with a proper splint for my wrist. The nurse worked fast — padding, rigid form, wrap. The new immobilization was tighter than the temporary job from intake. The bone still shifted but the wrist was supported now and I wouldn't make it worse by forgetting. Luna got her shoulder assessed at the same time. Range of motion was better than either of us expected. It still hurt. The nurse had her move through a few exercises and cleared her to start using it again. Marisol said "Both of you" when Luna started to argue. That ended the discussion.

James showed up just before we left for home. He came through the door in his navy coat and messenger satchel, sleeves pulled down past the elbow cuffs. No glyph projection visible — deliberate. I noted it and let it go.

He stopped inside the threshold and read the room the same way he reads a workshop: who's where, what's stable, what needs hands. Echo's posture shifted half an inch toward him before he reached her. He pressed his arm against her shoulder through the coat and left it there.

"Food's in the bag. Eat it."

Luna gave him the short version — where we were going, when we'd be back. James nodded twice. He didn't ask questions that could wait.

I left my lecture materials on the table near the window. James would still be here when we got back.

Luna and I ran home mid-morning. The drive is an hour and fifteen minutes each way. Luna drove. I sat passenger with my splinted wrist in my lap. The house was empty. The kitchen was still as we'd left it — dishwasher half-full, mail on the counter, my jacket over the back of a chair. Luna grabbed her laptop, charger, a few outfits. I grabbed my lecture materials and a second shirt. We didn't stay. We were back at the hospital by early afternoon.

James was in the visitor chair when we got back. Echo was gone.

I stopped one step inside the door. James straightened in the chair. Weave was at the window — her usual circuit, slow and steady. The clean cup was already on the corner chair beside Riley's bag. James had placed it during the morning vigil. Weave briefed us on it after we sat down.

"She went around twelve-forty," James said. "Concussion, throat, IV port. They wanted a quiet room for the monitoring."

I set my things down at the window table. Luna took the chair on Echo's side of the bed. The monitor was steady. I checked it twice before I sat.

"Fill me in."

James had been asking the right questions. He laid out the fight in sequence — what Echo had told him, what Weave had filled in during the quiet stretch. He was calm about it the same way he was calm about spellbook binding. No drama. Facts in order. When he finished, I sat with it for a minute. Echo's empty chair was still pulled close to the bed.

"She held the line," I said.

"Echo always does."

James let the silence sit. Then he said he'd stay in touch through *Sending* — handle the logistics Echo had been ignoring.

"Good."

He stayed another hour and change. Moved the water pitcher closer to Luna's side. Topped off my coffee without being asked. Around two-thirty he stood and straightened his coat.

"Tell her I'll be back. Whenever she wants."

Luna looked up. "We will."

He paused at the door. "The age-shifting changes her sizes. I couldn't bring clothes. Not willing to guess, ya know?" Not an apology. A fact.

"Understood."

James left. The door settled. The room felt smaller.

I set up at the small table near the window. Laptop on a stack of textbooks. Headphones plugged in. Lecture slides open to dual-layer containment theory. At the top of the hour, I unmuted: "I'm teaching from a hospital room. If I need to leave, I will leave. The recording will catch whatever I miss." No follow-up questions. I moved through the material on schedule — containment fundamentals, outer boundary and inner structure holding tension against each other. I've taught it before. The words came from practice.

Luna had her laptop on her knees by then. Light remote work — emails, schedule adjustments. Every few minutes her eyes went to Riley's face, the monitor, then back to the screen.

Echo came back around quarter to four. Fresh IV dressing, a sandwich she hadn't eaten. She settled into the chair and put her hand on the blanket in the same spot it had been. A thin ribbon of gold-green particles slipped back through the door and rejoined the main swarm. No one said anything about the chair being used or the extra food on the counter. Echo didn't ask about James. I didn't offer.

{{image: /lore/real-moments/maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux/room/room-maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux-3.png}}

That evening, Luna's phone rang — video call. Our parents. Luna angled the phone on the tray table so the room could see. Our parents' faces filled the screen. Twenty minutes of English accents and careful questions and the particular worry of parents an ocean away. I answered most of the questions. Luna filled the gaps. Echo listened from her spot at the bedside, saying nothing. Marisol added a line about the medical outlook. Weave stayed quiet and the gold-green shimmer at the window brightened half a shade when our mother said "Tell Weave we're thinking of her too."

The call ended. Quieter after, but not heavier. They said they'd send money for food and whatever else was needed.

That evening, after the video call, the Gustins came.

Grandma Gustin led them in — Aunt Che and Kelsey behind her. Grandma remembered me from the hospital two weeks ago. She gave me a nod from across the room and then she was moving, taking stock of the room the same way she had the first time.

Kelsey froze in the doorway when she saw Riley. She didn't need to be guided in. She managed it herself, crossed to the bed, and touched Riley's hair. She leaned close and whispered something into Riley's ear. Kelsey left a hair tie on the side table when they were done.

Weave had gone still inside the cup on the corner chair beside Riley's bag. Particles dimmed, barely drifting. I checked the monitor. Steady.

{{image: /lore/real-moments/maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux/room/room-maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux-5.png}}

Echo stayed planted at the bedside.

Grandma Gustin and Luna had a quiet exchange near the foot of the bed — two people who'd been the most present, saying more with pauses than words. Luna handled it. I stayed at the window.

Then Grandma Gustin turned to me. "The gun. Riley's. Do you still have it?"

"Trunk of my car."

"If you'd trust me with it, I'll take it home. Lock it up until she can get it back."

I thought about it for a second. "Yeah. When you're ready to leave, I'll walk you out."

Aunt Che kept up a steady conversation — light, easy. Being present without making it about herself. I tracked the room the same way I'd been tracking it for two days: monitor, Riley's face, Luna's posture, the door.

When the Gustins got ready to leave, I walked out with them. The night air was cooler than the room. I popped the trunk, pulled the gun case, and handed it to Grandma Gustin. She took it with both hands and nodded once. No fuss.

I watched them drive off and walked back inside. My wrist ached from the cold. I checked the monitor when I got back. The green line hadn't moved.

The Schaefers came after. Grandma and Grandpa. They came in loaded — Tupperware, blankets, a gift card, a scrap of paper with their landline. Real food. Blankets that smelled like a house, not a hospital.

Grandma Schaefer shook my hand with both of hers. "Leo," she said. Not a question. "She talks about you. All of you." Riley had shown them photos. Pre-briefed. Meeting them wasn't awkward. They already knew who we were to her.

They met Echo the same way — warm, familiar. Grandpa Schaefer found places for the food and blankets without being told where anything went.

Luna asked if Riley had sent them photos from bars and outings. Grandma Schaefer confirmed — then paused.

"There's one more of you, isn't there. Weave. Riley told me about her. But I don't think I've ever seen her in a photo."

Luna pointed to the cup on the corner chair. The particles did a slow, light spin inside it.

Grandma Schaefer looked at the cup. Didn't flinch. "Well. Now I've met her." She said it like it counted.

They stayed about forty minutes. Left the food, the blankets, the gift card, and their numbers. "Call us for anything."

The door closed. The room settled back to us: Echo at the bedside, Luna in her chair, me at the window. Weave's particles drifted out of the cup and back toward the window. The monitor was steady.

Day three was more of the same. That was the hard part.

I taught my other class — same setup, same window. Luna worked again. Hicks showed up between shifts — an hour's drive each way — stayed for forty minutes, checked on everyone, left a bag of convenience-store snacks on the counter, and went back to work.

{{image: /lore/real-moments/maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux/room/room-maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux-6.png}}

Marisol was back by dusk for the overnight watch, coffee in hand, and took the chair by the door.

Echo did not leave the bedside. The nurses stopped trying around noon on day two.

Riley did not move. The monitor traced her heart rate in the same steady green line it had held since the morning the doctors said days to unknown.

The wrong-light on her arms had faded by roughly half. Not as stark as the morning the doctors called it scar-light.

Weave had been in motion all day — a deliberate circuit: Riley's face, the monitor, Echo's hand, then past Luna in the visitor chair and me by the window, returning to the bed each time. I tracked the pattern without comment.

At the end of day three, Luna closed her laptop. I shut down my lecture slides. Echo's hand hadn't moved from Riley's. The gold-green particles at the window drew inward, tightened around Riley's bed, and steadied. The room was warm from five people in it.

When I woke on day four, Echo was already at the bedside. The window was still dark. Echo wore her cardigan.

She was still in the same spot — chair at Riley's bedside, her thumb hooked into the blanket near Riley's wrist. The wrong-light on Riley's arms had faded by roughly half. I wanted to study this kind of magic later. Noted. My wrist was still splinted and cradled against my chest. I had stopped noticing the ache somewhere in the night. Stopped noticing.

I was by the window. Paper cup cold at my elbow. Luna was in the visitor chair on the far side of the bed, shoulders folded, watching the same things I was watching. Marisol had the chair by the door — boots planted, attention split between hallway and bed. Weave's gold-green particles drifted near the monitor, the IV pole, the edge of Riley's bed. The same circuit she had been running for three days. I had tracked the pattern without comment since the morning it started.

Echo lifted her head. Her voice scraped on the first word.

"Did she—"

It snagged. She didn't try again.

Weave brightened once. Her voice came through in the back of my mind. __No significant change overnight.__

I looked to the monitor. The green line was tracing the same rhythm. Steady. Unchanged.

Echo swallowed and set her hand flat on the blanket again. She kept her palm there for a second, weight still in the chair, checking. Riley hadn't moved. Nothing had changed.

Her next swallow was harder. The chair leg caught when she shoved back from it.

"Four mornings," she said, on her feet now. "Four mornings and nothing."

No one cut in.

Her hand came off the blanket. "You can't just—" The breath hitched. "You can't do this and then just stay gone. You can't."

Her palm cracked against the rail. Metal rang.

Luna was out of her chair before the sound died. I stayed at the window. The monitor was still steady. My broken wrist stayed cradled against my chest. Close enough to step in. Far enough not to crowd. I checked Echo first, then Luna. I left my eyes there.

Echo swung again, sloppier. Weave moved first — particles sweeping in between Echo and the bed, sealing off the IV line, the monitor leads, the tray table corners. __Echo. Stop.__ A beat. __You are going to hurt yourself.__

Echo shoved at the light. Her hand went through it. The sound that came out of her was raw enough to make her double over, but her arm kept moving and caught the rail on the follow-through hard enough to ring the room a second time.

Luna caught Echo by the face with both hands — not hard, just enough to stop the next wild turn — and held her there until Echo's eyes found her instead of the bed.

"Echo," Luna said.

For one breath it worked. Then Echo tore sideways toward Weave's bright knot at the bed, twisting hard enough to clip Luna on the way through.

"No more updates," Echo snapped. "No more calm voice. No more floating there like this is fine. If you can do more for her, do it. Please. Fucking do something."

Luna held on. She shifted her hands — one at Echo's jaw, the other steadying her shoulder.

Echo grabbed at Luna's arm. Then her shoulders dropped and she started sobbing — quiet, ragged.

"She's not waking up," Echo said. Then again, smaller. "She's not waking up."

Luna stayed close. No speech. Just her hand at Echo's jaw, her other braced at Echo's shoulder.

Marisol crossed to ease the door most of the way shut, then stopped with one hand on the handle, holding the threshold without stepping into the middle of the room.

I kept watching the monitor. The green line was still tracing the same rhythm.

Echo's shoulders jerked once, twice. When she tried to talk, what came out first was a torn apology.

Luna's thumbs brushed once beneath Echo's eyes. "There you are," Luna said. Then Luna pulled her in — one brief hold that gave Echo somewhere to drop her weight. They sank back into the visitor chair together. Echo folded forward. One hand groped back toward the bed and stopped at the blanket near Riley's wrist. Not skin. The place she had been keeping.

{{image: /lore/real-moments/maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux/room/room-maneo-per-stratum-post-dies-lux-7.png}}

I stepped in to set a glass of water on the side table where she could reach it. Then I went back to the window.

Echo's breathing scraped less. Luna loosened without leaving. Marisol let the door rest against the frame. The water sat untouched. The monitor kept its rhythm. Nobody asked Echo to leave. Nobody pretended any of this had fixed anything.

Weave let the barrier peel back from the rail. The particles steadied close to Riley.

Her voice came through in the back of my mind. __Echo friend.__ __I have been running this problem in a loop.__ __There are actions available to me beyond waiting.__ __I have not taken them yet.__ __Fear entered the calculation.__ __Not only yours. Mine.__ __I may have one way to help Riley, but I do not yet trust it to help more than harm.__ __Let me turn it over a little longer.__

The shimmer hit the doorframe and pulled back fast. __Apologies. Range corrected.__

Echo's fingers knotted the blanket. "If you do try it — don't lose her trying."

Luna didn't take her hand off Echo. "Then take the extra time. Riley first."

Then the monitor gave a small, bright chirp.

Echo's hand locked on the blanket. Her head came up fast. Riley's eyes were open.

Fixed on the ceiling — blank, distant. Not on any of us.

I straightened before the silence broke. One hand came off the sill. I stayed by the window and checked the monitor first — the numbers were always the first thing I checked — then lifted my eyes back to her face.

Echo was already talking. "Riley... it's me, it's Echo." Luna was leaning in, one hand on Echo's shoulders, the other finding the call button. "Riley," Luna said. "I'm here."

"Riley." My voice was steady. "It's Leo."

Marisol's hand tightened on the door handle. Weave drew into a smaller, brighter ring.

Riley did not look at any of us.

"Monitor's steady," I said after a second. Then, quieter: "Riley. We're here."

The lids came down a minute or so later. Not fast. Not like sleep. Just closing without turning.

I kept watching the green line.

The door opened on Collins first, vitals cart rolling quiet. Dr. Brown behind her. Marisol shifted to give them the lane.

Collins looked at Riley, then at Echo's hand, then at Luna. She did not ask Echo to let go.

"What changed?"

Luna answered. "Her eyes opened. No tracking. No words. No response to us. Then they closed again."

"Vitals stayed steady," I said. "Heart rate didn't jump. Breathing didn't change."

Collins nodded. "Okay. Echo, you can keep your hand there unless I tell you otherwise. If I need it, I'll say it first." Weave drew back from the bedside rail — particles thinning away from the IV line, the call button, the strip of mattress where hands needed to pass.

Collins set two fingers at Riley's throat. "Riley, it's Collins. I'm going to check your eyes."

When the penlight clicked on, Marisol cast a low pulse of darkness across Echo's face — not across Riley, not over the doctor, just enough to blunt the spill of light. The glass rod tucked into her hair glowed once, brief and violet, then faded. A casted pulse. I noted it and stayed on the monitor.

Collins lifted one lid, then the other. The light came and went. Riley did not blink for it or turn. Dr. Brown stepped in when Collins shifted back.

"Riley, this is Dr. Brown. Can you open your eyes for me?"

Nothing.

"Can you squeeze Echo's hand?"

Echo's fingers tightened. Riley gave nothing back.

I checked the monitor again. Collins checked it too, half a beat behind me.

Collins clicked the penlight off. Marisol's darkness stayed for another second before folding back by degrees.

"She's not awake," Dr. Brown said. "And she is not showing awareness yet. I don't want to dress this up as more than it is."

He paused.

"But it is a change. After this many days with no response, spontaneous eye opening matters. If this pattern holds, we may start seeing signs of awareness in the next three to four days."

Three to four days. The set of my jaw changed by one small degree. I did not look away from the monitor.

Collins adjusted the blanket where the exam had shifted it and left Echo's hand alone. She paused on her way back to the cart, one hand on the rail.

"Walked past on the way in. One of the long-termers next door. Said someone told them *let me turn it over a little longer* while they were in the hall."

Weave's particles brightened — a quick flare of gold-green. Just light, pulled back.

Luna said, even. "Sorry about that. Echo had a meltdown, and Weave was a little loud about it. We're settled now."

Collins waited a breath before she moved the vitals cart back by inches. The wheel caught once on the floor seam — a soft plastic bump that made Echo's thumb stop again on Riley's hand.

"Call if she opens her eyes again, tracks movement, squeezes, or if her breathing changes," Dr. Brown said. He did not add anything brighter. He did not take back the three to four days.

The door closed. Not loud. Just metal settling into metal. It cut off the staff voices. The monitor stayed steady. Echo's hand was still on Riley's. Three to four days. Echo's fingers tightened on Riley's hand.

Echo's grip loosened. Not far. Her fingers shook, caught, and closed again. The chair took more of her weight. Her shoulders folded. "She didn't squeeze. I thought—" Her breath caught. "Three more days? I can't— I can't do three more if she keeps not—"

Luna set her hand on Echo's back and left it there. She didn't speak.

I stayed at the window. The monitor still steady. The light on the floor had shifted some.

When Echo's breathing scraped less, Luna's thumb moved once. Still no words.

Weave's voice came through in the back of my mind. Careful.

__There is one deeper option.__ __Layer 1 would not read her mind.__ __It would knock and ask permission.__ __If Riley refuses, shows distress, or gives no stable permission, I stop.__ __I am asking the room to witness this with me.__ __I am asking because I am afraid.__

Echo's head came up a little. "Ask her. Not us. Her."

Luna's thumb moved once against Echo's back. "Riley first." Then, lower: "I'm scared too, Echo. I'm not going to pretend I'm not. But if Weave can ask and Riley can answer, that's more than she has right now."

"You're scared," Echo said, not quite a question.

"Yeah. I'm scared. I'm still here."

I was still at the window, hand near the sill. The monitor didn't matter anymore — I was watching Weave.

"Can you stop fast?"

__Yes.__ __If the answer is absent, unstable, or no, I withdraw.__

"What if her vitals drop while you're in?" I forced the words out. "What if the monitor goes wrong and we don't know if it is you or her?"

The particles tightened. She heard what I was really asking — not suspicion. Fear.

__I would not cause a change in her heart rate or respiration through a knock alone.__ __If the monitor shifts for reasons unrelated to my contact, I will withdraw immediately.__ __If you call stop, I stop.__

I held the answer for a second and then nodded once. "Reasonable."

Then, quieter: "That's more fear than I have heard from you."

__Yes.__

She didn't defend it or soften it.

Marisol said from the threshold, "And if staff come back, I move first. No one crowds the bed. No one explains over Riley. If Collins or Dr. Brown step in while Weave is working, I brief them before they come through. The room stays open. Staff don't come in blind — not on my door."

Nobody questioned it.

Echo's hand shifted on Riley's. Her thumb dragged once over Riley's knuckles and found no answer — but the motion itself steadied something. "Don't scare her," she said, smaller. "If she is in there and she says no or she can't answer or she doesn't understand what you are asking... don't make her stay because we're scared. Don't make her feel like she hurt us by not being ready."

The particles dimmed once. Inward.

__I will not hold her.__ __I will ask.__ __Only ask.__ __I will stop at the first sign of refusal, distress, or absence.__ __She will not carry the cost of my fear.__

For a long moment, nobody filled the silence.

Then Echo breathed out, and the breath did not catch. Her hand stayed on Riley's — looser now. The monitor kept its rhythm.

Luna's hand stayed on Echo's back. Marisol eased the door open a handspan — not enough to let the hall in, only enough to keep the gap visible. Weave gathered at the edge of the bed, waiting.

Nobody spoke. The room was waiting for Weave to begin.

Weave's particles drew inward. Not the tight snap of the barrier from earlier, and not the bright defensive ring of the exam. Smaller. She pulled every edge of herself into a shape I had not seen before — a narrow, steady presence at the crown of Riley's head, gold-green and very still.

Echo's hand tightened on Riley's without meaning to. "Weave?"

No answer came that the rest of us could hear. Echo's mouth opened and then closed. She knew better than to ask twice.

Echo's thumb stopped moving on Riley's knuckles and then started again, smaller, slower. Luna's hand pressed once between her shoulders and stayed.

Echo's eyes were on the glow above the bed. She could not hear Weave, but she could read stillness. "She's in there," Echo said, her voice thin.

The particles settled into a new shape at the crown of Riley's head — not brighter, not larger, just steadier.

Weave's voice came through in the back of my mind. Two words.

__Contact established.__

The quiet held.

I looked at the monitor first — the green line was tracing the same rhythm, unchanged. Then at Weave's particle ring at the crown of Riley's head. Then back to the monitor.

Echo put her head down on the blanket. "Okay," she said into the blanket. "Okay, Riley. Okay."

Luna's hand stayed where it was between Echo's shoulders. Marisol eased the door back to its handspan and let the quiet hold.

The monitor stayed steady.
