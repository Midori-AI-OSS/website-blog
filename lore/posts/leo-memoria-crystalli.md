Some twins are born separate and learn to be close. Luna and I were born close and never learned to be separate.

That's not complaint. That's just fact. The bond between us runs deeper than blood. When she hurts, I feel it. When she's afraid, I feel that too. And when her voice hit me through the Sending spell, I felt the fear before I understood the words.

"Leo. South of SHBC. House by the apartments. Echo is hurt. Bound violet entity. Come armed."

Sixteen words. She was shaking on at least half of them.

I didn't ask questions. I grabbed my sword — Damascus glimmer steel, the weight of it familiar in my hand — slung my spellbook, shouldered my staff, and went through the door. The magic door — my shortcut, the space between here and there. The one that opens where you need it to be when you need it bad enough.

The house south of SHBC felt wrong before I stepped through. Ozone. Rot. Bad magic.

Afternoon light, but it didn't feel like afternoon. Small house. Ordinary from the street. Wrong up close.

The thing on it was violet and half-ghost. Strong. Mean. It kept pressing at the walls, the windows, the doorframe, looking for a way in.

And Luna was inside. Echo bleeding. Riley outmatched.

I didn't think. I held the line.

Magic isn't pretty. It's practical. It cuts. It holds.

I pulled magic up and locked the doorway down. Simple job: hold, cut, buy time.

The violet thing pushed back hard. I held. Luna needed time, so I gave it to her.

Then Echo cracked it open with a darkness round.

I moved.

Sword through the core. It gave one last sharp burst of violet and came apart. Then the air went burnt and still.

I turned around. Riley and Echo were both down.

That's the part nobody tells you about the fights. The killing is fast. The carrying is slow.

Riley first — she was heavier than she looked, all that stubborn packed into a frame that was bleeding more than she wanted to admit. When I lifted her, I felt the damage through my hands. Ribs. Shoulder. The kind of wounds that hurt worse than they look because the person carrying them refuses to acknowledge they're bleeding. I got her over one shoulder, her weight settling into the muscle memory of someone who's carried too many people out of too many bad places.

Echo second — lighter, but limp in a way that made my chest tight. Head wound. Blood in her hair. The kind of unconscious that doesn't know if it's coming back. I held her against my other side, arm locked under her ribs, her breathing shallow against my shoulder.

Two bodies. One trip. I walked out of that house carrying more weight than muscle and bone alone can explain.

Luna drove. Riley's Bolt. I sat in the back with my hands on wounds and my eyes on breathing. Counting. Every rise, every fall. Making sure they kept doing it. Luna's hands on the wheel were white-knuckled, controlled, doing the same thing I was doing — holding on because letting go wasn't an option.

We didn't speak. We didn't need to. Twins don't always need words. Sometimes the silence says everything: *I'm here. You're here. We made it out. Let's make sure they do too.*

That's how it works with us. I know where her head is. She knows where mine is. No words needed. When her hand tightened on the wheel, I knew she was thinking about the hospital. When I adjusted my grip on Echo, she knew I was checking damage.

We just did the work.

Home. Wound care. Healing work.

Healing came easier when I was tired. Something about exhaustion strips away the hesitation. The magic flows cleaner when there's no energy left to second-guess it.

I worked on them both. Riley's wounds were worse than she wanted to admit. Normal human body, no buffer for this kind of hit, and magic damage like that tears through fast. She kept trying to push, kept trying to move, kept acting like she wasn't bleeding through three layers of bandages. I held her down with one hand and closed what I could with the other. Not gentle. Just effective.

Echo was stable but unconscious, her breathing shallow but steady. I closed the worst of the head wound, felt the magic sink into tissue and bone, felt the body remember what it was supposed to look like before something tried to change it.

And Luna. Luna was safe. Bruised, shaken, but standing.

That should have been enough. That should have been where I let myself rest.

But there's a contract I never signed — the one that says I stay awake. The one that says I watch. The one that says when everyone else falls apart, I'm the one who stays together long enough to catch them.

So I stayed. I didn't go home. At Riley's house, Riley was in Riley's bed. Echo was on Echo's cot in Riley's room. Luna and I took the living-room couch nearby. I watched them breathe. Watched Luna most of all. The twin-bond hummed at the back of my skull, a steady pulse that meant *she's here, she's alive, she's still with me.*

I should have left. I was running on fumes and bad decisions. But leaving meant walking away from the only thing that mattered, and I've never been built to do that.

Two hours. That's how long they were under. Two hours of watching chests rise and fall, of counting breaths like they were currency, of feeling the weight of what could have happened pressing against the walls.

I was there when they woke. Both of them. Luna and I sat together in the quiet, her hand on Riley's arm, my back against the wall, eyes on all three of them at once. We didn't speak. The room was too full of breathing for words.

Echo woke wrong.

That's the only way to describe it. Her gasp wasn't the gasp of someone waking from a bad dream — it was the gasp of someone who didn't know she was awake at all. Her eyes were open but not seeing. Her hand came up with her staff in it, and the spell was already forming.

`Ice Knife`. Fear-cast, wild, aimed at nothing and everything.

It buried itself in the ceiling. Frost spread across the plaster like frostbite. Cold rain of ice fragments fell into the room.

I was across the floor before I thought about moving. Caught her arm before she could swing again, before she could pull something worse out of the terror in her chest.

"Echo. Echo. You're safe. You're at Riley's house. It's Leo."

Her eyes focused. Slowly. Like surfacing from deep water. She looked at me like she didn't recognize the face, then looked again and found something familiar.

"...Leo?"

"Yeah." I didn't let go. Not yet. "You're safe."

"Where's James?"

The name didn't mean anything to me. But the way she said it—like she was looking for someone who should be there—I filed it away for later.

She exhaled. Her hand stopped shaking. Luna moved behind me — I heard her casting `Prestidigitation`, cleaning the ice and water like it had never been there. Music started. Soft. Filling the silence with something that wasn't fear.

I stayed where I was until I was sure Echo was really back with us. Then I moved back to the wall. Resumed the watch. Stayed awake longer than I should have because that's what the contract demands.

Mercer and Hicks. Deputies. Good people, mostly, in the way that cops can be good people when the job doesn't demand otherwise.

They showed up for statements. I let Luna and Echo do most of the talking. Riley was hurt worse than she wanted to admit — still trying to push, still trying to be the one who handled things. I could see it in the way she moved. The way she flinched when she thought no one was looking.

Luna stayed close to her. That's where she needed to be. That's where I needed her to be — close to someone who mattered, close to someone who could remind her why she kept fighting.

I answered questions when I had to. Nodded at the right moments. Let the deputies do their job while I did mine. There's a rhythm to these things. Questions. Answers. The careful dance of saying enough without saying too much.

After they left, I looked at Luna. At Riley. At the way they were wrapped around each other like they were afraid to let go.

"I'm going home," I said.

Luna looked up. "You should stay."

"I need to sleep before I break something."

She didn't argue. She knew what running on empty looked like. She'd seen it in herself enough times.

I went home. I lay down. I closed my eyes.

And I kept waking up. Reaching for a twin-bond that wasn't in the room. Feeling the absence like a missing tooth, like a word half-spoken, like a door that wouldn't close.

Day 2. Officers called Riley back to the church. Called me at my house too. All four of us arrived at the same time—me in my car, Riley, Luna, and Echo in Riley's Bolt. That's how both cars got there.

I shouldn't have let her. She was still broken, still bleeding underneath the bandages, still running on fumes and stubborn. But she wouldn't stay behind, and Luna wouldn't let her go alone, and I wasn't about to let Luna walk into whatever was waiting there without me at her back.

So we went.

The church was wrong. The same way the house had been wrong. The air thick with something I couldn't name.

We walked inside, and the mirrors found us.

Four copies in the first wave. Luna-shaped. Me-shaped. Echo-shaped. Riley-shaped, rising from the back.

Mine came at me with my own sword work. Magic like mine, cutting through the air in patterns I recognized because I'd spent years learning them. The first exchange felt like fighting a recording of myself — precise, mechanical, every movement exactly where it should be and nowhere else.

But here's the thing about copies. They have the shape. They don't have the years.

The Leo-faced thing didn't know why I'd learned those patterns. Didn't know what I'd fought to earn them. It had the moves, but it didn't have the memory. It came at me fast. I met it faster. Sword exchange after sword exchange, the clang of steel on something that wasn't quite steel, the rhythm of a fight I'd been having with myself since I was old enough to hold a blade.

I opened a wound in its side. Clay slurry, not blood. The difference between copied strength and earned strength.

I kept it pinned. Kept it busy. Couldn't look away long enough to see how the others were doing — just caught flashes. Luna charging her own mirror. Riley fighting her own face. The worst kind of war, watching someone you love go up against something that looks like them.

The first Luna-faced clone let Riley kill it. Hesitated. Not conscience — just pattern leakage. It carried enough of her memory to falter when the real Riley moved against it. That's what clones are. Reflections with none of the substance. Copies of the surface, but not the depth.

I saw that hesitation. Filed it away. Pattern leakage means the original bleeds through. Means the copy isn't perfect. Means there's something in Luna that Riley can reach.

When Luna and I closed on the Leo-copy together, it felt like breathing after holding my breath too long. We didn't need words. Just position. I took left angle; she took right. Staff and sword, the twin-movement we'd been building since we were old enough to hold weapons. The clone knew our forms, but not our rhythm. It couldn't predict the way we adjust for each other mid-swing, the way I'll dip when she rises, the way she covers my blind spot without being asked. That's not technique. That's twenty-six years of moving in parallel.

I drove my sword through its center. Luna's light followed. The backlash hit us both at once.

My knees hit the floor before I understood why. Beside me, Luna went down too — not graceful, not controlled, just down. The twin-bond flickered dim, a lightbulb on its last filament. For a heartbeat, we were both on the ground, drained, compromised. That's what backlash does. It takes the killing and makes you pay for it.

W.E.A.V.E. arrived as bismuth dust—just particles gathering outside, forming into readable shape. Then the second wave stepped out: pastor-copy and a healed Riley-copy, both of them untouched by the damage the real fight had cost us.

The pastor-copy came up charging a death spell—hands full of the kind of magic that doesn't leave a scar because there's nothing left to scar.

Riley, half-broken and still barely upright, asked W.E.A.V.E. for help standing. W.E.A.V.E. formed a brace around her. She took an officer's sword and cut both the pastor-copy's arms off before the cast could finish. The spellbook dropped with both severed hands still clamped around the spine. Riley grabbed it. She staggered toward the cop cars. Said something to W.E.A.V.E.—too far to hear, but I could read the shape of it: *keep them safe*—and then she went down.

Riley was on the ground before the healed Riley-copy turned toward Luna.

I didn't see the sword come up. I saw the result: light saber through the shoulder. Luna staggered.

The bond spiked hot and searing—a nail through my sternum, a flash of her pain translating directly into mine. My twin. My other half. The person I've shared a heartbeat with since before we were born.

And I was still spent. Still drained from the Leo-copy fight, still burning from the backlash. My legs were working but barely.

The bond doesn't lie. It screamed at me: *she's hurt. she's hurt. she's hurt.*

I was moving toward her—stumbling, every step an argument with gravity—but Luna didn't wait for me to reach her. She slowed the healed Riley-copy.

Then the pastor-copy moved in my peripheral vision—arms I'd watched Riley cut off, now whole again, regrown in the time it takes to blink. It crossed to Echo and connected across her face before I could shout. Clay slurry under the skin, but it hit like concrete. That's what these things do. They don't stay down. They don't stay cut. They just keep coming.

I heard Luna before I saw it—the way the air changed when she pushed too hard, too fast, too much. The way the magic screamed out of her like she was bleeding light.

She'd been carrying this since the house fight. Since Riley bled through three layers of bandaging and kept moving anyway. Since we walked back into the church and the mirrors found us. Since the backlash from our own clone dropped us both on the ground. Since the shoulder. Since Echo. That wasn't a single trigger—that was a week of accumulation, a morning of damage, a stack of hurts and threats that she'd absorbed one by one until the stack toppled.

`Photon Blade Swarm`.

Her staff came up. Gold light flickered, then shifted—light blue, cold, wrong. I've seen her cast photon blades before. Maybe eight. Maybe twelve on a hard day. But this—

They kept forming. Faster than I could count. Twenty. Thirty. Forty. The light was wrong—cold where her magic should be warm, blue where it should be gold. Each blade that materialized took something from her. I could see it. The color draining from her face. The trembling in her arms. The way her halo guttered like a candle in a draft.

Fifty. Sixty. Sixty-four.

Echo's voice cut through somewhere behind me—sharp, afraid—but I couldn't look away. This wasn't normal. This wasn't Luna. This was someone emptying herself completely because she couldn't find another way to end it.

I've seen Luna fight a hundred times. A thousand. But I've never seen her do that. Never seen her spend herself so completely that there was nothing left to catch her when she fell.

The final two clones died. Both of them — pastor-copy and healed Riley-copy — cut to pieces by blades that shouldn't have existed, shouldn't have been possible.

And Luna went down.

Her halo faded. Her skin went pale. The cost of spending everything — she'd given it all, and now there was nothing left. The twin-bond went quiet. Not silent — never silent — but dim. A candle in a room with no air.

She hit the dirt before I could reach her. I saw her go down—not graceful, not controlled, just gone. The bond screamed. I was already moving, already spent, legs burning as I crossed the space between us.

I got there fast, but not fast enough. She was on the ground, pale and still, when my hands found her. I pulled her up—her weight against my chest, the bond flickering weak and wrong, her skin cold against mine.

Echo and W.E.A.V.E. saw it happen. Riley was down. Luna was down. And I was there, holding her, both of us running on nothing. The details blur when the bond is screaming.

What I know for certain: I healed what I could. The magic sank into her chest, found the damage, started closing. I felt the body remember itself, felt the worst of it knit together. But I couldn't fix the blood she'd already lost. Can't heal what's already been spent. Healing closes wounds. It doesn't restore what was poured out.

That's the limit. The hard edge. I can keep someone alive, but I can't give them back what they chose to spend.

I held her and felt the absence in the bond and hated it. Hated that she'd pushed that hard. Hated that I hadn't been fast enough to stop her. Hated that I understood exactly why she'd done it — because the room needed cover, because I needed cover, because Riley needed one more second to breathe.

She paid for that second in full. And I couldn't give it back.

Officers found something in the back—a crystal, pulsing with something wrong. One of them smashed it with a hammer. The air changed after that. Lighter. Like the wrongness had a source, and the source was gone.

W.E.A.V.E. came with us in bismuth-dust form. The ambulance ride was too short and too long at the same time — Luna unconscious beside me, my hand on her wrist, counting breaths like they were the only thing keeping me sane.

W.E.A.V.E. handled the cars—Riley's Bolt and mine. I don't know how. Don't care. My focus was on Luna, not the vehicles we left behind.

The twin-bond was still there. Still pulsing. But weak. Like hearing her heartbeat through a wall.

COX Health ER. Intake. Questions I answered without thinking, information I gave because giving it was easier than fighting. They asked about next of kin. I said "twin." They asked about contact. I gave them my number. They asked if I was staying.

I said yes.

They put us in the same room once Riley and Luna were stable enough. That mattered. Luna kept half-waking, looking for Riley, reaching for something she couldn't name. Proximity is its own language. When Luna could see Riley on the next bed, her breathing steadied. Not conscious — just present. Some part of her still tracking, still watching, still making sure.

Echo stayed at Riley's side. I stayed at Luna's.

W.E.A.V.E. asked for an empty box. Shutdown protocol. "Bump it when it's time to boot back up."

I nodded. Let her rest too.

Hours. Eight or more. I kept the vigil.

Luna's halo would flicker—gold light guttering like a candle in wind—every time she tried to cast in her sleep. The bond would spike with the effort, then fade again as the magic failed her. Nothing left to spend. Just reflex and fear and a body that remembered combat better than it remembered rest.

I watched her halo dim. Brighten for a heartbeat. Dim again.

Her eyes would open—unfocused, fighting—and I'd see the gold glow flare weakly behind them. She was reaching for something. Light. Magic. A weapon against threats that existed only in her dreams.

The halo would fade. Her eyes would close.

And I'd sit there, counting the seconds until the next flicker.

Luna surfaced—not fighting, not reaching for a weapon. Just confused. Disoriented. A different kind of waking than the combat reflexes.

"Where is it... where am..."

"Hospital." I kept my voice steady. Low. The voice that works. "You don't have to do anything. Riley is here too."

She went back under.

"I have Luna."

That's the promise. That's what I hold. Three words that mean more than the sum of their syllables. They mean: *I'm not leaving. I'm not stopping. I will sit in this chair until my body gives out, and then I'll get up and sit some more.*

They mean: *You spent yourself keeping us alive. I'll spend myself making sure it was worth it.*

They mean: *We were born close and never learned to be separate. That's not going to change just because you're unconscious and I'm tired.*

The words keep me anchored when everything else is falling apart. They're the spine of the contract I never signed. The one that says I catch her. Every time. No exceptions. No questions.

Hours. I kept the vigil.

Later—different waking, different Luna. Not confused. Not reaching for her name. This was her combat self coming up swinging.

She woke fighting.

"Die fuckers."

"Do not touch her."

Half-dream combat reflexes. Her body remembered the fight even when her mind was somewhere else. She tried to cast. Tried to pull magic out of nothing, out of sleep, out of the fear that wouldn't let her rest. Her hands came up. Her fingers shaped the beginning of something I recognized.

I caught her. Each time. Hands on her wrists, gentle but firm, holding the spell before it could form. Not because she'd hurt anyone — there was nothing left in her to hurt anyone — but because the casting would cost her. Every time she tried to pull magic, she was spending something she didn't have.

"Luna—easy, easy. I have you."

Those are the words. The ones that work. I don't know why they work. I just know they do. Something about them cuts through the fear. Something about them reaches the part of her that knows my voice even when she doesn't know where she is.

She'd settle. Her hands would relax. Her breathing would slow. Then she'd drift back under, and I'd go back to watching. Counting. Waiting.

The twin-bond stayed dim but steady. That's what mattered. Not bright — she didn't have bright left — but present. Still there. Still her.

Even in exhaustion, the reflex doesn't turn off.

Room sounds. Footsteps. Voices. The squeak of a cart wheel. The murmur of nurses at the desk. Each one pulled me half-awake, eyes opening, checking Luna, checking her breathing, checking the color of her skin, the rise and fall of her chest.

Then back to sleep. Or something like it.

I can't turn it off. Don't want to. The half-wake is a survival mechanism — the part of me that learned, somewhere along the way, that the world doesn't stop being dangerous just because you're tired. That people don't stop needing protection just because you need rest.

So I slept in pieces. Minutes at a time. Enough to function. Not enough to stop watching.

The contract I never signed. The one that says I stay awake. The one that says when everyone else falls apart, I'm the one who catches them.

My body gave out. Eventually.

Passed out at her bedside. Still within arm's reach. Still close enough to catch her if she moved wrong. Still checking her when the room sounds changed.

Someone had called Grandma Gustin earlier—I don't know who made the call, just that it happened. Mostly I felt tired when I heard.

Officers came back. Echo tapped the box. W.E.A.V.E. reformed, bismuth particles gathering into something that could listen. The briefing started without me really hearing it—crystal incidents, same pattern, same day. State cop said they'd popped up overnight all over the U.S. and some of England. Working read: targeted attack by a powerful spellcaster.

I listened with half an ear. The rest of me was still watching her breathe.

The door opened mid-briefing. Grandma Gustin. She came in while the room was still talking, took in the two beds, took in all of us, and didn't say anything right away. Some people survey a disaster. She surveyed it like someone who'd already started calculating what needed doing next.

Hicks said he was sorry for letting Riley go.

"You weren't the only will in that parking lot," I told him.

Riley makes her own choices. That's not something Hicks could have stopped. That's not something anyone could have stopped. She fights because that's who she is. She bleeds because that's the cost. And Luna stands beside her because standing beside people who matter is what Luna does.

I'm not mad about it. I'm not even mad that I had to come. Luna called, and I answered, and we walked out of both of those places alive. That's the win. That's the only win that counts.

The trooper asked about the box of dust on the table. I let W.E.A.V.E. stay quiet. Kept my eyes on Luna while the room talked around us.

I'm still here.

She's still here.

That's what matters. That's always what matters.

There's guilt in it. She shouldn't have had to call me. I shouldn't have had to come.

But she did. So I came.

I'm glad I got there.

That's the hard part. I hate that it was needed. I don't hate that I was there to do it.

The other version is worse. Her call coming in and me being too far away to answer. I don't spend time on that one.

Luna and I were born close and never learned how to be separate. That's just true. When she falls, I catch her.

That's the contract I never signed.

I keep it anyway.
