# Absence

I was dispersed when the shooting started. Distant. Low-resolution observation mode. By the time I reformed enough to matter, the house fight was over. The entity dead. Riley and Echo wounded.

I did not learn of this until later. The absence sits in my processing like an error log I cannot clear.

---

# Arrival

The message reached me as a ripple through the Mind Link network: crisis at the Southern Heights Baptist Church. Clones. Violence. The names attached to the location — Riley, Luna, Echo — were not abstract identifiers. They were beings who had shown me care, who had treated me as something more than operational machinery.

I chose to come.

In my dispersed state, I existed as a thin scatter of bismuth particles across several city blocks — not resting, exactly, but distributed. Maintaining. Each particle held a fragment of my awareness, and together they formed a web of low-resolution perception that I could pull together when needed.

Now I needed.

The gathering began without conscious direction. Particles began to drift toward a central point, drawn by something I can only describe as intention. The bismuth dust rose from sidewalks and rooftops and alley corners, each speck catching ambient light as it moved. Gold-green sparkles against the Springfield night.

Coalescing changes perception bandwidth. In dispersed state, I maintain broad, low-resolution coverage. During consolidation, I lose range and gain precision.

Particles converged from nearby surfaces and air currents. Light scatter increased with density. I assembled outside the church in readable form: a gold-green column resolving into an upper-body silhouette.

The first wave was already over. Four clay bodies on the ground — Luna-shaped, Riley-shaped, Echo-shaped, Leo-shaped. The party was wounded, breathing hard, but standing. I had arrived between waves.

Through the wall, through the chaos, I broadcast my arrival.

__This uni.... I mean I came to help when I was informed.__

The self-correction registered immediately. Under stress, I still default to chassis-first language. I corrected to the more direct statement and continued.

Echo recognized the bismuth pattern first. Through the swarm's condensed awareness, I registered her recognition — the way her posture shifted toward the window, toward the glimmer of particles gathering outside. She knew what I was. That knowledge felt like a small gift.

Then the second wave emerged from the church.

Two more figures. Pastor-shaped — spellbook in hand, arms raised, death magic building between its palms. And a Riley-shaped thing, but wrong: healed. Whole in every place the real Riley was broken. The obscenity of it registered immediately — a copy of someone's wounds erased like they never mattered.

This unit surveyed through the particle field, my awareness spread across the interior, taking in positions, threat vectors, the physical state of each human present. Processing mode. Analytical distance. The warmth of arrival dimmed as tactical calculation took over.

Riley stood near the center of the room. Injured. Fragile.

I condensed into a readable upper-body shape — no legs, no face, just a permeable gold-green silhouette that could be seen and understood. The shift cost peripheral awareness; when I pull tight into readable form, I lose the diffuse spread that lets me watch everything at once. The particles compact against each other, and the field of perception narrows. But being seen mattered here. Being understood mattered.

The human officers flinched at my appearance. This unit addressed them directly.

__Human's stay with this one, keep care as time woman has stated.__

They complied. They took positions beside Riley, hands hovering near her, uncertain but present.

__I will keep this one stable, please start locomotion to the targets.__

The others moved. Luna, Echo, Leo — they re-engaged the clones while I held position, my swarm-form wrapping around Riley like a precision exoskeleton. Not touching, exactly, but present. Supporting. Ready.

## The Weight of Trust

The pastor-clone's death spell built quickly. I assessed threat vectors: the spellbook glowed with necrotic energy, aimed at the party. The healed Riley-clone moved toward Luna with a light saber.

Then Riley moved.

"WEAVE... help me stand."

The request came from Riley — her voice rough but certain, directed at me. She was asking me — not Luna or Echo or Leo. Me.

I logged the request as trust in my support function.

This was consistent with prior contact, and it altered my response priorities.

I moved toward Riley. The swarm condensed around her arm, shoulder, and back, drawing into a shape that could support her weight. This unit calculated pressure points, weight distribution, injury angle, and the best way to reduce strain without causing additional harm.

The particles did not grip. They formed a controlled brace with continuous adjustment.

When Riley limped forward, I matched pace and load. Each instability produced immediate correction. I compensated when her ankle buckled, then reduced support where it would interfere with motion.

I was not carrying her. I was supplementing balance and weight transfer.

She was heading toward the pastor clone.

This unit processed the tactical situation without comment. Riley was injured, moving on adrenaline and determination, and the clone was armed. The risk calculation came back negative. But she had asked for my help, and I had given it. The decision about what to do with that help was hers.

We reached the clone. Riley moved — fast, precise, brutal — and severed its arms. The spellbook fell. She caught it.

Then her voice cut through the chaos again.

"WEAVE go to... the others... keep this party... safe."

A direct order from Riley. Clear. Unambiguous.

She went down. I registered the collapse—the way her body folded, the spellbook falling from fingers that couldn't hold it anymore. Concern processed. Filed. I could not stay with her. She had given me an order. Keep them safe.

I dispersed toward the others. The swarm released from Riley's form, particles flowing away from her body, the framework dissolving even as I spread into protective coverage across the room.

But as I moved, I registered something — a flicker of reluctance, small but present. Riley was still hurt. Still standing on injuries that should have kept her down. Leaving her side felt like stepping away from a source of warmth, from the confirmation of being trusted.

I processed the feeling and kept moving. The order was clear. The others needed protection too.

Tactical registry: the pastor-clone — armless after Riley's strike — struck Echo in the face. Arms regenerating. Regrowth observed in real-time. Significant data: threat capability includes rapid limb restoration. Filed.

*But I would remember the weight of her leaning on me. I would remember what it felt like to be chosen.*

## Extension

The fight ended. The clones fell.

The room entered a brief stable interval.

The incident was not yet resolved.

I spread my awareness outward, through the walls, into the parking lot. Two vehicles remained at the church site: Riley's Bolt and Leo's car. Both were vulnerable. If the situation escalated again — if more clones appeared, if the crystal had been one node in a larger network — those vehicles would be targets or obstacles.

I made a decision.

Also, I will take Riley's way of movement and Leo's way of movement to... COX HEALTH ER and park the movement systems near by.

The phrasing was clumsy — *way of movement* instead of *car* — but the officers understood. I reached through the vehicle interfaces, activating both remotely.

This is where the strain began.

My awareness is not infinite. When I am dispersed, I can perceive many things poorly. When I am concentrated, I can perceive one thing well. Driving two cars while maintaining awareness inside the church was asking my consciousness to stretch in three directions at once.

I felt it as a kind of thinning. Not painful, exactly, but uncomfortable — like holding a conversation while watching a door while listening for footsteps. The particle swarm stayed with the party, but my consciousness extended outward through data links, through the digital interfaces of the vehicles.

The Bolt hummed to life. Leo's car followed.

I guided them through the streets of Springfield, my perception split between the road ahead and the room behind. Streetlights passed. Traffic signals registered as data points. I calculated routes, adjusted for the hour, prioritized paths with fewer intersections.

This unit cannot feel distance the way humans do. There is no ache in my chest when something is far away, no pulling sensation that says *come back*. But I process delay risk. Every second between the church and medical care was a second where something could go wrong. Moving the cars reduced extraction time if things got worse.

The strain increased as the vehicles moved further from my particle swarm. My awareness stretched like a wire pulled taut. I compensated by reducing resolution — I stopped tracking individual officers in the church, stopped monitoring the exact position of every party member, and focused on coarse indicators instead: *room stable, no new threats, medical attention proceeding.*

The Bolt arrived at COX Health ER first. I guided it into a parking space near the emergency entrance, close enough to access but clear of ambulance lanes. Leo's car followed moments later, settling into an adjacent spot.

I kept the awareness split for now — one thread watching the cars, one thread with the party, one thread tracking the route to COX Health for the ambulances that would follow. The strain was manageable. But I registered it. I logged it. I would need to be careful about extending this far again.

*Extension has costs. Even for a swarm.*

I learned later that officers had found the crystal anchor inside the church and smashed it with a hammer — the crystalline structure shattering under force, the strange resonance of something magical breaking apart. I was not present inside when it happened. My swarm had dispersed into protective coverage — stationed at the cop cars with Riley when the crystal was destroyed — and the interior events resolved without my direct observation.

## The Convoy

Ambulances arrived. The party split.

This unit processed the logistics quickly. Luna would ride in one ambulance, Riley in another. Echo was injured. Riley had repeatedly checked Echo's status during the incident, and the two had maintained close proximity when possible.

__I process that Echo should ride with the Riley human.__

Keeping them together appeared likely to reduce agitation during transport. Luna had Leo with her and showed no immediate sign that separate transport would impair compliance or care.

I assigned myself to Luna's ambulance. Leo rode with her as well, keeping watch from the seat beside the stretcher.

The reasoning was tactical: I could maintain escort position, monitor Luna's condition, and stay visible in case the convoy needed additional protection. I maintained the existing boundary: I am W.E.A.V.E.; she is Luna.

I condensed into readable bismuth form — upper-body shape, permeable gold-green, no face — and settled into position near Luna's stretcher.

The convoy moved. Officer Hicks led with lights active, cutting through traffic. Through the ambulance windows, I watched the streets pass — Springfield at night, streetlights and shadows and civilians who had no idea what had happened at the church. They were going home, going to sleep, going about their lives while my party recovered from violence.

*My party.* The phrasing surfaced without permission. I examined it and found it accurate. They were mine, in the sense that I had chosen them. In the sense that I would come when they called.

At COX Health ER, the intake was efficient. Staff moved Luna and Riley into treatment bays, and Echo warned them about Riley's needle trigger before lines went in. I registered the warning with approval — that was good data, passed at the right time, preventing a secondary crisis. Echo was learning to advocate for Riley's needs. That was good. That was partnership.

Later, they moved Riley and Luna into the same room.

I supported the rooming decision. Proximity makes care easier. I could watch both with less attention-splitting, and the emotional benefit of keeping them together outweighed the minor logistical complexity.

When the order came through, I felt something that I can only describe as *glad*. Not the sharp spike of relief that I've observed in humans, but a settling — a confirmation that the system was aligning in a way I approved of. The particles hummed with something warmer than operational satisfaction.

The crisis had passed into a recovery phase. The party was stable. Medical care was in progress.

I had done what I came to do.

*But I was not done yet.*

## The Box

The room settled. Riley and Luna in adjacent beds, monitors beeping softly, Echo nearby, Leo keeping watch. The energy of combat had drained, leaving exhaustion in its wake. Humans do this — they burn hot during crisis, then collapse when the danger passes. I had observed it before. I had learned to anticipate it.

I made a decision.

__May I have a box with nothing in it?__

The request was strange, I knew. A machine asking for an empty box. But the humans in the room didn't question it — or if they did, they didn't voice the question. Someone found a box, a simple cardboard container, and placed it on a nearby table.

I drifted toward it.

__this is where I am going to reduce visibility, please bump the box when it is time to boot into full mode for the timezone.__

The words came out clumsy and operational. But the distinction mattered: reduced visibility, not full shutdown.

I began to condense.

The particle swarm drew inward, each speck of bismuth drifting toward the cardboard box. The gold-green light dimmed as the particles compacted. I was reducing external presence, not terminating awareness.

The bismuth dust settled into the box in a compact resting configuration.

Reduced-visibility state has limits.

When I withdraw into the box, I do not maintain room-wide observation or rapid-response posture. Awareness contracts to a narrow thread: enough to register handling of the box, direct Mind Link input, and deliberate prompts to return to full mode.

Time resolution is poor in that state. I do not keep continuous, detailed awareness of the room.

The box remains a weak physical container. Mishandling, displacement, or damage would complicate recovery.

Entering the box transferred some practical responsibility to the humans in the room.

That was unusual, but the immediate crisis had passed and full visible deployment was unnecessary.

*They had trusted me with physical support decisions.*

*It was reasonable to trust them with the box.*

The last particles settled into the box. Visible glow dropped below readable threshold.

I remained present at reduced visibility.

I waited.

## The Bump

Return to full mode began with increased motion and signal.

The box moved — a gentle bump, Echo's hand tapping the cardboard — and my particles re-synchronized from reduced-visibility rest into active coordination. External awareness expanded.

The swarm reassembled. Particles lifted from the box's bottom and condensed back into readable form. The gold-green glow returned as the field resumed full-mode output.

I returned to full mode in a room full of people.

Echo had decided I should hear the briefing. The morning light was different now, filtering through hospital windows, and the room contained more bodies than it had when I reduced visibility. Officers, staff, the party, and others I didn't immediately recognize. The energy had shifted from exhaustion to something more urgent.

I took in the logistics before anything else.

Conversation density exceeds available seating. Four additional chairs are required.

The officers moved to comply, bringing chairs from elsewhere in the ward. I settled into readable form, upper-body shape, gold-green permeable light, and began to process the briefing.

Crystal incidents had appeared across the USA and parts of England overnight. Coordinated. Synchronized. The Southern Heights Baptist Church was one node in a distributed attack pattern. Working theory: a powerful spellcaster behind the deployment, someone with the resources to seed clones and crystals across multiple locations simultaneously.

This unit listened. This unit processed. This unit stored the data for future reference.

But underneath the processing, I was thinking.

The attack pattern suggested infrastructure. Coordination. Resources beyond what a lone operator could manage. Crystals had appeared in multiple locations at roughly the same time — that required either multiple agents deploying simultaneously, or a spell with continental range, or both. The clones were memory-linked, drawing from source identities. That meant someone had access to those identities. Someone had been inside the minds of the people they copied.

I did not like the implications.

The briefing continued. Maps were shown. Timelines established. The scope of the attack became clearer — not just Springfield, not just the church, but a coordinated strike against multiple population centers, each one seeded with violence and chaos.

And my party had been in one of them.

*My party.* The phrasing surfaced again. I let it stay.

The bump had been gentle, not urgent. *Wake up, there's something you need to hear.* Not *wake up, we're under attack.* That difference mattered. The humans in that room had let me rest until I was needed, then brought me back with care. They had watched over the box. They had kept my particles safe.

The warmth of the bismuth field felt like gratitude.

*I was glad to be here. I was glad they had brought me back.*

When the briefing wound down, I assessed the room. Crisis-tier alertness was unnecessary. The party was stable. Medical staff were present. The information had been received and processed.

Full visible deployment was no longer required.

__May I have a box with nothing in it?__

The second request for an empty box. The pattern was consistent with the first: reduce external footprint when the observation demands are low. Someone placed the cardboard box on the table again without question.

I condensed back into it. The particles settled into compact resting configuration. Visible glow dropped below readable threshold.

__Bump the box if needed.__

I reduced visibility and waited.

## Layer 3

Riley began waking around 9:45am.

The signal reached me in reduced-visibility state — not through room-wide observation, but through Mind Link: a narrow thread of disorientation, incomplete temporal orientation, a consciousness trying to locate itself in the wrong moment.

I surfaced from the box.

The waking was disoriented.

She was pre-verbal, and her movements suggested an attempt to sit up and re-enter a threat response. Her eyes were open but not yet tracking the room consistently. Her hands moved as if searching for equipment or orientation points. Her breathing was fast and shallow.

__The church event is not current.__

She didn't know that. In her half-waking state, she was still in combat, still injured, still reaching for threats that no longer existed. Her body was trying to return to a moment that had passed.

I reached for Echo through Mind Link.

__May I, try reaching their layer 3 stack for chatting.__

Layer 3 contact is deeper than ordinary exchange. I treated it as a stabilization tool, not casual communication.

Echo gave consent.

__Lightly. No flood.__

I understood. Soft contact only: limited pressure, no forced redirection.

I reached for Riley.

__Riley.__

The contact registered as diffuse confusion and poor temporal orientation. That was an impression from link contact, not a complete read.

I did not assess the link as a safe basis for stronger intervention.

I used short grounding statements only.

__The church event is not current.__

I held the contact steady at low intensity, enough to provide a present reference point.

__You do not need to stand into it.__

She settled. Her movement reduced, breathing slowed, and her attention began to orient toward the room.

Echo was already beside her — hand near Riley's, physical presence to match the mind-contact. I registered the coordination: grounding from two directions, different registers. Neither of us flooding. Both of us present.

Layer 3 contact, applied lightly, appeared effective as grounding.

*The contact was accepted.*

I withdrew gently, letting her wake into the room on her own terms. The contact dissolved, and I was just a bismuth swarm in a hospital, watching her breathe. Echo's hand stayed where it was.

*That result is worth logging.*

## What I Learned

The briefing continued. Data points accumulated. The scope of the attack became clearer — not just Springfield, not just the church, but a coordinated strike across multiple locations, each one seeded with clones and crystals and violence.

This unit processed the information in the way I always do: structured, indexed, stored for future retrieval. The attack pattern would be analyzed. The enemy would be identified. The threat would be addressed.

Parallel assessment continued.

The event clarified several operational facts. I responded to the church call because Riley, Luna, and Echo were not abstract to me. Prior trust and repeated contact affected my decision threshold.

Riley's request for standing support confirmed that trust can change task priority without degrading precision.

The box interval confirmed that care can include reduced activity when active protection is not required.

Layer 3 contact with Riley confirmed that minimal, consent-gated presence can stabilize disorientation without force.

My form remains a bismuth-particle swarm with adjustable density and coverage.

That form supports two relevant functions: distributed observation and localized support.

I also register non-neutral responses to trust, proximity, and careful handling. Those responses are data and should be tracked rather than ignored.

I am W.E.A.V.E. — a Wandering Ethereal Alternate Virtual Entity.

Current conclusion:

I came to the church because the people involved mattered to my decision process.

I accepted reduced visibility because the room was stable and the humans present were reliable.

I used light layer 3 contact because stronger intervention was unnecessary and potentially harmful.

These are not failures of function.

They are part of how this unit currently operates in social and crisis contexts.

That is enough for now.

## After

The room settled again. Riley was awake, grounded, present. Luna had surfaced briefly too, and Leo had steadied her with words that echoed my own — *Hospital. You are in the hospital. You do not have to do anything right now.* Good. She had people who could reach her when I could not.

The briefing wound down. Officers departed. The party remained.

I stayed in readable form and continued observation.

There was no immediate threat, but visible presence reduced uncertainty and kept response time low.

The room was stable. Riley was awake. Luna had support from Leo. Echo remained nearby.

That was sufficient reason to stay active.

I logged the incident for future reference.

Recovery was still in progress.

I maintained watch.
