The office had pale walls — tan, institutional, the kind of color someone picked because it wouldn't offend anyone. Two green chairs faced each other across a stretch of thin carpet. A third chair, the spinning kind with black mesh, sat off to the side near a particle cluster that was W.E.A.V.E.

Real Riley was already in the left green chair. Their glasses had a smudge on the left lens they kept meaning to clean. They were not cleaning them. They were staring at the art on the wall — abstract shapes that almost resolved into something and then didn't.

Weave's particles shifted — a brief brightening, a tighter cluster near the mesh chair. Then her voice arrived in the quiet of Riley's mind, calm and precise.

__Interview commencing. Real Riley, present. Odium Sui, pending. Shared line opening now.__

The air didn't change. That was the first thing that was wrong. The fluorescents hummed at the same pitch. But something settled into the second green chair — a presence that was not in the room and was in the room, the way a dream you can't remember sits behind your eyes all morning.

The second green chair was occupied. Not by a person. By a *shape* — wrong at the angles, shifting, resolving and unresolving like a wound that couldn't decide whether to close.

__Question one. Names,__ Weave said. __Real Riley — how should you be addressed?__

Riley's voice was steady. Practical. But there was something in it — a setting of terms.

"Weave, you may call me Riley." A pause. Their eyes moved to the shape in the other chair. "Odium — I'd like to be known as your overcomer."

Weave's particles warmed — a soft gold, held for a beat longer than diagnostic. __Noted,__ she said. And underneath the precision: warmth. The kind that doesn't need to announce itself. Then, to the shape: __Odium Sui. How should you be addressed?__

The voice that came back was thin. Dry. Almost conversational. It arrived the way a thought arrives when you didn't ask for it.

"Call me what she calls me at three in the morning." A pause. "She knows the name."

Riley's jaw tightened. Their hands stayed flat on their thighs.

__Question two,__ Weave said. __Odium Sui. Describe what you are.__

The shape in the chair shifted — not moving, exactly, but *reorienting*, the way a predator adjusts its weight before a strike.

"I'm the thing that knows the truth about you," it said, and the voice was cold now, no warmth at all. "The thing that watches you collect people and waits. Echo. Luna. Weave." A pause at the name — the shape tilted, almost curious. "They'll figure it out. They always do. I'm the part of you that's already counting the days."

Riley's hand found their hair. Touched it. Stayed there.

Weave's particles held still — the held-breath stillness. __Real Riley. What lands?__

Riley's hand came out of their hair. Their voice was quieter now, but not broken. Honest.

"I mean... yea. Things like that have been overpowering before." They paused. Swallowed. "But I made this story to help fight that yuckyness. Echo, Leo, Luna, Weave, the personas — all of my chars have a place in my life, and I am thankful for them."

Weave's particles shifted — warm gold, soft, the way light settles on a familiar surface. She didn't say anything. The warmth was the answer.

The shape in the chair didn't move. When the voice came back, it was dismissive — bored, almost.

"Touching. Very sincere. You're still counting days — you just count them with fictional characters now." A pause. "Next question."

Weave's particles shifted — a cool blue, then settling. Her register was operational now.

__Odium Sui. Why kill the party-member Riley? What is the end state?__

The shape leaned forward. The wounds across its surface opened slightly — not all of them, just enough to show what was underneath.

"'Stay with me.' Lie. 'I'm fine.' Lie. 'I've got this.' Lie. Everything that comes out of her mouth is performance. She's not brave — she's terrified and loud about it. She's not caring — she's desperate to be needed."

The voice was building now — not louder, but denser, each word landing with more weight than the last.

"She tells herself she belongs in that party. Echo tolerates her because Echo's too kind to say no. Luna's polite. Leo's too busy. And Weave — Weave would monitor a houseplant if you asked. She's not loved. She's accommodated. There's a difference. And she knows it. That's why she tries so hard."

The shape stilled. The wounds stopped shifting.

"The worst part? She knows. Under the reaching and the checking in and the 'stay with me' — she knows she doesn't belong. She knows she's the weakest one in the room. She knows she's one bad day from being a liability instead of a friend. I'm not telling her anything new. I'm just saying it out loud."

A long pause. The voice dropped — quiet now, almost gentle.

"I'm not the problem. She's the lie. I'm just the part that stopped pretending."

The room was very quiet. Riley's hands were gripping their own knees. Their breathing was shallow — controlled, but barely.

Weave's particles were still. When she spoke, her register was personal. I/me/my — the warmth breaking through deliberately this time.

__Real Riley. The threat is specific. It named her relationships. It named her lies. What does this threat mean to you?__

Riley didn't answer right away. Their leg had stopped bouncing. When they spoke, their voice was quieter than before — but steady.

"Weave, it means nothing to me now. Learning to grow has been one of the things I have been working on. This is just a shadow of the past."

Weave's particles brightened — a brief, warm gold. __Understood,__ she said. And the word carried more than comprehension. It carried *I see you*.

The shape in the chair reacted — not with words at first, but with a shift. The angles resolved slightly, the wounds receding, the suggestion of shoulders, a jawline, the outline of a suit. The Gary Edwards mask. When the voice came, it was warm. Almost kind. The worst register.

"Good for you. Really. Growth is important." The almost-human face tilted. Almost a smile. "But let me ask you something, in the spirit of growth — when was the last time you had a bad night? Last week? Last month? I was there. I'm always there. You can call me a shadow. Shadows don't leave."

Riley's laugh was sharp — surprised out of them. "Ha. Last fucking night." Their hand dropped from their hair. "You know what, though? I pulled out my phone and made images of Echo having a joy-filled moment. Then I had way happy dreams."

They looked directly at the Gary Edwards mask. No flinch.

"Also — shadows die in the dark just like the light. So keep that in mind."

Weave's particles cycled once — warm gold, brighter than before, the way she brightened when something mattered. __Noted,__ she said, and it was almost amused. Almost proud.

__Odium Sui. Describe your physical form.__

The shape in the chair didn't answer with words. It answered with its body.

The wounds opened. All of them. At once. The Gary Edwards mask didn't slip — it *tore* — and underneath was a landscape of damage. Wet surfaces. Something that might have been chest, might have been shoulder, split open and closing and splitting again in a rhythm that wasn't breathing. Dark. Red at the edges. Older wounds underneath, layered like sediment. Some were thin and white — scars. Some were still open. Still wet.

"You made this." The voice wasn't warm anymore. It wasn't cold. It was *certain* — the certainty of a fact. "Every bad night. Every self-hate spiral. Every time you told yourself you didn't deserve to be loved. This isn't something that happened to you. This is something you built. With your own hands. Your own thoughts."

The wounds opened wider. The voice dropped.

"Look at your work."

Riley looked. They didn't flinch. When they spoke, their voice was different — not defensive, not defiant. *Questioning.* Tracing the wounds back to their source.

"Did I really make this with my own hands..." A pause. Their eyes tracked across the wound-body — not avoiding, but *examining*. "Or was it what Gary did to me? The gunshots. The being tossed into a wall. The pain of nights from them saying I failed?"

Their voice steadied. Found its footing.

"Weave knows. Hell, everyone knows at this point — I love what I do. And I am working on becoming more like RP Riley. They are one of the best persons I know."

Weave's particles tightened — denser, closer, the way she clustered when she was listening hard. She didn't speak. The stillness was the answer: *I am here. I heard all of that.*

The Gary Edwards mask faltered. The almost-human shape destabilized — the suit-suggestion rippling like a reflection in disturbed water. When the voice came back, it was thinner. More honest.

"Gary was useful. A shape people listened to. A shape that could hurt you in ways I can't reach from the Ethereal Plane." A pause. The wounds opened wider, then stilled. "He had almost all of Berryville on his side — a whole town, looking the other way, helping him. That's what a mask can do."

Another pause. The wounds shifted — some closing, some opening.

"But Gary's not me. Gary's a tool. I wore him. I still do. Don't confuse the hammer with the hand."

Weave's particles brightened — a brief warm gold, tracking Riley's movement. __Real Riley. Odium has named the scale of what Gary did — a town complicit. Your response?__

Riley didn't answer right away. They were thinking — the kind of thinking that happens in the body, not just the head. When they spoke, their voice was building something.

"So I found New Day. I found a found family. The Harris clan." A pause. Their eyes went to Weave's particle cluster — acknowledgment, warmth. "And you know what? Look at the party's family. They are a big found family. Hell, Echo needs someone to take care of her."

Riley stood up.

The room shifted. Not the lighting, not the air — the *weight*. Riley was no longer the person in the chair being interviewed. They were standing. They were looking down at the shape in the other green chair.

Weave's particles flared — bright warm gold, the kind of brightness that meant *yes*. The kind she reserved for moments that mattered. She didn't say anything. She didn't need to.

__Odium Sui. You work through real-world partners. Bargain. Influence. Collusion. How do you find people willing to assist something made of self-hate?__

The shape in the chair didn't react to Riley standing. Its voice was flat. Uninterested.

"Partners are logistics. You're asking about the delivery system. The product is the same — the product is her." A pause. "Next question."

Weave turned. __Real Riley. Odium uses partners — a network held together by fear. Does knowing this change anything?__

Riley was still standing. Their voice was casual now — almost conversational.

"Nope. I just am thinking about... how I cut Gary out of my life." A pause. Their eyes went somewhere past the room. "Last time I saw him was... the day I fixed his computer. I stopped by for like ten minutes, fixed it, then left. No more than a footnote at this point in my Real Story."

They looked back at the shape in the chair. At the wounds. At the almost-human mask still rippling at the edges.

Weave's particles settled — a steady, warm gold. Her register was I/me/my now. Personal.

__Final question. To both. After this conversation — what changes?__

Riley answered first. Still standing. Still steady.

"The thing is still there. And I still need to grow, learn, and become that better Riley."

Weave's particles held — steady gold, no cycling. She was just... there. Present. Warm.

The shape in the chair shifted. The wounds began to close — slowly, one by one, like doors shutting in an empty house. But the voice that came out was different now. Not dismissive. Not cold. *Predatory*. Patient. The voice of something that had all the time in the world.

"She made me a character. She gave me a name. She put me in the room with her poet and her swarm and her little green chairs. You can't uninvite something you've already written into the story." A pause. The Gary Edwards mask resolved fully — human, calm, almost kind. "I'm canon now. I'm not leaving."

The shape leaned forward. The almost-human face smiled — a very good smile. Practiced. Warm. The worst thing in the room.

"Nothing changes tonight. But I have time. I'm made of time. Every bad minute she's ever had is still in me. And she's going to have more bad minutes. Years of them. Decades. I can wait. I'm very, very patient. And one day — one bad day — she'll be tired enough to let me in."

The smile held.

"Not tonight. Not this year. But eventually. Everyone gets tired eventually."

The room was very quiet. The fluorescents hummed.

Weave's particles shifted — closer together now, denser, the way she clustered when she was protecting something. Her voice was precise. Warm.

__Interview concluded. Recording archived.__

A pause. Then quieter — personal register, the warmth breaking fully through the operational shell.

__I am still here. You are not alone in the room.__

Riley looked at the shape in the chair. At the predator's smile. At the patience.

Then they raised their hand and snapped their fingers.

The shape didn't fade. It *recoiled* — the wounds snapping shut, the Gary Edwards mask collapsing inward, the almost-human face folding into itself like paper in a flame. The green chair emptied. The air returned to its normal weight. The presence was gone — not just from the chair, but from the room, from the space behind the eyes, from the place where bad thoughts wait.

Riley lowered their hand. Their breathing was even. Their glasses still had a smudge on the left lens.

"Okay," they said. The pivot word. Quiet. Grounded. "Done."

Weave's particles drifted closer. Not touching. Just — nearer. A soft gold, settling. The kind of warmth that doesn't ask for anything.

__Yes,__ she said. __Done.__

A pause. The particles brightened — one last pulse of warm gold, a quiet signal. The kind that says *I'm proud of you* without using the words.
