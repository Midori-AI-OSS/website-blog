Two lights went out around the bend.

Not headlights. Not porch lamps. Not anything a man sees with eyes.

Pieces.

Mine.

Forty-odd little works were moving that night, some close to the big work, some running other roads, some wearing faces good enough to make folks trust the wrong breath. You do not listen close to each one when you got that many. You do not get the scrape of boots or the smell of blood or the fine little pleasure of a throat closing under a copied hand.

You get enough.

Alive.

Alive.

Gone.

I waited.

Sometimes a piece dips low and comes back. Sometimes the line thins under pressure. Sometimes good work gets buried under noise and answers late.

Nothing.

Then the other good one went out too.

Those were not throwaway bodies. Not door-knockers. Not road-trash meant to test a ward and die for the knowing. Those two were shaped proper. Best I had set on that road.

I looked toward Highway 62 and clicked my tongue against my teeth.

“Well, ain't that something.”

A man ought not get sentimental over tools. Still, when good work quits answering, a man ought to go see what kind of teeth the board grew while he had his back turned.

So I stepped.

The dark folded tight around my ribs, mean as wire pulled through a fist, and let me out beyond the headlights near the turn.

Pine smell. Hot brakes. Torn road. Blood. Burnt spellwork. Fear trying to sit still and pass itself off as discipline.

The party was bunched around the bend by Thorncrown, all crooked vehicles and bad footing and people too hurt to know they were done. State boys and local law stood spread out like badges meant something if you held them hard enough. My best pieces were gone quiet somewhere behind all that mess.

That irritated me.

Then I saw the little wounded woman.

Blue in her hair. Blood through the front of her shirt. Face pale and tight, the way folks get when they are spending more body than they own. She stood in the road like standing had been decided by committee and her legs had lost the vote.

Gold-green glitter held around her.

Not a body. Not wings. Not a person. Just a pretty little shimmer under her hands and around her ribs, setting braces where meat had failed. Some summon-looking house trick, keeping her upright when any honest wound should have put her down.

I disliked her for that before she even opened her mouth.

Off to one side, a woman clutched a silver staff with both hands. Throat wrong. Body wrong. Face wrong in a way that said something had got into her and left fingerprints. She held that staff like it was the last fencepost in a flood.

Near her stood a smoke-winged woman, steady in a way that did not match the wreck around her. Dark wings behind her. Glass pins in her hair catching stray light. Wing-blood, or something close enough to stink the same.

Two green-haired ones were still upright too. Same bloodline in the face. Hurt. Worn thin.

No rings yet.

No shine yet.

Just people.

For the moment.

I started clapping.

Once.

Then again.

Slow enough to make them look.

Folks always look. Fear is polite that way.

I stepped into the headlights still clapping.

“Good job,” I said. I made it warm. Warm is worse when it ain't earned. “Those were my best clones yet. Be a shame not to use the others too.”

Nobody answered.

That told me plenty.

Scared people talk. Brave people talk louder. Tired people talk because silence leaves them alone with the shaking.

This bunch had no spare breath.

Then the blue-haired woman cut through it anyway.

“Maybe we try something better. One-on-one. Me and you.”

I looked her over slow.

Not checking for weapons. Not really. She had a sword. She had a bag. She had glitter holding her up like a mean little miracle. Blood darkened the front of her shirt bad enough to make the guess easy.

I had not seen her back.

Did not need to.

A wound that wet in front usually has a back door somewhere, and even if it did not, saying it like fact would make her wonder. Make the others wonder. Make everybody in earshot picture the part of herself she could not look at.

“Well now.” I let the words stretch. “That's... real cute.”

I took one step closer. Casual. Folks hate casual when the road is full of dead things.

“Little thing like you. All tore up. Barely standin'. Got blood soakin' through your front and your back both.” I let my mouth twist. “And you want to go one-on-one?”

Her face did not give me enough.

That bothered me.

So I laughed.

Deep. Long enough that the joke stopped being what she said and started being her.

“Alright,” I said. “Sure. Why not. Been a while since I got to play with my food.”

She did not answer me.

She turned her head toward the green-haired pair.

“Luna. Leo. Please keep us locked together.”

Names, then. Little labels she handed them. Not mine.

They moved.

The one with the book pulled gold out of the pages. The other took a staff left-handed and answered with dark threads, smoke-like and cold-looking. Gold and shadow reached for each other. Not fighting. Twining.

Then the rings came.

Thin gold over both their heads. Their eyes caught it too, lit from inside like the world had put candles behind the meat.

Halo-blood.

Of course.

I felt my lip curl before I bothered stopping it.

Pretty rings over their heads like that made them less dirt-bound. Like blood touched by sky was not still blood. I had known plenty of shining-blood folk. Soft voices. Sad eyes. That look like they were already forgiving you for what they were fixing to do.

Give them a ring and they think the road should part.

Give them wings and they think the air belongs to their family.

The smoke-winged one fit right in with them. Wing-blood was worse in some ways. Halo-blood wanted you to look up. Wing-blood wanted you to step aside.

Pretty rings. Pretty wings. Same rot underneath.

The road turned black around us.

Glass, or something close enough to glass to be rude. Smoked. Thick. Walls rising where open road had been. A ceiling closing overhead. The hum of joined spellwork settled into my teeth.

For half a second, I liked it.

The little wounded thing had shut herself in with me.

Then I felt what else the box had done.

The staff-clutcher was outside. The winged woman was outside. The halo-blood pair were outside, holding the walls where I could not put hands on them. Cops outside. Cars outside. All that leverage, all those soft places, set just beyond the pane where they could watch and not be used.

Clever.

Still stupid.

But clever.

Then the staff-clutcher moved.

She should have been back by the book-caster. She had been there, hunched around that silver staff like pain had tied her to it.

The staff-tip flashed.

Once.

Pale and sharp.

Then she was at the glass.

Just there.

Pressed to the pane with both palms flat, the silver staff still caught in her arm. Distance cheated. Some staff trick. Some little blink. Some desperate bit of shine pulled through a body that had no business moving that fast.

It cost her. I could see that even through the smoked wall. Standing looked like a bill she had not meant to pay yet.

Good.

Let it collect.

Inside the box, the blue-haired woman turned her mouth toward the glitter by her shoulder.

I could not hear her. Might have been one word. Might have been two. Might have been a name, if a body was foolish enough to name dust.

The glitter answered like it understood.

A ribbon of it slipped toward her hand.

Then her hand went into the bag.

Careful.

Not the sword. Not yet.

Inner pocket.

Folks do not reach careful for nothing.

When her hand came back, it held a small sealed case.

Only then did I know there was a case.

Compact. Opaque. Held like it had rules.

Outside the glass, the smoke-winged woman went still when she saw it. Wings tucked tighter. Face sharpened. The kind of look a person gets when they know enough to be afraid and not enough to stop it.

The glitter wrapped around the case and lifted it. Held it steady. Pretty little servant with no hands.

Then the sword came out.

Dark patterned steel. Plain-looking until it was not. Practical in shape. Working blade. The kind a person trusts because it does not look like it needs trust.

The edge changed.

Not light. Not dark.

Wrong.

The air along it cracked into thin pale lines, prismatic and mean, refusing to sit where sight put them. Like the blade had found seams in the room and started worrying at them.

The case opened.

Inside sat a little white thing with red along the edges.

Small.

Mean-looking.

Like a mint made by somebody who hated supper.

A charge, maybe. A load. Ammunition for that ugly-edged sword. Some Stillglass house trick too small to deserve the care she gave it, which meant it deserved exactly that.

She lifted it where I could see.

Something in my mouth went wrong before I understood what.

“What—”

I stopped.

Started again.

“What is that?”

The last word landed wrong.

Early.

Late.

Could not tell which, and the not-telling was worse than the mistake.

My mouth felt like it had moved before I gave it leave.

The little thing saw me notice.

“Stillglass special,” she said.

Her words were not clean either. She had to drag each one up, check it, and throw it.

“It's really picky. Likes farm kids and 'little things.'”

That one had teeth.

I tried to answer.

Had something ready. Something mean. Something that would put her back where she belonged.

My lips moved.

A syllable came out.

Maybe half a word.

Then nothing.

My mouth closed on air.

I stared at her.

Then at the white charge.

Then at my hands.

No.

I lifted my hand to cast.

That was supposed to be easy. Wrist. Fingers. Focus. Let the belt take the route. Let the spell find the groove.

Nothing.

My fingers shaped empty air.

The bull at my belt sat heavy. Its gem eyes should have warmed. Should have caught. Should have answered.

Nothing.

I tried again.

A pale thread lit at my knuckles and died.

So I went simpler.

No pretty work. No layered shape. Just force. A shove through air. Raw as a boot in a chest.

That one fired.

It hit her.

The blue-haired woman staggered half a step, breath knocked out, feet scraping asphalt.

There.

Then the hit happened again.

Or remembered itself.

Her body ran the same half-second more than once. Impact. Stagger. Recovery. Impact. Stagger. Recovery. Sound came late. My hand was down and still raised. The spell had fired and was firing and had not fired yet.

I shifted my feet wider.

“No,” I said. “No, that's—”

The next word fell out of the world.

The staff-clutcher at the glass made a sound.

“Ri—”

Barely anything. Half-air. Broken raw. The kind of sound a crushed throat makes when the person using it is too stubborn to be sensible.

The winged woman caught her elbow. Held her up. Said something low I could not hear through the pane.

The blue-haired woman used it.

I saw her use it.

She steadied off that broken little sound like it was a handhold.

That made me hate the staff-clutcher too.

All these little pieces outside the glass, trying to loan her spine.

The blue-haired woman checked her hands.

Left. White charge.

Right. Sword.

Feet.

Me.

She was doing herself in pieces.

Good.

Let her break in pieces too.

Then she said, “Still here.”

To herself. To the staff-clutcher. To the glitter. Did not matter.

Still here.

Like that meant anything.

I laughed.

It did not come out like before.

Too high. Too tight. It cracked in the middle and came back ugly.

“You think—”

The sentence collapsed.

My hand came up to cast again. To threaten. To do anything that put the world back where it belonged.

It stopped halfway.

My arm had moved.

My trust in the arm had not followed.

I tried anyway.

Spark.

Nothing.

Again.

The pale thread bit my palm and left a little burn.

Again.

Not even spark.

The bull at my belt had gone cold.

That was when anger changed shape.

Not fear yet. A man can get angry before he gets afraid. Gives him somewhere to stand.

The blue-haired woman took a breath.

“Fight's not over,” she said.

Then stopped.

Her eyes shifted, not away from me but through the moment, like she had misplaced the end of her own mouth.

“Fight's... not over.” Slower. “But you're going to hate—”

The word failed her.

I saw that.

Enjoyed it.

Then she found the piece.

“—the rest of it.”

I wanted my hand around her throat.

Not quick. Not clean. Not yet.

I wanted to feel all that borrowed courage turn back into body. Everybody turns back into body eventually. Halo-blood. Wing-blood. Staff-clutching little loyal things. Blue-haired road trash with glitter holding her ribs in place.

Everybody.

My magic sparked again and failed again.

The burn on my palm looked small.

I hated that too.

Then I moved.

Not a spell.

Spells were being contrary.

A lunge.

If the white thing was a load, I would unload it. If it was a charge, I would take it. If it was some Stillglass trick, I would break the hand holding it and see how special it looked in the dirt.

The glitter moved.

Not like a wall.

Worse.

It caught me.

The gold-green dust thickened between us, and the air went heavy as creek water in flood. My feet lost their say in the matter. My chest hit pressure that was not pressure. For a sick little breath, it felt like the sparkle picked me up by the ribs and shoved me back without ever making hands.

I snarled.

My reach slowed.

The white charge stayed just beyond my fingers.

Almost.

Right there.

Gone.

The staff-clutcher hit the glass.

Not tapped. Hit.

Her whole body came forward before sense caught up. Palm, fist, shoulder maybe. The pane shivered with the impact. Her mouth opened around the blue-haired woman’s name and tore itself bloody trying to throw it through the box.

“RILEY!”

There was the name, then.

The glitter held me off.

The blue-haired thing did not even have to step back.

“The crystal stays with—”

She stopped.

Checked her own mouth.

“—with me.”

Her breath hitched. She was paying for every word.

Good.

“You're going to—”

Gone again.

I bared my teeth.

Then she found it.

“The crystal. Stays. With me. You're going to have to earn it.”

I snarled.

No words.

Just sound.

The man who had walked into the headlights clapping was not the thing standing there anymore.

Fine.

Some fights are won with manners. Some are won with teeth.

Her hand moved toward the sword.

The white charge followed.

I saw then that the sword had a secret. A place for it, or a way to hold it, or some glitter-fed lie that made metal keep what it was given. I could not tell. The box was cheating distance and order and proof.

She pressed the white thing to the hilt.

It stayed.

The sword did not click.

It pulsed.

The wrong edge changed.

Not brighter. Not darker. Worse.

Red from the little white load crawled into the cracked light along the blade until the whole thing looked like a wound in seeing itself.

The charge made the moments slip.

The edge made the cuts wrong.

That was all I knew.

That was enough.

Cold tore through the box.

Not winter cold. Not river cold. This went through meaning first and skin after.

The bull at my belt bucked hard against my waist. The gem eyes flickered.

Once.

Twice.

Then flat dark.

I felt the route fail.

Not a spell.

The route.

The old trusted line between hand and focus and result.

I tried to cast anyway.

It came backward.

Pain cracked up my palm and arm and shoulder, bright and mean. My chest locked. My knee hit the road before I knew I had gone down.

The blue-haired woman moved.

Sword in both hands. Body wrong. Steps wrong. Still coming.

The glitter held around her like a lie that kept proving itself true.

The blade caught my shoulder.

Not deep enough.

I got my arm up for the next one. Something shimmered between us, half a shield, half a habit. It held for less than a blink. The sword shoved through and cut forearm instead of throat.

Something hit the outside of the box.

Metal. Fast. Hard enough to make the black glass buck and the road jump under my knee. For one blessed second the whole seal thinned.

Then the halo-bloods outside caught it.

Of course they did.

Pretty rings burning, bodies half-broken, still holding the wall because the world is full of pests that refuse to die when they ought to.

The sword cut ribs next.

That one opened me.

Wet sound. Real sound. Mine.

My hand rose again by reflex, stupid body still reaching for spellwork the way a dog worries a bone after the meat is gone.

The spell fired backward into me.

Harder.

It crawled up my arm and into my chest. Muscles seized. Breath tore out. My right knee hit down and stayed down.

The bull’s eyes were dead now.

Dead dark.

Not sleeping. Not struggling.

Dead.

I looked at the belt like it had betrayed me.

It had.

Or I had.

Or the little white load had.

Or the sword.

Or that hateful glitter.

All of it had gotten together and made a liar out of the world.

The blue-haired woman kept coming.

The staff-clutcher kept making broken sounds at the glass.

The winged woman’s glass pins glowed while her mouth moved, little private casting aimed through the pane. Wing-blood meddling. Smoke and shine and soft hands holding up the weak while pretending that made them good.

The halo-bloods held the box.

The whole rotten sky-marked lot of them holding the road against me like they had a right.

I pushed up.

The charge was in the hilt.

If I took it out, the rest would settle.

Had to.

A weapon with a load could be unloaded. A spell with a focus could be cut. A little thing with help could have that help stripped away.

Simple.

Hand work.

Dirt work.

I reached.

The hilt was close.

Almost under my hand.

No.

Farther.

No, my fingers were on it.

No, the glitter had me by the ribs again, dragging without hands.

The white charge sat in the sword like an eye.

I was close enough.

I was not close enough.

The box blinked black.

My hand closed on air and on the hilt at the same time.

The blue-haired thing looked at me.

Not steady. Not clean. But there.

She answered with the sword.

There was no room for a proper swing.

That should have mattered.

Inside a blade’s reach, a sword gets awkward. Everybody knows that. Get close enough and steel becomes elbows, grip, weight, handle. Get close enough and a man can take the tool away from the hand.

I was close enough.

I was not close enough.

I was—

The edge moved in a short arc.

Not pretty.

Not grand.

Just weight and decision and cracked wrong-light opening where the world ought to have stayed shut.

It touched the side of my neck.

For a blink I knew five things and none of them agreed.

Cut.

Spell.

Light.

Charge.

Mine—

---

I stood up in gray air.

Or maybe I had already been standing.

The road lay under me and not under me. The black-glass box below. My body below. Head off apart from it. Blood doing its slow honest work on the asphalt.

Well.

That was inconvenient.

I looked down.

The blue-haired little thing still stood in the box for a breath with the sword in both hands. Her hands opened slow, one finger at a time, like she had to ask each one for permission.

The sword dropped.

The glitter caught it.

Of course it did.

Pretty little summon. Hateful little sparkle. Still working after the work was done.

It held the blade suspended at her side. Then it widened around the case, around her hand, around the white charge as she pulled it free and tried to put it away. Her movements were clumsy now. Slow. Like her own hand was across the room from her and she had to talk it home.

The charge went into the case.

The case shut.

The blue-haired thing’s knees gave.

The glitter caught her too.

I watched it ease her down like she mattered.

Below, outside the box, the staff-clutcher was still at the glass. The winged woman had her. The halo-bloods were still holding the work, though one of them had dropped low and the other looked hollowed out. Cops moved in and then moved back. Some stared at their own hands and radios like the wrongness had reached out and touched them too.

More headlights came around the bend.

Cars.

Doors.

People.

The light one got out carefully.

Not my copy.

The real one.

I knew her by the shape of what my work had tried to wear and failed to become. She moved like pain had lived in her long enough to know the floorplan. One side wrong. Face guarded. Blood on her. Wounds over old trouble.

Darkness came after her.

The real dark one.

I watched them take in the road.

Then the light one stopped.

Her head turned.

Not toward my body.

Toward me.

Straight at me.

Across gray air. Across the place where dead men ought to be private.

Her eyes found mine like there was no veil at all.

For one mean little second, neither of us moved.

I did not know what she was.

Did not know how she saw.

Only knew she did.

The look on her face did not change much. Quiet thing. Light thing. But she saw me, and that put a bitter taste in a mouth I no longer had.

A shape stood beside me in the gray.

Waiting.

Quiet as a fencepost at midnight.

I looked down at the body again.

Mine had looked better.

“Welp… reckon the good ol’ boys done got themselves killed.”

I looked down at my body, then over at the shape beside me.

“Tag, partner. You’re it.”

The shape did not look at me first.

It pointed down through the gray, through the black glass, through the glitter and the blood and the sealed case, straight at the blue-haired thing lying on the floor of the box.

Its voice was thin enough to cut with.

“It will kill it.”
