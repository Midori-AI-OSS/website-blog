Hicks took the exit. The convoy slowed through surface streets — the Malibu's hazards still pulsing behind us in the rearview. Then the hospital came into view. Washington Regional Medical Center. Hicks pulled into the drop-off lane and stopped.

"We're here."

Her staff preceded her. Her right arm was still tucked. The two swords at her hips shifted as she straightened — pulling her stance slightly off-center. She didn't try to fix it.

I braced my good hand against the door frame and pulled myself upright in stages — legs first, then torso, then the careful shift of weight that kept the broken wrist from taking any load. The pain spiked hard and settled.

Luna looked toward the visitor lot. "Weave. Bring the Malibu around."

The gold-green shimmer along the second cruiser's dashboard brightened in answer. A moment later, the Malibu's headlights pulsed once in the visitor lot and the sedan rolled forward — driver's seat empty. Weave threaded it back through the lot and pulled it to a stop behind the second cruiser.

The Malibu's doors closed. The sedan pulled away under Weave's control, slipped back into the same visitor spot, and went dark — headlights off, engine cut, still.

No visible weapons. Two cruisers in the drop-off lane and a parked sedan with a trunk full of things that belonged to the fight.

Marisol walked toward the second cruiser. She had been at the roadside when they loaded. She would be the first to meet them now.

The gold-green shimmer in the second cruiser's rear window brightened once — a pulse of acknowledgment. Then it dimmed back to watch-form, holding steady.

Intake started before I got my feet under me.

The ER doors sighed open. Two people in scrubs came out with a gurney — one broad-shouldered with a short ponytail, the other quick-stepping, young. A woman in navy followed with a tablet in one hand and a soft collar looped over the other wrist. Nobody stopped at the empty driver's seat in Weave's cruiser. Nobody looked twice at Marisol's wings. Bleeding first, questions after.

"Riley first," Marisol said. Low. Steady. "Penetrating wound. Sealed on scene, but she's getting worse. Echo has a concussion and cervical compression. Leo's hand is bad."

She didn't look at me when she said it. She didn't have to.

I stayed where I was. Wrist tucked. Watching.

The rear door of the second cruiser opened and gold-green light washed across the cabin in a soft pulse. The two in scrubs leaned in. Riley didn't stir. The wound in her side was closed but she looked worse than she had at the roadside — face pale under the entrance lights, breathing thin and uneven with too much space between pulls. Her bag was still with her, strap curled across her like she'd refused to let go even unconscious.

Echo blinked hard at the light. Then harder, like the brightness kept arriving in separate hits. Her hand tightened once on Riley's arm. Her other hand twitched toward her cardigan, missed, caught on the blanket instead.

Marisol touched Echo's wrist with two fingers. "She's going in now. You stay still. I'm here."

One dark wing shifted forward with her — smoke-soft, deliberate — cutting the worst of the ceiling glare off Echo's face.

"I heard you," Marisol said. "I'll tell them. Luna is here."

The two in scrubs worked fast. Riley's body shifted onto the board with the kind of gentleness reserved for people who might break in a place nobody could see yet. She didn't flinch. Her bag strap dragged and settled lower across her side. Her hand never moved to fix it.

The woman in navy checked her color. Pressed fingers briefly at her throat. Looked up.

The whole pace changed.

"Move," she said, and the gurney rolled.

"Needle trigger," Marisol called after them.

I shifted my weight off my bad side.

Another stretcher was already coming through the entrance.

Echo tried to follow Riley with her eyes and almost lost the effort halfway. The woman in navy steadied the back of her head, fit the soft collar into place, talked her through each step in the same calm tone she might have used for a late-night concussion patient who needed everything broken down one step at a time.

"Don't help," she said. "Let us do the lifting."

"Riley?" Echo managed.

"Inside," Marisol said. Her thumb pressed once against Echo's knuckles. Steady. Countable.

By the time they were rolling her inside, her face had gone tight and her breath wouldn't slow. Staff had to repeat questions before she could answer.

I was upright only in the technical sense.

Broken wrist tucked in close. Forearm held too carefully. Jaw locked against the pain. Every step built around not letting the injured arm swing. Luna stayed near my good side without crowding me — worn down to the edges, still favoring the shoulder, turning with her whole torso instead of risking one fast reach.

The nurse with the tablet pivoted toward us. "He ambulatory?"

"Barely," I said.

"Good enough." She pointed at the wheelchair that had come out with the first team. "Sit before you hit the floor and make me busier."

On any other night I might have argued. Tonight I didn't. I lowered myself into the chair one slow piece at a time. Breath going thin through my teeth as soon as the motion pulled at my wrist. One breath stalled high in my chest before I forced it out.

The nurse glanced up sharply, then kept moving.

Luna stayed beside me.

Inside, the ER moved with the clipped rhythm of practiced triage. Waiting area lights too bright. Intake desk with a printer running, a phone ringing, and a half-drunk coffee sweating beside a keyboard.

Real names went onto the forms. Marisol gave Riley's first. Echo's next. Mine after that. Luna, jaw tight, added Riley's needle trigger again in a flat voice that made it clear she expected every person in the room to remember it.

The woman behind the intake screen looked up — gray streaks in dark hair pulled back tight, reading glasses perched on her head. Eyes flicking once between Luna and me. "Twins?"

"Yeah," Luna said.

I held out Riley's glasses. Luna took them, set them on the counter beside the keyboard.

I got my species card out with my good hand. Two tries. Luna took it from me before I could fumble it, pulled her own from her pocket and set them both on the counter, and kept one hand at the back of my chair while she scanned them.

The effect was immediate. The nurse stopped reaching for the default medication sheet and swapped to a different set of questions — not just how much it hurt, but how much pain was getting through the reflex that normally pushed some of it down for people like me. A note flagged for imaging before I'd even been rolled deeper into the department.

If I was still feeling it this openly, they knew it was bad. Nobody had to stop and argue over what counted as normal.
