The shot came for Echo.

I heard Riley's voice crack on the word *no* — or maybe that was later, memory isn't clean about when things happened, only how they felt. The car drifting, metal and glass and the smell of cordite. I reached for magic before I could think, and my halo flared with it — heat at the crown of my skull, soft gold bleeding into the air. My eyes lit up too, warm and bright, the same light that spills from my hands when I cast.

You learn to pray fast when you're running. You learn to pray like it's another muscle, something you can fire without thinking. I don't do prayer right. I never have. But I did it anyway, in the space between one heartbeat and the next: *Let me reach her. Let me be fast enough. Let me be enough.*

The magic bullet had already done its work. Echo's head. Blood on the upholstery. The shape of fear I know too well — someone I'm supposed to protect bleeding out in front of me.

Riley carried her. I got the door.

The house was a wound in the world.

Not metaphorically. Something purple came through it — bound and snarling, made of something too close to death for me to name. I still started sorting it in my head because that's what I do when I'm scared.

Bound. Planar or dead or something in between. Purple means liminal — the space between spaces. Anchored in the house itself. I didn't know what it was. I only knew it was stronger than me.

Breaking it into neat little pieces felt safer than admitting how badly I wanted to scream.

My hands were shaking. I told myself it was adrenaline. It wasn't. It was the knowledge that I wasn't enough — that whatever this thing was, it was stronger than me, and Echo was bleeding, and Riley was shouting, and I had twenty-five words.

Sixteen words for Leo.

I cast `Sending` and felt the spell reach across the distance between us. For one heartbeat, I felt him — twin-shaped, warm, irritated-slash-worried in the way only he gets. The specific texture of his irritation. I knew it like I know my own pulse. We don't need to say things out loud. We feel them. A gravity between us that doesn't require language.

*Leo. South of SHBC. House by the apartments. Echo is hurt. Bound violet entity. Come armed.*

Sixteen words. I counted.

Then he was moving. I felt him go, the spell collapsing back into itself. I exhaled for the first time since the shot.

Leo came through a door that shouldn't exist.

Sword drawn. Spellbook visible. Staff on his back. He stepped into the room like he'd been waiting for the invitation his whole life, and the relief hit me like a second pulse — fast, painful, grateful in ways I'd never say out loud.

My breathing steadied. Not because the danger got smaller, just because he was here. We weren't alone against this anymore.

I didn't say *thank you.* I never do. He knows anyway. Twins don't need words the way other people do. There's a shorthand between us — half-formed sentences, glances that mean whole paragraphs, the way we can stand in the same room and know exactly where the other one is without looking.

He caught my eye for half a second. That was enough.

*You good?*

*I'm holding.*

*That's not the same thing.*

He's right. It never is.

Light magic didn't work on it.

I hate when that happens. I hate the feeling of reaching for something that should be there and getting absence instead. My light — the thing I am, the thing I was born with — slid off the entity like water off glass. Useless.

I tried to catalog why. *Conjuration subtype? Necrotic infusion? Something about the purple wavelength that resists photonic interaction?* The scholar in me wanted to take notes. The girl in me wanted to scream.

Echo fired a darkness round. It worked.

Leo killed it.

I watched my brother hold a line I couldn't hold alone. Watched him move through the fight with that particular economy of motion he has — nothing wasted, nothing dramatic, just the clean geometry of someone who learned early that the world doesn't forgive mistakes. We learned that together. The same lessons, the same scars, written into both of us in matching script.

The guilt settled in like old weather. He shouldn't have had to come. I should've been enough.

He'd tell me that's stupid if I said it out loud. He'd say *you called, I came, that's the deal.* And he'd mean it. And I'd still feel like a failure.

That's the twin bargain, isn't it? We carry each other, and we both feel like we're the one being carried.

Afterward: Riley and Echo both blacked out.

~2 hours. Leo and I handled transport. I drove Riley's Bolt. Leo took the back seat with both of them — Riley and Echo laid out, him running healing and wound care while I kept us on the road.

I didn't cast. Couldn't, not while driving. Just listened to Leo's ministrations from behind me — the rhythm of his hands, the soft sounds of spells closing wounds, the way his breathing changed when something went wrong or right. I watched the road and thought about nothing, which is a lie. I thought about everything. I thought about Echo's blood and Riley's voice and the weight of carrying two people I love through traffic I couldn't afford to notice.

Wound care. Healing. Watching them breathe.

At Riley's house, Riley was in Riley's bed and Echo was in Echo's cot in Riley's room. Leo and I took the living-room couch just outside, close enough to hear if either of them needed us.

From there, with the door open, we watched Riley's chest rise and fall. Didn't speak. Didn't need to. When you've shared a heartbeat with someone since before you were born, you learn to share silence too.

When Echo woke confused, I was there. When Riley stirred in bed, I was there. Leo and I traded looks between the couch and the doorway — *you watching her? I'm watching her. Okay. We're okay.*

I was tired.

Light doesn't come easy when you're this drained. When I cast, my halo only came up as a soft, thin ring and faded as soon as the spell left my hands.

Echo woke confused.

Asked for James. Fear-cast `Ice Knife` into the ceiling — a spike of frozen violence that bloomed against the plaster and then hung there, suspended in the aftermath of her panic.

I was already moving. `Prestidigitation`. Didn't flinch. Didn't correct her. Just let the ice melt and vanish like it had never been there.

The spell cost me nothing.

The look on her face cost me everything.

I hummed something soft. Didn't think about what. Just sound. Just presence. The way my mother used to do when I couldn't sleep. The melody came out half-formed, and I let it.

She didn't know where she was. Didn't know who was safe. And I couldn't blame her. I know what it's like to wake up fighting — to have your body remember combat before your mind remembers the room. The wrongness of it. The way fear lives in your muscles before it reaches your thoughts.

I stayed close. Let my presence say what my voice couldn't: *You're safe. I'm here. No one's going to hurt you.*

Leo caught my eye. We didn't speak. We both knew what we were seeing — the shape of trauma, the cost of surviving.

Cops arrived. Mercer and Hicks.

Statements about the house fight. I kept my answers short. Kept my hands visible. Gave them the smallest true things I could.

*Yes, we were south of SHBC. Yes, there was an incident. No, I didn't see who shot.*

All technically correct. All carefully incomplete.

I don't lie to cops. I just don't tell them everything. There's a difference. I think.

Leo stayed close. Not interfering. Just present. His own version of steady — quiet, watchful, close enough to catch me if I tipped sideways. I felt him anchor me without saying anything.

*You're fine. We're fine. We'll get through this.*

I didn't sleep.

I stretched out beside Leo on the living-room couch just outside Riley's room, close enough to hear if either of them needed help, and listened to him breathe. The familiar rhythm of it. The way his chest rose and fell in a pattern I could map with my eyes closed, because I've been mapping it since before I was born.

Twins don't need silence the way other people do. We're never really alone with each other. There's always someone else in the room, even when the room is empty. A presence that doesn't require proof.

I thought about how close today came to being the day I lost someone anyway.

I thought about the bargain I make every time I cast — *charge it to me, just not them* — and wondered if the universe was keeping score.

Probably. The universe keeps score on everything.

Leo shifted in his sleep. Turned toward me. Not awake, but aware, the way he always is when I'm spiraling. His hand found my arm. Stayed there.

*You're here. I'm here. We're still here.*

I didn't need him to say it. I felt it.

Eventually, he had to go. Home. Sleep. He'd already given us hours he didn't have to spare. I watched him leave and felt the twin-bond stretch — not break, never break, just thin out across the distance between us. A string pulled taut.

I stayed. Riley needed someone in the room. Echo needed someone in the room. And I couldn't have slept anyway.

Day 2.

Officers called. Return to SHBC. The same church. Twice.

That's not coincidence. That's a message. Someone is telling us *I know where you were. I know where you'll be. I can reach you whenever I want.*

*Targeted. Escalating. This isn't random.*

We went anyway.

Fifteen police cars already on scene. Riley put her hand on the back door and cracked it open.

Clay-bodied copies.

Me-copy. Leo-copy. Echo-copy. Riley-copy.

Four stolen faces moving through the church at once.

I looked at the thing wearing my face and felt my stomach turn.

It moved like me. Cast like me. Knew where I would stand, where I would reach, where I would flinch. Fighting your own ghost isn't combat — it's argument with a mirror that wants you dead.

The clone raised its staff the same way I do. Held it at the same angle. When it cast, its halo flared — gold light, my light, stolen and repurposed.

*I know you*, it seemed to say. *I am you. And I want you gone.*

The wrongness of it hit me like nausea. That's my face. My body. My magic. The thing I've spent my whole life learning to use, turned against me by something that doesn't understand what it's wearing.

Leo's clone moved through the room too, and I felt my chest tighten — watching my brother fight a version of himself that knows all his moves. Echo's copy stalked its own lane. Riley's came in with her stance and none of her restraint. The whole room turned into mirrors and target priority.

Riley tackled the thing wearing my face. Hard. For one impossible half-second it hesitated when she came down on it — some scrap of stolen pattern failing to strike her cleanly. The clone let her kill it. Pattern leakage carrying something of me that couldn't raise a blade against her.

Riley had a loaned sword from one of the officers. She didn't hesitate. The blade came down. The Luna-copy fell.

She pivoted—same blade—to the thing wearing her own face. The Riley-copy had copied her gun too, had already shot an officer. It didn't hesitate like mine did. It fought back. But Riley was faster. Angrier. Two swings. The Riley-copy went down.

Two clones. Same sword.

Echo fought her own face across the room. I watched from the edge—still processing, still tasting clay and ozone—as she dropped her staff, pulled her dagger, and cut the Echo-copy's head off when it pivoted toward Riley. Solo. Clean. No assist needed.

That left Leo's copy.

Leo and I closed on it together. Twin shorthand. We didn't need words—just position. He took left angle; I took right. Staff and sword, the twin-movement we'd been building since we were old enough to hold weapons. The clone knew our forms, but not our rhythm. It couldn't predict the way we adjust for each other mid-swing, the way he'll dip when I rise, the way I cover his blind spot without being asked. That's not technique. That's twenty-six years of moving in parallel.

The clone knew the shape of us but not the years. I drove light through its center and the recoil hit both of us at once — too much power through too familiar a pattern. The twin-bond snapped tight around the backlash. We both crumpled before I'd fully registered the Leo-copy hitting the floor.

For a moment, I couldn't move. Couldn't breathe. My halo was gone—just gone—and my eyes felt dim, like someone had turned down the brightness inside my skull. The gold warmth that usually lives at the edges of my vision wasn't there. I was gray inside. Hollowed out.

Every breath cost something. The air felt thick, resistant, like my lungs had forgotten how to pull it in properly. My skin was cold—wrong cold, not the cold of winter but the cold of empty. Like something had been scooped out of my center and the edges were still standing, confused about why they weren't falling.

*You okay?*

*Still breathing.*

W.E.A.V.E. arrived as a lift of bismuth dust at the edge of my vision, glittering in the church light. The particles folded toward person-shape beside us. I barely had enough focus left to land her name, only the fact of help.

Then the next two walked out.

Pastor-copy. Healed Riley-copy.

The pastor-copy had a spellbook open in his hands. The healed Riley-copy was whole in every place the real Riley had already paid for with blood. That was its own kind of obscenity.

Real Riley got back up with W.E.A.V.E.'s help. Good. Necessary. Terrifying.

*That isn't Riley. That's theft with her face. Move.*

I tracked both of them.

The pastor-copy had the spellbook open, death magic pooling purple-black across the pages. If it finished, it would catch all of us. The healed Riley-clone held a saber loose at its side and watched me calculate.

Real Riley got back up.

W.E.A.V.E. held her like a second skeleton — bismuth and precision, bearing the weight she couldn't hold on her own. I watched Riley take the loaned sword and think, and go. No flourish. Just need.

She went straight for the pastor-clone and took its left arm, then the right. Nothing pretty about it. Two cuts. The spellbook hit the ground with both hands still clamped around it, the death spell folding in on itself with a sound like a lung giving out.

Riley still bent, grabbed the book, and staggered toward the cop cars with it because of course she did.

Then her knees went out.

*No. No. No.*

She hit the ground. She didn't get back up.

I registered it — Riley down, book still in her arms — and kept moving, because the healed Riley-clone still had a saber and the pastor-copy was armless and not dead.

With Riley down, the clone turned its full attention to me.

The clone smiled. Held the saber loose.

*Off.*

The blade vanished. It touched my shoulder. Friendly. Almost gentle. The way Riley might tap me to get my attention.

I realized too late that this was the point.

*On.*

The saber lit up inside me.

Pain is information.

My shoulder. The blade burning through muscle, through meat, through everything that keeps a body together. The smell of my own seared flesh — copper and char.

I didn't scream.

I didn't have time.

The clone pulled the saber free and I felt the damage catalog itself — *rotator cuff. Deltoid. The meat of my shoulder separating. Blood spreading warm and wrong.* I cataloged the damage because it was easier than feeling it.

The clinical part kept listing.

The rest of me burned.

Hot. Wet. Wrong.

The room narrowed to targets and vectors. My shirt was going red at the shoulder.

The pastor-clone stood armless at the edge of my vision. Clay knitting back into stolen limbs — wet and wrong, arms regrowing from nothing.

It struck before I could shout. Echo's face. The impact. My friend staggering back, hand to her cheek, blood between her fingers.

*They hurt her.*

*They hurt my twin.*

Something cracked open in my chest. Not the shoulder wound — deeper. The same part of me that reaches for Leo when I can't breathe, the part that knows exactly where he is in any room. The part that counts his heartbeats like they're my own.

These things wear our faces. They steal our movements. They hurt the people I'm supposed to protect and they don't even have the decency to stay down.

The rage came like a second heartbeat.

*You hurt me. You're still standing.*

*No.*

I cast `Slow` on the healed Riley-clone.

The spell reached out, dragging at its limbs, warping its movements. Buying time. Finally making it share some fraction of the drag the real Riley had been carrying all night.

But Slow wasn't enough. Not with Riley down. Not with the pastor-copy still moving on the edge of my vision. Not with Leo and Echo still in the room.

*You hurt me.*

*You are both still here.*

*End it.*

Then `Photon Blade Swarm`.

Sixty-four light-blue photon blades.

The spell ripped out of my reserves like it was taking more than mana. Taking heat. Taking color. Taking the strength in my knees and the clarity in my vision and something I couldn't name — something deeper, something that felt like a prayer I didn't get to finish.

I felt each blade leave me. Sixty-four small wounds. Sixty-four pieces of myself given away.

*Too much. I paid too much. The spell took more than mana — it took heat, took color, took the strength in my knees.*

No.

The clones needed to end. Both of them. Pastor-copy, arms regrown and still moving toward us. Healed Riley-copy, saber up and whole where the real one wasn't. Gone. Ash. Ended.

I paid for that ending in full.

My skin went cold. My hands went numb. The light in my blood felt like it was draining out through invisible cuts, leaving me empty and gray and shaking.

This is what overcasting feels like for me: not plain exhaustion, not just being empty. More like something scooped out of the center of me and left the edges standing.

And I knew, even shaking and half-gone, that I would do it again.

That's the part that scares me.

Blood loss.

Overcast.

I passed out.

I think Leo caught me. The memory is wrong — blood loss does that, scrambles sequence into feeling. I remember his arms, the familiar grip, the way he's always there when I fall. But the truth is probably dirt. Concrete. My shoulder hitting the ground before he reached me. The world went sideways and then his hands were there, lifting, steadying.

The twin-bond says *he caught me*. The physics says *I hit first*. Both are true. The sensation of being held — that's real, even if the timeline isn't.

*You fall, I catch. I fall, you catch. That's just who we are.*

For a second — just a second — I felt the shape of us. Two halves of something that shouldn't be separated. The way my gravity finds his, automatic, animal. The way we've always moved together.

He was saying something. I couldn't hear the words, but I knew the tone. The same tone he's used since we were children.

*Stay with me. Don't go. I've got you.*

I drifted. Somewhere between awake and not. The sound of cops finding the crystal. A hammer striking it. Blunt. Final.

I didn't see it. I was already gone.

The church kept catching me even with my eyes closed.

Every time I almost made it back, I was in the sanctuary again — pews split open, air gone wrong, my shoulder burning like the saber had just left it.

I would try to move and the whole world turned thick around me.

My body stayed in that last second before collapse. Every step dragged. Every turn came late.

I kept reaching for light and getting a stutter instead — a spark that died in my hand before it could become anything useful.

Even half-conscious, I knew where everyone was by feel more than sight.

Leo to my left.

Riley refusing to stay down.

Echo too close to danger for my nerves to bear.

I kept trying to get to them. The room kept stretching away from me. I would lunge and stay exactly where I was.

I hate dreams like that. All need, no arrival.

Sometimes I thought I'd broken through — a shoulder under my hand, fabric, heat, the start of a name in my mouth — and then it all slid sideways again. Sanctuary. Parking lot. Sanctuary.

White kept cutting through it in flashes. Noise too. Wheels maybe. Metal maybe. Somebody calling from very far away.

Gold-green light somewhere nearby. W.E.A.V.E. I think. Couldn't focus on it. Just registered the shape — particles folded close, riding with us.

None of it stayed. The church would close over me again, and I would be right back in that impossible second, trying to wake up fighting.

When I finally surfaced for real, I was gray and shaking. My skin had gone pale in a way that didn't look like me, and the light in me barely answered.

When I tried to cast, all I got were flickers — half-shaped things that collapsed before they could mean anything.

Over the next few hours, that didn't change much. If I forced even a small spell through, my halo only managed one weak gold flicker before it died again. My color came back slower: pink instead of gray, then something closer to warm.

*Not full. Not yet. Maybe soon. Maybe not.*

"Where is it... where am..."

Then I had to shut my eyes again.

"Die fuckers."

"Do not touch her."

I reached for Leo in broken words.

"I... I... tried... Le... o..."

My voice cracked on his name. The one word that matters. The one word that cuts through everything else.

My body remembered combat before my mind remembered where I was. Hospital lights. Riley on the next bed. No threat in the room. Magic kept catching at the edge of the panic anyway — instinct, not a real cast. The casting reflex reaching for a threat that wasn't there.

The muscle memory of violence. The way survival lives in your blood even when the danger is gone.

Leo kept grounding me.

*Hospital. You're safe. No casting.*

His voice was the only thing that could cut through the noise. Steady. Familiar. The sound I've been listening for my whole life.

He didn't touch me. Didn't need to. The twin bond did the work — a gravity between us that doesn't require contact. I felt him anchor me from across the room, the way he always has, the way he always does.

*You're okay. We're okay. Breathe.*

I hate that I needed it.

I'm grateful he gave it anyway.

Echo stayed with Riley.

Needle trigger warning to staff. I watched her do it — stay present, stay close, hold the line. She's better at that than she knows. Better than I ever was.

Hicks apologized to her about letting Riley go. I heard it from somewhere far away — dim, like voices through water.

Couldn't respond.

Should've said something.

Didn't.

Riley's heart rate rising slowly.

Coming closer to the surface.

I watched the monitor more than I watched her face. Numbers are easier than expressions. Numbers don't ask anything back.

*Seventy-two. Rising. Good.*

I cataloged the numbers like a spellbook entry. Anything to keep from drowning in the waiting.

I stayed where I was.

Close to Riley. Close enough to matter, quiet enough not to take up space.

Leo's presence anchored the whole thing. He moved through the room like he always does — managing, steadying, holding the shape of us together without anyone noticing. He'd been keeping watch for hours by then. I watched him from the bed and felt the twin-bond hum between us.

*We're okay. They're okay. We're not alone.*

At some point — I didn't see when exactly, my eyes were still coming and going — W.E.A.V.E. asked for an empty box. A cardboard one. Said she was going to reduce visibility and to tap it when it was time to bring her back for the briefing.

I remember the gold-green light condensing, slow and deliberate, into a small cardboard square on the side table. And then the room got quieter in a way that had nothing to do with sound.

Once I could actually hold a conversation, Officers Hicks and an Arkansas State trooper came by with a briefing.

Echo tapped the box first.

I watched her do it — a gentle knock on the cardboard, nothing dramatic — and the bismuth particles lifted and gathered, W.E.A.V.E. rebuilding herself into readable form. The gold-green light returned. They told her what was going on. Overnight. Coordinated. Across the U.S. England too.

The door opened while the officers were still talking.

Grandma Gustin.

She came in the way family does when they've been driving hard and praying the whole way — fast and quiet, eyes going straight to the beds, taking inventory before she'd even closed the door behind her. Her presence changed the air. Made it warmer and harder to hold at the same time.

I didn't say anything. I didn't need to. She looked at me and I looked at her and we both understood exactly what had happened to this room and what it had cost.

The officers finished. Someone is building something across time zones, seeding crystals into cities like a pattern. We'd been thinking in single fights, single threats. We'd been inside one node of it, bleeding on the church floor, while the rest was already moving.

Not just us. Not just here. The weight of it settled somewhere I couldn't name.

Leo and I traded the kind of look that means we'll talk about it later.

*Someone is building something. We are going to have to figure out what.*

Riley began waking around 9:45.

I was watching her monitor when it started — the slow shift in heart rate, the way her breathing changed. Her eyes opened and she wasn't in the room yet. Still somewhere else. Black and slow and nothing, with the texture of jelly. Her hand moved as if reaching for equipment that wasn't there.

W.E.A.V.E. moved first.

Layer 3 contact — I didn't know exactly what that meant, only what I saw: the bismuth field shifting closer to Riley, gold-green at the edge of her peripheral vision, short grounding statements at a frequency I could barely hear. *The church is not current. You do not have to stand into it.*

Echo was already at her side. I watched her catch Riley's hand before it could reach the IV line — the instinct, the reach, Echo's fingers closing over hers before the grab could complete. Not restraining. Just there. Present. Saying *I've got you* without making it a bigger deal than it was.

Riley waited.

Breathed.

Came back.

Recovery isn't a moment.

It's a stretch of time where you wait for your body to remember how to be itself again. Where you let the people who love you hold the room while you figure out where the walls are.

We did that. All of us. Together.
