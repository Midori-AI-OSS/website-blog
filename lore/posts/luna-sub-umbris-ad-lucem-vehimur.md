They came from the dark past the cop cars.

Lady Light first. Then Lady Darkness — a step behind, half in her sister's shadow, the way she always was. I saw the damage before I saw anything else — not the kind where you take inventory, the kind where you recognize the shape of it because you're carrying your own. Her left side was the working side, always had been, but now gray with the same exhaustion that was eating at the edges of me. Her right side — the still side — was worse than at the house. Fresh blood across it. A bruise dark along her cheek where something had connected and she hadn't been able to turn away from it. She was carrying what we were carrying. The arm that never worked hung lower. The limp was heavier. Every step paid for. Lady Darkness was not better. Just quieter about it.

They stopped a few paces from Leo and me. Lady Light looked past us — toward Echo, toward Riley in her lap.

"She did it," Lady Light said. Her voice came from the working side of her face. The rest of her mouth stayed still. "The plan — the crystal, the box, him. It worked. But it was supposed to be cleaner than this."

"It never is," Lady Darkness said.

Lady Light's working eye found me. Found Leo. "He was the source. The crystals in the cities — the copies — all of it. He was the hand behind it. You stopped the spread. Tonight. Right here."

I kept my right arm tucked. My voice came out low. The exhaustion was in it. "All of them. Every city."

Leo's halo flashed. Thin gold pulse. His voice was flat. "Then it's done. Tonight."

Lady Darkness was looking at Echo now. At Riley. Her voice was soft but direct. "What happened to her?"

I glanced toward the iridescent gold-green shimmer around the cop car. "Weave was inside with her. Weave — can you join us?"

Her voice came. __Acknowledged. Shifting focus.__

A thin ribbon of particles detached from the field around Echo and Riley. Drifted toward us. I saw the cops glance toward the shimmer. Marisol's attention shifted. The air around us sharpened.

Then her voice came, in the back of my mind — not to me alone. To all of us. The words steady and precise — the full account of what happened inside the box.

__Riley deployed the Lacuna Crystalli inside the sealed box. The caster's sequence trust fractured — his spells failed, backfired. She fought him hand-to-hand. The Fracture blade made contact — shoulder, ribs, then the neck. Decapitation. The crystal was returned to the safety case and sealed. She collapsed immediately after. The through-and-through wound from the earlier clone encounter was sealed by Luna. Riley is stable but unconscious. Exhaustion, not injury.__

Lady Darkness stilled. Lady Light's working eye sharpened. Echo — barely conscious against the cop car — lifted her head a fraction at the sound of Riley's name. The cops within range traded glances.

Lady Darkness looked from Echo to Leo. Her voice stayed soft. "And Echo? What happened to her? And him —" She tilted her head toward Leo's cradled wrist.

Weave answered. __Echo: cervical compression injury from the false Lady Darkness clone — limited vocal output. Blunt cranial impact against the driver-side window. Residue exposure from the destroyed clone — visible age progression. Approximately five years. Cause: Lady Darkness clone residue — exposure from the clone's destruction at the containment site. Leo: right wrist — multiple fracture. Sustained during the initial clone engagement.__

Lady Darkness looked at Riley again. Her delivery was soft. "She's still breathing. That's more than the plan owed us."

Lady Light glanced at the body on the road. Then back. "We had him cornered. Before he got away. This time —" She looked at Riley. "— she made sure."

"She made sure," I said. Quiet. Looking toward Riley.

Leo's voice was hoarse. "Was there anyone else? Working with him."

Lady Light shook her head. Small motion. The working side of her face was grim. "No. He was alone tonight. But there were nearly forty clones at the crystal before we arrived. We killed them all before he got away."

Then she looked at us. At me. At Leo. At the shoulder and the wrist and the hollow places behind our eyes.

"That seal you held — we felt it from outside. That was not a small thing."

"It held," I said. "That's what mattered."

Leo's halo flickered once more. Thin pulse. Gone. "He's done."

A man stepped from the shadows from the cliff side, as if out of the air. One figure. He held both light and dark in the same bearing — the way someone holds two things that do not belong together but have learned to share the same hands. He moved to Lady Light first. Lifted her. Careful. Gentle in a way that spoke of long practice.

Magic flared. A doorway opened — not light, not dark, something else. A threshold that did not belong on an Arkansas highway at two in the morning. He carried Lady Light through. Lady Darkness followed. The doorway closed.

They were gone.

Marisol left our side. She crossed to Echo and knelt beside her — steady, calm, her hands finding Echo's vitals the way a medic reads a chart.

"Echo," she said. "Let me see you. You're fading."

Echo's voice was barely a sound. Her throat was wrecked. Her hands were still on Riley. "Riley—"

"She's stable. Weave has her. Breathe."

Hicks approached. Direct. Professional. His eyes moved from the body on the road to me to Leo and back.

"The body," he said. "Some of us saw something on it — magic, residue. What do I tell them?"

My shoulder hurt. My voice came out tired but clear. "Two things. One: the crystal. Lacuna Crystalli. She used it on him — it breaks your trust in sequence. What just happened, what you just did. His spells couldn't route. They started failing, backfiring. That's why he couldn't fight back at the end."

I paused. Glanced toward Riley. "Two: the sword. The light on the blade left something behind. Wrong-light. Under her skin, and in the wound. His neck. Wrong color. Like it can't close."

Leo's voice was hoarse but steady. "The crystal's sealed. The case is in her bag. There's nothing active on him. But the neck — the wound's still got that wrong-light in it. Tell them not to touch the edges."

"Anything else they need to stay clear of?" Hicks asked.

"Just the body," I said. "The head's separate. Keep them back from both."

A cop approached. Cautious. His flashlight swept across Echo and Riley — and stopped on Riley's arms. The wrong-light. Stopped at her shoulders. Quiescent. Not advancing. But there.

"What's on her arms?" he asked. "That light — is that—"

I glanced toward Riley. "Residual. From the fight. She's stable."

Hicks turned. His voice cut through the chatter. To the cop whose cruiser they were taking: "I'm taking your vehicle. Washington Regional."

Then to us. Decisive. "Washington Regional. Two hours. I'll take point." He looked toward the shimmer. "Weave — you've got the cruiser."

Weave's voice. __Acknowledged. Cruiser interface ready. Route confirmed.__

"And the Malibu — bring Leo's car behind us."

__Malibu interface queued. Will follow the cruiser.__

Marisol straightened from beside Echo. To Hicks. To all of us. "I'll meet you there."

Hicks looked at me. At Leo. "You two, with me."

"Yeah," I said. Depleted.

Leo's voice was thin. "Let's go."

Marisol turned to the shimmer. Practical. Steady. "Weave. Help me get them in the cruiser. Echo first — she's about to drop."

__Field adjusting. Lifting Echo.__

The bismuth field shifted. It lifted Riley first — she was in Echo's lap, and lifting Echo first would have dropped her to the ground. Then Echo. Both together. The field kept the contact between them. Echo never lost her grip.

Echo's voice came out as a sound more than a word. "...Riley."

Marisol spoke before she departed. "I'll see you there. She's in good hands."

Hicks' cruiser was thirty feet down the road. Dark paint. Headlights cutting cones across the asphalt. The engine was running.

I walked. Leo walked beside me. Neither of us fast. Neither of us talking. His halo flashed once — gold pulse as his broken wrist shifted with each step — and then again a few strides later. I did not look at it. He did not need me to look at it.

The staff took my weight. The spellbook was clipped to my right side — I'd grabbed it when Marisol helped me up and I hadn't let go. My right shoulder carried the rest — a dull, grinding throb that had stopped being pain and started being background noise. The swords at my hips — mine on the left, Riley's on the right — pulled at my belt with every step. The weight was wrong. Not heavy. Just wrong. Her weapon where her hand should have been.

Hicks opened the rear door. I climbed in. The motion pulled at my shoulder and I let it. Leo followed. Slow. Careful with the wrist. The door closed.

The cruiser was warm. The seats were hard plastic and the radio was muttering cop chatter I did not listen to. Leo settled beside me. His shoulder against mine. His breath shallow but steady. I did not look at him. I looked out the side mirror.

The scene receded. The road behind us: the caster's body, the flashing lights, the cops keeping their distance from the neck wound and its wrong-light. The commandeered cruiser pulled out behind us — Weave driving, Echo and Riley in the back. The Malibu's headlights came on behind that. Marisol was out of sight. The road ahead was dark and quiet and very long.

Leo's halo pulsed once more. Thin gold. There and gone.

I watched the mirror until the body was a small dark shape and the lights were a smudge of red and blue and then nothing. The road was just road. The night was just night.

Hicks turned onto the highway and the cruiser settled into a long, dark rhythm. The radio muttered. The engine hummed. The road unspooled under the tires in a straight line north.

I kept my staff between my knees. I unclipped the spellbook and rested it in my lap, cover cool against my thigh. The crystal at the staff tip kept catching the dashboard glow every time we hit a bump — faint prism flickers on the headliner, there and gone. My right arm stayed tucked against my side. The shoulder had stopped being pain and started being a presence — a dull, grinding weight that wasn't going anywhere. Riley's sword pressed into my right hip through my pants. Every time the cruiser swayed, the pommel dug in a little deeper. I didn't shift it. I wanted the reminder.

Leo was watching me. I could feel it without looking — the thin stubborn thread of his presence behind my ribs, stronger now that he was right there beside me instead of on the ground where I'd left him. He'd been watching since we pulled out. The angle of my shoulder. The arm I wasn't using. The way I wasn't leaning back.

"Shoulder." His voice was quiet. Rough at the edges. "Not the short version."

I kept my eyes on the road ahead. The highway was dark, the cruiser's headlights cutting a narrow tunnel through the night. "Weave braced it during the fight. Held through. It'll hold through the drive."

"That's still pretty short."

A breath that was almost a laugh. Not quite. "It's the version I've got, Leo. I can cast left-handed. I've been doing it. The shoulder's not going to get worse sitting still."

He looked at me for a moment longer. A jolt in the road — his halo flickered, one sharp gold pulse, then dark. He faced forward again.

"I'll check in an hour."

"I know."

The silence that followed was not the silence of a closed subject. It was the silence of my brother not being done with it and knowing better than to push.

I broke the silence first. Because if I didn't say it, the words were going to sit in my chest and calcify.

"Riley took a blade for Echo."

"I saw."

"She collapsed when the sigil dropped. I had her heartbeat in my hand and it was —"

The words stopped. Just stopped. My throat closed around them and I let it. The sentence that would have followed stayed behind my teeth where it belonged.

"She's alive. Echo's alive. Everyone's alive."

"Barely." Leo's voice was flat. Not cold — I know the difference. "Echo can barely talk. My wrist is done. Riley's still down. Your shoulder's wrecked. The caster's dead. Doesn't mean we're fixed."

"Everyone's alive," I said again. Quieter. Not arguing. Holding onto it with both hands — metaphorically, anyway, since one of them wasn't doing much.

He said nothing for a beat. Then: "Yeah." The word landed like something he was choosing to believe.

The road hummed under the tires. The silence stretched, then I broke it again. Somebody had to.

"We need a story. For when we get there. What happened to us — the kind of shape we're in — someone's going to ask."

"We were attacked." Leo didn't hesitate. "That's all anyone needs to know right now."

"Echo."

"Friend. Same car."

My good hand tightened on the staff. "The weapons."

"Rear bench of the Malibu. Weave can bring it around when we get there."

Hicks spoke without taking his eyes off the road. His voice was quiet, calm — the voice of a man who had seen worse and was still driving. "We'll figure it out when we get there."

"Thank you," I said. And meant it.

I turned toward Leo. The motion was small — the shoulder refused more than a few degrees — but it was enough to face him. His right wrist was cradled in his lap. His good hand still held Riley's glasses, the bent temple resting against his palm. He hadn't let go of them since the roadside.

"You. Talk to me."

"I'm fine."

"You pulled yourself out of a crater with a broken wrist." I kept my voice level. "You're not fine."

His jaw tightened. His halo flickered — a single sharp pulse, pain-driven, then dark. He stared at the back of the passenger seat like it had personally offended him.

"Wrist is gone." The words came out flat. "Every bump in the road is a problem." He stopped. Started again, lower. "I'm holding. That's what I've got."

I reached with my right hand. The shoulder screamed — a dull spike up the joint that turned my vision white at the edges for half a second — but the reach was short and I kept it low. My fingers found his arm. I didn't squeeze. I just rested them there, above where the worst of it was, on the part of him that wasn't broken.

He didn't pull away. His left hand — the good one — stayed cradled in his lap. But his shoulder dropped half an inch. The line of his spine softened by a fraction. Like something in him had been holding its breath and finally let go.

I left my hand there. Told myself it was for the hourly check.

A mile passed. The silence had shifted — still heavy, but no longer brittle. Less like a wound being protected, more like a breath after a long exhale.

Hicks was definitely not getting paid enough for this.

My hand stayed on Leo's arm. Leo hadn't moved it. Leo hadn't moved away.

I turned, looking past Leo through the rear window. The second cruiser was right behind us — Weave's cruiser, gold-green shimmer bright along the dash, the empty driver's seat lit from within by the bismuth glow. I could make out their shapes through the glass — two silhouettes in the back seat, one lying flat, the other upright beside her. The distance blurred the details, but the picture was enough: two people I loved, both still there, the shimmer still wrapped around them.

Two cars back, the Malibu followed. Headlights steady. Hazards pulsing in a slow amber rhythm. Nobody at the wheel.

"She drives better than you," I said. Not quite smiling.

Leo followed my gaze through the rear window. A beat. Then, flat: "We're in the back of a cop car."

"Yeah."

"Just checking."

He didn't pull his arm away. And I didn't move my hand.

Hicks checked the rearview once. The road was dark. The Malibu's hazards kept their steady amber beat behind us. He said nothing. The cruiser pressed on.

The highway opened into four lanes. The treeline on either side softened — the dense dark breaking into scattered house lights set back from the shoulder. Porch lights. A few lit windows. People who had no idea what the road behind us looked like.

Leo's halo had gone quiet. Brief gold pulses only when the road jostled his wrist — a flicker, then dark. Not the sharp pain-spikes from earlier. Just the road being the road.

I leaned toward the window, temple resting against the glass. The glass was cold. The road ahead was long and dark and slowly filling with the faint orange glow of something that wasn't just night anymore. I didn't speak. Neither did Leo. The silence between us was the kind you don't need to fill.

My shoulder had been making its presence known for the last ten miles. I let it.

The convoy merged onto I-49 south. The orange glow on the horizon sharpened into something with a name: Fayetteville.

Hicks took the exit. He had named the hospital back at the roadside — Washington Regional — and I hadn't let myself think about what that meant until now. The cruiser slowed through surface streets. Stoplights. Strip malls. The kind of normal that felt like it belonged to someone else's night. The Malibu's hazards pulsed behind us in the rearview — a steady amber heartbeat that Weave was keeping for us.
